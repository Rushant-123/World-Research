# How to Make Limestone

**Top Market Leaders:**
- [Vulcan Materials](/companies/vulcan-materials)
- [Martin Marietta](/companies/martin-marietta)
- [HeidelbergCement](/companies/heidelbergcement)



Sedimentary rock - calcium carbonate (CaCO₃), formed from marine organism shells/skeletons over millions of years. Uses: cement (70% of limestone, clinker feedstock), aggregate (construction roads/buildings, 15%), flux (steel/glass manufacturing, 8%), agriculture (soil pH correction, 3%), chemicals (lime, soda ash production, 2%), other 2%. Global production: 4.5 billion tonnes/year (crushed limestone + quicklime production, 2023). Quarrying: open pit mining, drill-blast-crush-screen.

**Step 1-18: Geology and Deposit Formation**
Limestone: sedimentary rock, 80-99% CaCO₃ (calcite polymorph), remainder clay, quartz, iron oxides, organic matter. Formation: (1) Biological (90% of limestone): marine organisms (foraminifera, coccolithophores, coral, mollusks, brachiopods) secrete calcium carbonate shells, accumulate on seafloor (pelagic ooze, reef deposits), lithify over 1-200 million years (compaction, cementation, diagenesis). (2) Chemical precipitation: supersaturated seawater precipitates CaCO₃ (warm shallow seas, evaporation increases Ca²⁺ and CO₃²⁻ concentrations, ooids form - concentric layers around nucleus grain). (3) Evaporitic: saline lakes precipitate calcite. **Limestone types:** Chalk (fine-grained, porous, white, Upper Cretaceous England/France, 95-99% CaCO₃, soft). Oolitic limestone (spherical ooids 0.25-2mm, Jurassic Indiana Bedford limestone, dimension stone). Fossiliferous (visible shells, Ordovician-Devonian corals/crinoids). Travertine (hot spring precipitate, porous, decorative stone). Dolomite/dolostone (CaMg(CO₃)₂, 10-50% MgCO₃, diagenetic replacement of calcite by Mg²⁺-rich fluids, harder than limestone). **Global deposits:** Widespread sedimentary basins (karst topography, cave systems from dissolution). Major producing regions: China (1.5 Gt/year), India (350 Mt), USA (900 Mt crushed stone, Texas, Florida, Missouri, Kentucky), EU (800 Mt, Germany, France, Poland, UK), Brazil (200 Mt), others 700 Mt.

**Step 19-40: [Quarry Site Selection](open-pit-mining) and Development**
Exploration: geological mapping, drill cores confirm CaCO₃ content (>85% for cement grade, >90% for chemical/flux), thickness (prefer 20-100m beds, minimize overburden), impurities (SiO₂, Al₂O₃, Fe₂O₃, MgO limits depend on application). Chemical analysis: X-ray fluorescence (XRF) measures oxide composition (CaO 50-56% indicates high-purity limestone, equivalent to 89-100% CaCO₃). Petrographic thin sections: identify minerals, texture, porosity. **Overburden removal:** Topsoil (0.1-2m, stockpile for reclamation). Overburden (soil, weathered rock, non-limestone layers, 1-20m depending on deposit). Stripping ratio: 0.1-2:1 (waste:ore, low for surface-exposed limestone beds, high for deeply buried). Equipment: bulldozers, scrapers, draglines (remove overburden to expose limestone bedrock). **Quarry planning:** Bench mining (step down in 10-20m vertical benches, 5-15m wide safety berms between benches, final depth 50-300m below surface). Haul roads (12-18m wide, <10% gradient for 100-400 tonne trucks). Pit dewatering: pumps remove groundwater, rainfall (limestone aquifers, karstic porosity, dewatering 100-10,000 L/min depending on hydrogeology). Environmental permits: 2-10 years (impact assessments, blasting, dust, noise, water discharge, habitat mitigation, reclamation bonds $50,000-10 million).

**Step 41-65: Drilling and Blasting**
**Bench preparation:** Rotary drills (310-380mm diameter, tricone roller bits or DTH down-the-hole hammer drills) drill vertical holes 10-25m deep, spacing 3-6m in square or staggered pattern. Drilling rate: 10-30 m/hour (hard crystalline limestone slower, soft chalk faster). **Explosive loading:** Ammonium nitrate fuel oil (ANFO, 94% NH₄NO₃ porous prills + 6% diesel, bulk density 0.8-0.9 g/cm³, detonation velocity 2,500-3,500 m/s, safe to handle, economical $0.50-1.50/kg). Load into blast holes: pneumatic truck fills holes from bottom (leave 1-3m stemming collar unfilled). Primer: high-explosive booster (PETN, dynamite, or emulsion, 0.5-2 kg per hole) at bottom with detonator. Stemming: crushed stone or drill cuttings fill top 1-3m (confines explosion gases, directs energy into rock fracturing vs venting to atmosphere). **Blasting:** Electronic delay detonators (millisecond-delay timing, 8-500ms intervals between holes, sequential detonation row-by-row, prevents over-fragmentation or flyrock). Powder factor: 0.2-0.6 kg explosive per tonne rock (soft limestone 0.2, hard crystalline 0.6). Blast size: 50,000-500,000 tonnes per shot (large quarries blast weekly, small monthly). Fragmentation: target 0.5-1.5m boulders (optimal for primary crusher feed, minimize fines <50mm that bypass crusher, minimize oversize >2m requiring secondary blasting). Vibration monitoring: seismographs measure peak particle velocity PPV (limit <25 mm/s at nearest structures, 50-100 mm/s damage threshold for residential, blasting permits enforce setback distances 150-500m). Flyrock: exclude zone 300-500m radius (rare incidents, fatal if projectile exits quarry).

**Step 66-88: Loading and Hauling**
**Primary loading:** Hydraulic excavators (200-800 tonne operating weight, 12-50 m³ buckets, load 500-3,000 tonne/hour) or front-end loaders (10-30 m³ buckets, more mobile for scattered piles). Load blasted limestone into haul trucks. **[Haul trucks](mining-trucks):** Off-highway rigid dump trucks (50-400 tonne payload, Caterpillar 777 90-tonne, 785 150-tonne, 793 220-tonne, Komatsu 830E 320-tonne, Liebherr T284 400-tonne). Articulated dump trucks (ADT, 25-50 tonne, better for steep grades or soft ground). Cycle time: load 2-5 min (3-8 passes per truck), haul 5-20 min (distance 0.5-5 km quarry to crusher, speed 30-50 km/h loaded, 40-60 km/h empty, grades <10%), dump 1-2 min, return 5-15 min. Total cycle: 15-45 min. Fleet size: 5-50 trucks per quarry (match loader capacity, minimize wait time, fleet management systems optimize dispatch). **Crushing plant feed:** Dump limestone into hopper (steel-reinforced concrete, 50-500 m³ capacity, 2-10m deep) with grizzly bars (screen oversize >1.5m, divert to secondary blasting or rockbreaker hydraulic hammer). Apron feeder or belt feeder (chain-driven or rubber belt, 100-5,000 tph) regulates flow to crusher (prevent surges, protect equipment).

**Step 89-112: [Primary Crushing](jaw-crusher)**
Reduce 1,000mm boulders to 100-300mm. **[Jaw crusher](jaw-crusher):** Most common primary crusher (70% of quarries). Two jaws: fixed jaw (stationary) and swing jaw (pitman, reciprocates via eccentric shaft). Feed: gravity from hopper, limestone enters top. Compression: swing jaw moves toward fixed jaw (100-300 cycles/min, stroke 20-50mm, crushing force 200-2,000 tonnes), fractures rock along weakest planes. Discharge: crushed product exits bottom (closed side setting CSS 100-200mm, determines maximum product size). Throughput: 100-1,500 tph (depending on feed size, limestone hardness, machine size 600×900mm to 1,500×2,100mm jaw dimensions). **Gyratory crusher (alternative, large operations):** Conical crushing head gyrates within concave bowl (continuous crushing vs intermittent jaw). Advantages: higher capacity 1,000-10,000 tph, smoother operation. Disadvantages: higher capex $2-10M vs jaw $0.5-2M, requires more headroom (15-30m tall vs jaw 4-8m), difficult maintenance. **Product:** Primary crushed limestone, 100-300mm (mixed size distribution, <10% fines -10mm, 30-50% mid-size 50-150mm, 40-60% oversize 150-300mm). Convey to secondary crushing or screening.

**Step 113-135: [Screening](vibrating-screen) and Secondary Crushing**
**Scalping screen:** Remove fines before secondary crushing (reduce crusher load, wear). Vibrating screen (inclined 15-25°, 2-3 decks, 50-150mm aperture top deck, motor-driven eccentric weights generate 3-5g acceleration, 900-1,200 rpm). Oversize (>150mm) to secondary crusher. Undersize (<150mm) bypasses to tertiary crushing or product stockpiles. **Secondary crushing:** Cone crusher (standard or shorthead), impact crusher (horizontal shaft impactor HSI), or roll crusher. **Cone crusher:** Rotating mantle within stationary concave (similar principle to gyratory but smaller, 1-5m diameter vs gyratory 2-10m). Feed 100-300mm, discharge 10-50mm (CSS). Throughput: 50-1,000 tph. Limestone hardness: medium-soft (Mohs 3, compressive strength 50-150 MPa, easy to crush, lower wear vs hard rock granite basalt). **Impact crusher:** Rotor with hammers or blow bars (800-1,500 rpm) strikes limestone, throws against impact plates (breaker bars), produces cubical particles (better shape than compression crushers, preferred for concrete aggregate). Reduction ratio: 10-20:1 (feed 300mm → product 15-30mm single stage). High wear: limestone abrasive if silica content >5%, replace blow bars every 500-5,000 hours depending on hardness. **Tertiary crushing (if needed):** Further reduce to 5-20mm for specific products (fine aggregate, filler). Cone crusher or VSI (vertical shaft impactor, rock-on-rock crushing, produces manufactured sand).

**Step 136-155: Classification and Product Sizing**
**Multi-deck screening:** Separate crushed limestone into size fractions. Vibrating screens (3-5 decks stacked vertically, largest aperture on top deck, progressively smaller below). **Size fractions:** (1) Riprap (200-600mm, erosion control, coastal protection, quarry-run large stones, minimal processing). (2) Large aggregate (50-200mm, 2"-6", railroad ballast, drainage stone, gabion fill). (3) Coarse aggregate (20-50mm, 1"-2", concrete mix, asphalt, road base). (4) Medium aggregate (10-20mm, 1/2"-3/4", concrete, asphalt surface). (5) Fine aggregate (2-10mm, concrete sand fraction, mortar). (6) Filler/screenings (<2mm, asphalt filler, agricultural lime). **Specification compliance:** ASTM C33 (concrete aggregate gradation), AASHTO M43 (road base), railroad ballast AREMA 24 (angularity, durability, size). Testing: sieve analysis (particle size distribution PSD), Los Angeles abrasion (tumbling test, <40% mass loss indicates durable aggregate), soundness (magnesium sulfate freeze-thaw, <12% loss for high-quality). **Washing (optional):** Spray water removes clay, silt, fines adhering to aggregate (improves quality for concrete, removes deleterious materials). Scrubber: rotating log washer or trommel screen with water jets. Clarifier: thickener tank settles fines (0.1-10 µm clay particles, flocculant addition), underflow (20-40% solids slurry) to filter press or settling pond, overflow recirculates (closed-loop water system 80-95% recycling). **Stockpiling:** Radial stacking conveyors build conical piles (5,000-100,000 tonnes per stockpile, 10-25m tall, 30-50m diameter base). Load trucks, railcars, barges for shipment.

**Step 156-170: Cement Industry - Dominant Use**
70% of limestone (3.2 Gt/year) consumed in cement production. Portland cement manufacturing: blend limestone (80%) + clay/shale (15%) + gypsum (5%), grind, fire in rotary kiln at 1,450°C → clinker (nodules, calcium silicates C₃S + C₂S), grind → cement powder. **Limestone specifications for cement:** 75-85% CaCO₃ (balance SiO₂ 5-15%, Al₂O₃ 2-5%, Fe₂O₃ 1-4%, MgO <5% critical - high MgO causes expansion/cracking in concrete). Limestone quarries often vertically integrated with cement plants (co-located, minimize transport 0-5 km vs 50-200 km if separate, truck haulage $0.50-2/tonne·km uneconomical for low-value commodity). Global cement: 4.2 billion tonnes/year (2023, China 2.2 Gt 52%, India 380 Mt, EU 200 Mt, USA 90 Mt).

**Step 171-180: Flux Applications - Steel and Glass**
**Steel flux (8% of limestone, 350 Mt/year):** Blast furnace and BOF steelmaking use limestone/lime to form slag (removes sulfur, phosphorus, silica impurities from iron). Add 50-200 kg limestone per tonne steel (depending on iron ore impurities, coal ash). Flux reacts: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (decomposes at 900°C in furnace). CaO + SiO₂ → CaSiO₃ (slag, melts at 1,200-1,500°C, floats on molten metal, tap separately). Specification: >90% CaCO₃, <4% SiO₂, <0.1% P, low sulfur. Size: 10-50mm lump (minimize fines, gas permeability in blast furnace). **Glass flux:** Soda-lime glass (bottles, windows) composition: 70% SiO₂ (sand) + 15% Na₂O (soda ash) + 10% CaO (limestone provides calcium oxide) + 5% others (Al₂O₃, MgO, colorants). Limestone decomposes in glass furnace (1,500-1,600°C), CaO stabilizes glass structure (prevents water solubility, improves durability). Specification: >97% CaCO₃, <0.1% Fe₂O₃ (iron causes green tint, unacceptable for flint clear glass), low heavy metals. Global glass: 180 Mt/year consumes 18 Mt limestone.

**Step 181-190: Agricultural Lime and Chemical Uses**
**Agricultural lime (aglime, 3% of limestone, 130 Mt/year):** Neutralize acidic soils (pH <6.0, common in humid climates, high rainfall leaches Ca/Mg). Apply crushed limestone 2-10 tonnes/hectare every 3-5 years. Reaction: CaCO₃ + H₂O + CO₂ ⇌ Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻ (dissolves slowly, raises pH to 6.5-7.5 optimum for most crops). Effectiveness: calcium carbonate equivalent CCE (compares neutralizing value to pure CaCO₃ 100% CCE, dolomitic lime 90-108% CCE due to MgCO₃ content), fineness (pass 60-mesh sieve, surface area increases reactivity). **Quicklime production (2% limestone, 90 Mt/year):** Calcine limestone 900-1,200°C in rotary kiln or vertical shaft kiln. CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ ↑ (endothermic, 178 kJ/mol, requires 3.5-4.5 GJ/tonne CaO, fuel natural gas or coal). Quicklime (calcium oxide, 90-95% CaO, caustic white powder, reacts exothermically with water CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + 65 kJ/mol). Uses: steelmaking flux, water/wastewater treatment (pH adjustment, softening), construction (mortar, plaster), chemicals (calcium carbide, calcium cyanamide, precipitated calcium carbonate PCC). **Soda ash (Solvay process, historical):** React limestone + salt + ammonia → sodium carbonate Na₂CO₃ + CaCl₂. Declining (synthetic soda ash from trona ore USA, cheaper).

**Step 191-195: Economics, Pricing, and Sustainability**
Limestone: low-value high-volume commodity ($5-50/tonne depending on grade, processing, location). Crushed stone aggregate: $10-20/tonne (ex-quarry, add $0.50-2/tonne·km transport). High-calcium chemical-grade: $30-80/tonne. Dimension stone (polished limestone, travertine for buildings): $200-2,000/tonne (architectural, low volume). **Cost structure:** Mining (drilling, blasting, loading) 30-40%, crushing/screening 20-30%, overhead (land, permits, reclamation bonds, insurance) 15-25%, transport 10-30% (dominates if >50 km to market). Quarry profitability: high-volume low-margin (sell 500,000-5 million tonnes/year, $2-10/tonne profit margin, $5-50M annual profit large quarry). **Environmental:** Dust (PM10/PM2.5 emissions from blasting, crushing, haul roads, water spray suppression, bag houses on crushers). Noise (blasting, crushing 85-110 dB, residential complaints if <1 km distance, noise barriers, limited hours). Groundwater (dewatering lowers aquifer, impact wells, springs, monitor discharge quality pH, TSS). Reclamation: post-mining land use (lakes, wetlands, wildlife habitat, development). Quarries operate 20-100 years (reserves), progressive reclamation (restore mined-out benches while active mining continues). **CO₂ emissions:** Limestone extraction/processing 0.01-0.02 tonnes CO₂/tonne (diesel fuel, electricity). Cement production: 0.5-0.6 tonnes CO₂ per tonne cement from limestone calcination (CaCO₃ → CaO releases CO₂, 60% of cement industry emissions process-related, 40% fuel combustion, total 2.5 Gt CO₂/year from global cement 8% of anthropogenic emissions). Mitigation: alternative binders (geopolymer, calcium sulfoaluminate cements), carbon capture (post-combustion amine scrubbing, add $30-80/tonne cement cost).
