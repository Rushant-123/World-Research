Enameled copper motor wire, also known as magnet wire or winding wire, is a specialized conductor used in electric motor coils, transformers, inductors, and electromagnetic devices. The wire consists of high-purity copper drawn to precise diameters and coated with thin, electrically insulating enamel layers. This manufacturing process produces wire ranging from AWG 10 (2.588mm) to AWG 44 (0.051mm) with thermal ratings from 155°C to 240°C.

## Raw Material Preparation and Inspection

The process begins with receiving 8mm diameter oxygen-free high-conductivity copper rod with 99.99% purity. Incoming copper rods are inspected for surface defects, measuring electrical conductivity at 101% IACS minimum using eddy current testing. Rods are stored in humidity-controlled warehouse at 20-25°C to prevent oxidation. Each coil is tagged with batch number, mill certificate, and date of receipt.

Surface preparation involves removing any oxidation or contamination using mechanical brushing with nylon wheels. Rods pass through ultrasonic cleaning bath containing mild alkaline detergent at 60°C for 5 minutes. Rinsing occurs in three-stage deionized water cascade, followed by hot air drying at 80°C. Pre-draw inspection checks for surface scratches exceeding 0.05mm depth using optical comparator. Cleaned rods are lubricated with water-soluble drawing compound applied by spray nozzles.

## Coarse Wire Drawing Operations

The copper rod enters the breakdown drawing machine, which reduces diameter from 8mm to 3.5mm in single pass through tungsten carbide die. Drawing speed operates at 8 meters per second with die angle of 12 degrees and reduction ratio of 1.65:1. Lubricant flood cooling maintains die temperature below 60°C during operation. Wire tension is monitored at 45-55% of tensile strength to prevent work hardening.

Second-stage drawing reduces wire from 3.5mm to 2.0mm using four sequential dies in tandem arrangement. Each die provides 15-20% reduction in cross-sectional area with 10-degree approach angle. Intermediate annealing occurs between second and third die at 450°C in nitrogen atmosphere for 3 seconds. Wire passes through pinch rollers maintaining 8kg tension between drawing stages. Dancer arm tension control compensates for speed variations between drawing blocks.

Third-stage drawing continues reduction from 2.0mm to 1.2mm through six dies in wet drawing configuration. Water-based lubricant is recycled through 10-micron filtration system removing copper fines. Die wear is monitored every 50km of production using laser measurement system. Wire velocity increases to 12 meters per second as diameter decreases. Accumulator system between stages provides 30 seconds of buffer capacity.

## Intermediate Wire Drawing

Fourth-stage drawing reduces diameter from 1.2mm to 0.7mm through eight sequentially positioned dies. Drawing blocks operate with individual servo motors maintaining precise speed synchronization. Laser diameter measurement after each die provides real-time feedback for tension control. Wire temperature rises to 120°C due to plastic deformation energy. Cooling jets between dies maintain temperature below 150°C to prevent copper oxidation.

Fifth-stage drawing targets 0.4mm diameter using twelve dies arranged in vertical drawing tower. Capstan accumulator provides continuous operation during coil changeover at input. Each die is temperature-monitored with automatic shutdown if temperature exceeds 80°C. Vibration sensors detect die chipping or bearing failure. Wire straightness is measured using laser alignment system with tolerance of 0.3mm over 1-meter length.

## Fine Wire Drawing to Target Gauge

For AWG 20 (0.812mm) production, wire passes through six additional dies reducing from 0.4mm. Drawing speed decreases to 6 meters per second to maintain surface finish quality. Die material changes to synthetic diamond providing 20x longer life than carbide. Lubricant composition switches to specialized ester-based fluid for better wetting. Wire is continuously monitored with laser micrometer measuring at 1000Hz sampling rate.

For AWG 28 (0.321mm) production, fourteen additional drawing stages reduce from 0.4mm baseline. Each pass provides 8-12% area reduction to prevent wire breakage. Die replacement occurs every 150km of production to maintain diameter tolerance. Surface roughness is verified at 0.1μm Ra maximum using contact profilometer sampling. Tension control maintains 30-40% of ultimate tensile strength throughout fine drawing.

For AWG 36 (0.127mm) production, twenty-two drawing passes are required from 0.4mm intermediate diameter. Drawing speed reduces to 2 meters per second due to low wire breaking strength. Dies are replaced every 80km to maintain ±0.001mm diameter tolerance. Humidity control in drawing area maintains 40-50% RH to prevent static electricity. Wire cleanliness reaches Class 100 cleanroom standard before enamel coating.

## Thermal Annealing Process

Drawn wire enters continuous annealing furnace with four temperature zones under nitrogen atmosphere. Entry zone preheats wire to 250°C over 3-second residence time to drive off moisture. Main annealing zone maintains 550°C for 8 seconds to recrystallize copper grain structure. Cooling zone reduces temperature to 200°C using nitrogen quenching over 5 seconds. Exit zone completes cooling to 50°C before wire contacts spooler.

Annealing atmosphere contains 99.995% pure nitrogen with oxygen content below 50ppm to prevent oxidation. Furnace pressure maintains +2mm water column positive pressure preventing air ingress. Heating elements are silicon carbide providing uniform temperature profile within ±5°C. Wire tension through furnace maintains 2-3kg preventing sagging into heating elements. Temperature is verified every hour using calibrated thermocouples traceable to NIST standards.

Post-annealing testing measures electrical conductivity at minimum 101% IACS using Kelvin bridge method. Tensile strength reduces to 220-250 MPa indicating proper annealing. Elongation increases to 25-35% measured on 250mm test specimens. Grain size is verified at 0.020-0.040mm using metallographic cross-sections. Wire hardness measures 50-60 HV using Vickers microhardness tester with 100g load.

## Enamel Coating Preparation

Polyurethane enamel is prepared by mixing base resin with solvent in 40:60 ratio by weight. Resin molecular weight ranges from 30,000 to 50,000 Daltons for optimal film formation. Naphtha solvent provides appropriate viscosity of 25-30 seconds measured by Zahn #4 cup. Pigments and stabilizers are added at 2% concentration to improve thermal aging. Mixture is filtered through 25-micron mesh removing particles that could cause coating defects.

Polyester-imide enamel preparation involves blending modified polyester resin with polyimide precursor. Solvent mixture contains 70% cresol and 30% xylene to dissolve high-temperature resins. Viscosity is adjusted to 35-40 seconds Zahn #4 cup for proper die filling. Catalysts are added at 1% concentration to promote cross-linking during curing. Enamel is degassed under vacuum to remove dissolved air preventing coating bubbles.

Polyimide enamel for highest temperature class uses polyamic acid precursor dissolved in N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone. Solids content is maintained at 30-35% for first coat application. Viscosity reaches 45-50 seconds Zahn #4 cup due to high molecular weight polymer. Additives include flow promoters and adhesion enhancers at 3% total concentration. Storage temperature is controlled at 15-20°C to prevent premature imidization reaction.

## First Enamel Coat Application

Annealed copper wire passes through first coating die at 1-3 meters per second depending on wire gauge. Die orifice diameter exceeds wire diameter by 0.015-0.025mm controlling wet enamel thickness. Felt wipers before die remove any oil or contamination from copper surface. Wire draws enamel from reservoir maintained at constant level by overflow system. Die temperature is controlled at 25°C ±2°C affecting enamel viscosity and flow.

Coating die material is tungsten carbide with diamond-polished bore finish below 0.05μm Ra. Die geometry includes 30-degree entrance angle, parallel land of 3mm, and slight exit taper. Multiple dies in series allow viscosity grading from low to high solids content. Wire tension through coating section maintains 1-2kg for thin gauges, 3-5kg for heavy gauges. Wiping dies after coating remove excess enamel controlling final wet film thickness.

## First Coat Curing Process

Coated wire immediately enters vertical curing oven with 8-meter heated length. First zone raises temperature to 180°C over 2 seconds causing solvent flash-off. Second zone maintains 250°C for 3 seconds partially curing enamel film. Third zone reaches peak temperature of 420°C for 4 seconds completing polyurethane cure. Fourth zone cools to 150°C over 3 seconds preventing thermal shock. Final cooling to 40°C occurs in ambient air before second coating.

Oven atmosphere uses forced convection with air velocity of 3-5 meters per second for efficient heat transfer. Solvent vapors are exhausted to thermal oxidizer operating at 750°C destroying volatile organic compounds. Temperature uniformity within zones maintains ±8°C across oven width measured by profiling sensors. Wire speed through oven is precisely controlled maintaining 3±0.1 seconds at peak temperature. Oven insulation limits heat loss to 5% of input energy.

First coat thickness is measured inline using laser optical micrometer with 0.001mm resolution. Target thickness ranges from 0.008mm for thin wire to 0.015mm for heavy gauge wire. Coating uniformity is verified by rotating laser measurement around wire circumference. Any thickness variation exceeding ±15% triggers speed adjustment or die cleaning. Pinhole detection uses high-voltage probe at 50V per micron of insulation applying 400-1000V test voltage.

## Second Enamel Coat Application

Wire with cured first coat passes through second coating die after surface inspection station. Second coat enamel may be same formulation or higher-build version with 40-45% solids content. Die clearance is adjusted to 0.020-0.030mm over first-coat diameter for proper wet thickness. Coating speed may reduce by 20% allowing thicker film application without sagging. Temperature control becomes critical as first coat affects second coat adhesion through surface energy.

Intercoat adhesion is enhanced by plasma treatment between coats for some wire types. Corona discharge at 10kV ionizes air improving enamel wetting of cured first coat. Treatment power is adjusted based on line speed maintaining 2-3W/cm² surface energy density. Ozone generated by plasma is exhausted to prevent copper oxidation. Wire grounding prevents static charge accumulation during plasma exposure.

Second die configuration may use higher-viscosity enamel requiring increased reservoir temperature to 35°C. Die land length extends to 5mm providing additional flow development length. Post-coating wipers are closer tolerance removing more excess enamel. Tension through second coating increases to 2-3kg for thin wire maintaining straightness. Die inspection occurs every 4 hours checking for enamel buildup or carbide wear.

## Second Coat Curing Process

Second coat curing uses modified temperature profile optimized for film lamination. Entry zone at 160°C drives off solvent more slowly preventing first coat reflow. Intermediate zone at 280°C begins cross-linking second layer to first coat. Peak temperature reaches 450°C for polyurethane or 500°C for polyester-imide formulations. Extended dwell time of 5 seconds at peak ensures complete cure through both layers.

Oven for second coat may be longer at 12-meter heated length accommodating slower speed. Air velocity increases to 6 meters per second improving heat transfer to thicker coating. Exhaust volume increases by 40% handling higher solvent load from thicker application. Temperature profiling occurs weekly verifying cure schedule using thermocouples attached to wire. Cross-linking is verified by solvent rub test checking for incomplete cure.

## Third Enamel Coat Application and Curing

For heavy-build insulation, third coat is applied using highest-viscosity enamel at 50% solids. Die clearance expands to 0.025-0.035mm accommodating cumulative coating thickness. Line speed reduces by additional 15% allowing proper leveling before cure. Third coat formulation often includes toughening agents improving mechanical properties. Surface appearance after third coat shows characteristic gloss indicating proper flow.

Third coat curing peaks at 480°C for polyurethane, 540°C for polyester-imide systems. Total oven residence time extends to 25 seconds allowing heat penetration through all layers. Cooling rate is controlled below 50°C per second preventing thermal stress cracks. Wire exits final oven at 30-35°C ready for testing and spooling. Total coating thickness after three coats ranges from 0.025mm to 0.060mm depending on wire gauge.

## Polyimide High-Temperature Wire Processing

Polyimide coating requires different processing due to imidization chemistry converting polyamic acid to polyimide. First coat applies polyamic acid solution at reduced line speed of 0.5-1.5 meters per second. Initial drying at 150°C removes solvent without imidization over 5 seconds. Imidization occurs in two stages: 300°C for 5 seconds forming intermediate structure, then 450°C for 8 seconds completing reaction.

Second polyimide coat applies over fully imidized first layer using same resin system. Intercoat adhesion develops through residual amine groups bonding during second imidization cycle. Peak cure temperature reaches 500°C driving off water formed during imidization reaction. Extended cooling to 50°C takes 10 seconds preventing coating embrittlement from thermal shock. Polyimide wire achieves 220°C-240°C thermal class rating after complete cure.

## Dielectric Breakdown Voltage Testing

High-voltage testing applies AC voltage between wire and grounded electrode submerged in water bath. Test voltage for standard build wire is calculated as 2000V plus 500V per mil of coating thickness. Heavy-build wire tests at 4000-8000V depending on insulation thickness and thermal class. Voltage ramp rate is controlled at 500V per second reaching test level without overshoot. Duration at test voltage is 3-5 seconds detecting weak spots in insulation.

Testing electrode consists of brass tube with 10mm length providing defined contact area. Water resistivity is maintained at 10,000 ohm-cm minimum using deionization system. Any breakdown current exceeding 10mA triggers reject signal marking defect location. Breakdown locations are analyzed microscopically identifying cause: contamination, pinhole, or coating thin spot. Testing frequency is 100% continuous inline for critical applications, or 1000-foot intervals for standard grades.

## Continuity and Pinhole Detection

Continuity test applies low voltage detecting wire breaks or high-resistance splices. Four-wire resistance measurement eliminates contact resistance errors using Kelvin connection method. Resistance tolerance is calculated from copper resistivity, wire gauge, and temperature coefficient. Any resistance increase exceeding 5% from calculated value indicates diameter reduction or alloy contamination. Testing occurs every 500 feet with automatic marking of suspect sections.

Pinhole detection employs water-bath high-voltage test similar to breakdown testing but lower voltage. Test voltage is 50% of rated breakdown voltage ensuring non-destructive evaluation. Electrode length extends to 500mm detecting multiple defects per test zone. Current threshold sets at 1mA detecting pinholes before they cause breakdown. Detection rate exceeds 99.9% for pinholes larger than 10 microns diameter.

## Flexibility and Elongation Testing

Bend test wraps wire around mandrel with diameter 3-5x the wire diameter depending on gauge. Wire is wrapped 10 complete turns under minimal tension checking for enamel cracking or peeling. Mandrel surface is inspected under magnification detecting cracks larger than 1 micron. Test is performed at room temperature and again at maximum rated temperature verifying thermal flexibility. Some specifications require flexibility after heat aging at 20°C above rated temperature.

Elongation measures on 250mm test specimen pulled at 50mm per minute strain rate. Finished enameled wire achieves 15-25% elongation to failure depending on coating thickness. Bare wire after stripping enamel should recover to 25-35% elongation verifying proper annealing. Reduction in elongation indicates coating brittleness or copper work hardening. Testing frequency is once per production lot or every 50,000 feet.

## Adherence Testing by Snap Test

Snap test rapidly elongates wire specimen causing enamel to crack at characteristic elongation level. Wire is secured in tensile tester grips 100mm apart and pulled at 500mm per minute. Microscope examination of fractured region counts number of coating pieces remaining on copper. Good adherence shows 20+ coating fragments; poor adherence shows 5 or fewer. Test correlates to thermal cycling performance and mechanical winding stress resistance.

## Heat Shock Testing for Thermal Class

Heat shock test subjects wire to temperature 25°C above rated class in air-circulation oven. Wire is wrapped around mandrel matching flexibility test diameter and aged for 30 minutes. After cooling to room temperature, wire is unwrapped and examined under magnification. Any coating cracks, checks, or delamination indicate inadequate thermal performance. Test is performed on production start-up and repeated every 8 hours during run.

## Thermal Aging for Life Prediction

Accelerated thermal aging exposes wire to elevated temperatures predicting motor life expectancy. For Class F (155°C) wire, aging occurs at 175°C, 200°C, and 225°C for 1000+ hours. ## Solderability Testing

Solder test verifies enamel removes easily allowing coil termination without mechanical stripping. Dip soldering immerses wire end in 350°C solder pot containing 60/40 tin-lead alloy. Enamel should decompose in 3-5 seconds allowing solder to wet copper surface completely. Dwell time and temperature are recorded ensuring consistent performance across production. Solderability grades range from Grade 1 (self-fluxing instant removal) to Grade 3 (requires pre-strip or abrasion).

Some magnet wire uses solderable enamel overcoat allowing direct soldering of coil connections. These wires undergo modified solder test at 400°C for 8 seconds verifying overcoat removal. Solder bond strength is tested pulling wire from connection pad measuring force required. Minimum bond strength of 5N for AWG 30 increases proportionally for heavier gauges. Thermosetting overcoats require separate curing process after winding before soldering.

## Chemical Resistance Testing

Solvent resistance test immerses wire in common motor chemicals: refrigerant oil, transformer oil, coolant. Wire wraps around mandrel forming coil simulating motor winding geometry. Immersion at 90°C for 1000 hours stresses coating with thermal and chemical degradation. Post-exposure testing measures dielectric breakdown voltage, flexibility, and adherence changes. Refrigerant compatibility testing exposes wire to R134a, R410a, or other refrigerants under pressure. Sealed vessel containing wire sample is pressurized to 300psi at elevated temperature. ## Motor Winding Application Considerations

Automated winding equipment tensions magnet wire from 50g to 500g depending on gauge. Improper tension causes enamel damage or winding errors requiring careful tensioner calibration. Layer insulation between winding layers prevents turn-to-turn voltage breakdown. Impregnation with varnish fills air voids improving thermal conductivity and voltage withstand.

Thermal cycling in motor operation expands and contracts copper causing enamel stress. Differential expansion between copper and enamel requires flexible coating formulation. Mechanical vibration can abrade insulation at crossover points necessitating abrasion-resistant coatings. End-of-life failure typically occurs from thermal degradation after 40,000-100,000 operating hours.
