# How to Make Thermal Interface Paste for Mac Cooling

**Market Leader:** [Shin-Etsu](/companies/shin-etsu)



Thermal interface material (TIM) that conducts heat between processor dies and heatsinks in Mac computers. High-performance paste achieves 3-8 W/mK thermal conductivity through optimized filler loading and viscosity control. Applied in ultra-thin layers (25-75 microns) to maximize thermal transfer while resisting pump-out during thermal cycling.

## What is it?

Viscous compound consisting of silicone oil matrix filled with thermally conductive particles (silver, aluminum oxide, zinc oxide, or boron nitride). Consistency ranges from 150-400 Pa·s at 25°C. Fills microscopic surface irregularities on CPU/GPU and heatsink contact surfaces. Non-curing formulation maintains pliability across -40°C to +180°C temperature range. Critical for Mac thermal management due to compact chassis and limited airflow.

## Manufacturing Process

### Raw Material Preparation

1. Source pharmaceutical-grade polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) silicone oil with 1000-10000 cSt viscosity
2. Verify silicone oil purity exceeds 99.5% through gas chromatography-mass spectrometry
3. Inspect silver powder particle size distribution: 1-5 micron diameter with D50 at 2.5 microns
4. Measure silver particle sphericity using scanning electron microscopy (target >0.85 sphericity index)
5. Confirm silver purity at 99.9%+ through X-ray fluorescence spectroscopy
6. Analyze ceramic filler (aluminum oxide or boron nitride) for mean particle size 0.5-2 microns
7. Verify ceramic filler aspect ratio <3:1 for optimal packing density
8. Pre-dry all filler materials at 120°C for 4 hours to remove absorbed moisture
9. Store dried fillers in nitrogen-purged containers with <50 ppm humidity
10. Prepare mixing equipment by cleaning with isopropanol and drying with filtered air
11. Calibrate high-shear mixer to 2000-5000 rpm operational range
12. Set up three-roll mill with gap settings: feed gap 50 microns, apron gap 10 microns
13. Install vacuum degassing chamber capable of <10 mbar absolute pressure
14. Prepare stainless steel mixing vessels pre-cleaned with acetone and plasma treatment
15. Set up cleanroom environment with ISO Class 7 (10,000 particles/ft³) specification

### Formulation Development

16. Calculate target filler loading at 65-75% by weight for optimal thermal conductivity
17. Determine silver-to-ceramic filler ratio at 60:40 for balanced performance and cost
18. Weigh silicone oil base: 30% of total formulation weight using precision balance (±0.01g)
19. Measure silver particles: 42% of total weight in separate container
20. Weigh ceramic filler: 28% of total weight for supplementary thermal conduction
21. Add surface treatment agent (silane coupling agent) at 0.5-1% of filler weight
22. Pre-mix silane with small portion of silicone oil to create wetting agent
23. Coat filler particles with wetting agent through low-shear mixing (500 rpm, 10 minutes)
24. Allow coated particles to rest for 30 minutes for complete surface modification
25. Heat silicone oil base to 60°C to reduce initial viscosity for easier filler incorporation
26. Transfer heated silicone oil to high-shear mixer vessel under nitrogen atmosphere
27. Begin mixing at 1000 rpm while maintaining 60°C temperature
28. Gradually add pre-treated silver particles over 15-minute period to prevent agglomeration
29. Increase mixing speed to 3000 rpm after all silver particles are incorporated
30. Mix for 30 minutes at high shear to break up particle clusters

### Particle Dispersion

31. Slowly introduce ceramic filler particles while maintaining 3000 rpm mixing speed
32. Add ceramic particles over 20-minute duration to ensure gradual incorporation
33. Monitor mixture temperature (should not exceed 80°C due to shear heating)
34. Install water cooling jacket if temperature rises above 75°C
35. Continue high-shear mixing for additional 45 minutes after all fillers are added
36. Sample mixture and examine under optical microscope at 400x magnification
37. Verify no visible particle clusters larger than 10 microns remain in dispersion
38. Transfer mixture to three-roll mill for further particle size reduction
39. Set feed gap at 50 microns and apron gap at 10 microns for first pass
40. Pass mixture through mill at 100 rpm roll speed
41. Collect material from apron roll and recirculate for second pass
42. Reduce apron gap to 5 microns for second milling pass
43. Examine post-milling sample under SEM to confirm particle distribution
44. Verify particle spacing averages 0.5-2 microns between nearest neighbors
45. Measure post-milling viscosity using rotational viscometer with cone-and-plate geometry

### Viscosity Adjustment

46. Target final viscosity of 200-350 Pa·s at 25°C and 10 s⁻¹ shear rate
47. If viscosity exceeds target, add small amounts (0.5-1%) of low-viscosity silicone oil
48. If viscosity is too low, increase filler loading incrementally by 1-2%
49. Re-mix after each adjustment at 2000 rpm for 15 minutes
50. Measure viscosity after each adjustment cycle until target is reached
51. Perform rheology sweep from 0.1 to 100 s⁻¹ shear rate to characterize flow behavior
52. Verify paste exhibits slight shear-thinning behavior (pseudoplastic)
53. Confirm viscosity at 0.1 s⁻¹ (low shear) is 2-3x higher than at 10 s⁻¹
54. Test thixotropic recovery: measure viscosity immediately after high shear and after 5 minutes rest
55. Ensure viscosity recovers to within 90% of original value after rest period
56. Adjust thixotropic agent (fumed silica at 0.5-2%) if recovery is insufficient
57. Add fumed silica in 0.1% increments with thorough mixing between additions
58. Verify fumed silica integration by checking for uniform opacity throughout paste
59. Perform final viscosity measurement at 5, 25, and 40°C to characterize temperature dependence
60. Calculate viscosity temperature coefficient (should be -3% to -5% per °C)

### Degassing Process

61. Transfer paste to vacuum degassing chamber with planetary mixer capability
62. Start gentle planetary mixing at 30 rpm to expose internal bubbles
63. Gradually reduce chamber pressure from atmospheric to 100 mbar over 5 minutes
64. Hold at 100 mbar for 10 minutes while continuing planetary mixing
65. Further reduce pressure to 10 mbar over next 10 minutes
66. Observe vigorous bubbling as entrapped air escapes from paste
67. Continue mixing at 10 mbar for 30 minutes until bubbling ceases
68. Increase mixing speed to 50 rpm for final 10 minutes to mobilize remaining bubbles
69. Slowly vent chamber to atmospheric pressure over 5 minutes to prevent bubble re-entrainment
70. Inspect degassed paste for bubble-free appearance (smooth, uniform surface)
71. Measure paste density using helium pycnometry to verify complete degassing
72. Compare measured density to theoretical density (should be >98% of theoretical)
73. If density is low, repeat degassing with longer hold time at low pressure
74. Check for proper particle suspension: no settling after 24 hours standing
75. Add anti-settling agent (organoclay at 0.5-1%) if settling is observed

### Thermal Conductivity Optimization

76. Prepare thermal conductivity test samples using laser flash apparatus standards
77. Cast paste between two aluminum plates separated by 100-micron spacer
78. Cure sample fixture in thermal chamber at 80°C for 2 hours to stabilize
79. Cool sample to 25°C and measure thickness with micrometer (target 100±5 microns)
80. Load sample into laser flash apparatus for through-thickness conductivity measurement
81. Perform measurements at 25°C, 50°C, and 75°C to characterize temperature dependence
82. Calculate thermal conductivity from thermal diffusivity, specific heat, and density
83. Target thermal conductivity: 3-5 W/mK for ceramic-filled, 5-8 W/mK for silver-filled
84. If conductivity is below target, increase filler loading by 2-5% and re-test
85. Verify viscosity remains in acceptable range after filler adjustment
86. Optimize silver-to-ceramic ratio: increase silver content in 5% increments for higher conductivity
87. Monitor cost impact of increased silver content (silver is 50-100x more expensive than ceramic)
88. Test alternative high-conductivity fillers: boron nitride (30 W/mK bulk) or aluminum nitride (150 W/mK bulk)
89. Evaluate diamond powder addition at 1-5% for premium formulations (achieves 8-12 W/mK)
90. Measure contact resistance contribution using reference material method

### Pump-Out Resistance Testing

91. Prepare test fixture with two aluminum blocks (25x25mm) and four corner bolts
92. Apply 50mg of thermal paste to center of bottom block surface
93. Lower top block onto paste and torque corner bolts to create 0.35 MPa pressure
94. Measure initial bondline thickness using displacement sensor (target 50-75 microns)
95. Place assembly in thermal cycling chamber
96. Program thermal cycle: -40°C for 30 minutes, ramp to +125°C over 15 minutes, hold 30 minutes
97. Execute 100 thermal cycles over 5 days
98. Monitor bondline thickness continuously throughout cycling
99. Acceptable pump-out: bondline thickness increase <20% after 100 cycles
100. Remove assembly and photograph paste spread pattern from edge view
101. Measure paste extrusion distance from original contact area perimeter
102. Excessive pump-out indicated by >2mm extrusion or >50% bondline increase
103. If pump-out is excessive, increase paste viscosity by 10-20%
104. Alternatively, add higher molecular weight PDMS (50,000+ cSt) at 5-10% to improve cohesion
105. Re-test modified formulation for pump-out resistance

### Thermal Cycling Stability

106. Prepare multiple thermal conductivity test samples as described in steps 76-79
107. Place three samples in 25°C control environment (no cycling)
108. Subject three samples to accelerated thermal cycling: -40°C to +150°C, 500 cycles
109. Each cycle includes 15-minute dwell at each temperature extreme
110. Remove samples at 100, 250, and 500 cycle intervals for testing
111. Measure thermal conductivity at each interval and compare to uncycled control
112. Acceptable stability: <10% conductivity decrease after 500 cycles
113. Examine post-cycling samples under microscope for phase separation or filler settling
114. Perform differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) to detect polymer degradation
115. Check for weight loss indicating volatile component evaporation
116. If conductivity degrades significantly, evaluate thermal stability of silicone base
117. Consider phenyl-modified silicone oil for improved high-temperature stability
118. Add antioxidant package (hindered phenols at 0.5-1%) to prevent oxidative degradation
119. Re-test thermal cycling stability of modified formulation
120. Verify no bleeding of low molecular weight components during high-temperature exposure

### Rheological Characterization

121. Load paste sample into parallel plate rheometer with 25mm diameter plates
122. Set gap to 1000 microns and trim excess paste from plate edges
123. Perform amplitude sweep at 1 Hz frequency to determine linear viscoelastic region
124. Identify critical strain amplitude where storage modulus (G') begins to decrease
125. Conduct frequency sweep from 0.1 to 100 Hz at strain within linear region
126. Plot storage modulus G' and loss modulus G'' versus frequency
127. Calculate loss tangent (tan δ = G''/G') across frequency range
128. Verify paste exhibits gel-like behavior: G' > G'' across all frequencies
129. Measure yield stress using stress ramp protocol: 0.1 to 1000 Pa at 1 Pa/s
130. Determine yield stress as crossover point where flow initiates (typically 100-500 Pa)
131. Perform temperature ramp from 25°C to 100°C at 2°C/min while measuring viscosity
132. Verify viscosity decreases smoothly with no discontinuities indicating degradation
133. Calculate activation energy for viscous flow from Arrhenius plot
134. Test paste recovery after shear: apply high shear (100 s⁻¹) for 1 minute, then rest
135. Monitor viscosity recovery over 10-minute rest period at low shear (1 s⁻¹)

### Quality Control Testing

136. Establish batch acceptance criteria based on key parameters
137. Thermal conductivity specification: 3.0-5.0 W/mK for standard grade, 5.0-8.0 W/mK for premium
138. Viscosity specification: 200-350 Pa·s at 25°C and 10 s⁻¹ shear rate
139. Measure paste color using spectrophotometer: silver pastes should be consistent gray (L* 45-55)
140. Check for surface skin formation: spread paste thinly and observe after 24 hours exposure
141. No skin formation acceptable for non-curing formulations
142. Test electrical resistivity using two-point probe method (target >10¹¹ Ohm·cm for non-conductive)
143. Conductive formulations with high silver content may exhibit 0.001-0.1 Ohm·cm resistivity
144. Measure volatile content by weight loss at 150°C for 24 hours (should be <1%)
145. Perform particle size analysis using laser diffraction on diluted sample
146. Verify D90 particle size <10 microns to prevent bondline thickness issues
147. Check pH of aqueous extract (should be neutral, 6-8) to ensure no corrosive species
148. Test paste compatibility with common metal surfaces: aluminum, copper, nickel
149. Apply paste to metal coupons and heat at 100°C for 1000 hours, then inspect for corrosion
150. No visible corrosion, discoloration, or weight change acceptable

### Application Method Development

151. Determine optimal application pattern for Mac CPU/GPU geometries
152. Small dies (<100mm²): single dot method with 5-10mg paste
153. Medium dies (100-400mm²): thin line method or small cross pattern
154. Large dies (>400mm²): spread method using plastic spreader to 50-micron thickness
155. Calibrate dispensing equipment: pneumatic syringe dispenser or robotic needle dispenser
156. Set dispense pressure at 40-60 psi for consistent dot size (3-4mm diameter)
157. Program dispense time at 0.5-1.0 seconds for targeted paste volume
158. Verify dispense weight using precision balance (±1mg accuracy)
159. Test clamping force required for optimal bondline: 0.2-0.5 MPa for most applications
160. Apply paste to CPU simulator heater block and measure bondline thickness after clamping
161. Adjust paste volume to achieve target bondline of 50-75 microns
162. Excessive paste leads to thick bondline and reduced thermal performance
163. Insufficient paste causes dry spots and high thermal resistance
164. Create application instruction documents with photos showing proper technique
165. Include warnings about over-application and uneven spreading

### Packaging and Storage

166. Select packaging format: 1-5 gram syringes for consumer, 30-50 gram cartridges for industrial
167. Use polypropylene syringe barrels with airtight plunger seals
168. Install precision tip with 1-2mm inner diameter for controlled dispensing
169. Fill syringes using automated paste filling machine with positive displacement pump
170. Dispense paste into syringe barrel from bottom to prevent air entrapment
171. Insert plunger slowly to avoid creating voids
172. Cap syringe tip with removable seal to prevent drying during storage
173. Label each syringe with batch number, manufacturing date, and expiration date
174. Establish shelf life through accelerated aging studies
175. Store samples at 25°C, 40°C, and 60°C for 6 months
176. Test viscosity and thermal conductivity at monthly intervals
177. Calculate expiration date using Arrhenius extrapolation from elevated temperature data
178. Typical shelf life: 2-3 years at 25°C storage for silicone-based formulations
179. Package syringes in sealed foil pouches with desiccant packet for humidity control
180. Store finished goods at 15-25°C in low-humidity environment (<50% RH)

### Performance Validation

181. Prepare test vehicle using actual Mac CPU or equivalent thermal test chip
182. Install thermocouples or RTD sensors on die surface and heatsink base
183. Apply thermal paste using documented application method
184. Clamp heatsink with specified mounting pressure (0.3-0.4 MPa typical)
185. Allow paste to settle for 30 minutes before powering test chip
186. Apply 50W thermal load to test chip using precision power supply
187. Monitor die temperature and heatsink temperature until thermal equilibrium reached
188. Calculate thermal resistance from temperature difference and power input
189. Thermal resistance (°C/W) = (T_die - T_heatsink) / Power
190. Target thermal resistance: 0.15-0.25 °C/W for typical Mac CPU application
191. Compare performance to baseline reference paste and competitor products
192. Repeat testing with minimum of 5 samples for statistical significance
193. Perform long-term reliability test: 1000 hours at 85°C junction temperature
194. Monitor thermal resistance drift over time (should remain within ±10% of initial value)
195. Document final validated application guidelines, thermal performance data, and reliability test results for product datasheet

## Key Performance Parameters

**Thermal Conductivity**: 3-8 W/mK depending on filler selection and loading
**Viscosity**: 200-350 Pa·s at 25°C for optimal spreadability and pump-out resistance
**Operating Temperature**: -40°C to +150°C continuous, -50°C to +180°C short-term
**Bondline Thickness**: 50-75 microns optimal, 25-100 microns acceptable range
**Pump-Out Resistance**: <20% bondline thickness increase after 100 thermal cycles
**Thermal Resistance**: 0.15-0.25 °C/W for typical Mac CPU/GPU applications
**Shelf Life**: 2-3 years at room temperature storage
**Electrical Resistivity**: >10¹¹ Ohm·cm for non-conductive formulations, 0.001-0.1 Ohm·cm for conductive versions
