Corrugating medium is the wavy, fluted paper layer that forms the core structure of corrugated cardboard. This manufacturing process transforms recycled paper fibers into strong, lightweight board suitable for shipping containers and packaging. The production combines pulping, papermaking, and corrugating technologies to create various flute profiles (A, B, C, E) with specific edge crush test (ECT) values and basis weights ranging from 23-42 lbs per 1000 square feet.

## Recycled Fiber Processing (Steps 1-15)

1. **Bale Reception**: Receive compressed bales of recycled paper (OCC - old corrugated containers) weighing 800-1200 lbs each at the pulping facility loading dock
2. **Bale Inspection**: Inspect incoming bales for contamination including plastic films, metal straps, foam, and prohibited materials that could damage equipment
3. **Bale Storage**: Store bales in climate-controlled warehouse maintaining 40-60% humidity to prevent fiber degradation and spontaneous combustion
4. **Bale Conveyance**: Transport bales via conveyor system to pulping area, removing metal bands and plastic wrapping before processing
5. **Bale Breaking**: Feed bales into chain conveyor that breaks apart compressed layers, creating loose paper pieces suitable for pulping
6. **Foreign Object Detection**: Pass paper through magnetic separators removing ferrous metals and manual inspection stations for non-magnetic contaminants
7. **Pre-Wetting**: Apply water spray (120-140°F) to soften paper fibers and begin dissolving adhesives and binders before pulping
8. **Hydrapulper Loading**: Load pre-wetted paper into cylindrical hydrapulper tank (10,000-20,000 gallon capacity) with agitator rotor at bottom
9. **Pulping Water Addition**: Add process water at 140-160°F to achieve 4-6% consistency (fiber concentration), requiring 12-15 gallons water per pound of paper
10. **Mechanical Pulping**: Rotate hydrapulper rotor at 80-120 RPM for 15-25 minutes, mechanically separating fibers while preserving fiber length
11. **Chemical Addition**: Inject sodium hydroxide (0.5-1.5% on fiber weight) to adjust pH to 8.5-9.5, aiding in ink dispersion and contaminant removal
12. **Heavy Contaminant Removal**: Extract rocks, glass, metal fragments through bottom grate, collecting 2-5% of input weight as heavy rejects
13. **Pulper Extraction**: Pump dilute pulp slurry (4% consistency) through extraction plate with 3/8-inch holes, screening out large contaminants
14. **Ragger Removal**: Continuously remove rope, wire, plastic strapping caught by rotating ragger mechanism, preventing rotor damage
15. **Pulp Chest Transfer**: Pump screened pulp to intermediate chest tank (15,000 gallon capacity) with agitation maintaining fiber suspension

## Cleaning and Screening (Steps 16-35)

16. **Coarse Screening**: Pass pulp through vibrating flat screens with 0.15-0.20 inch slots, removing large fiber bundles, plastics, and undefibered paper
17. **Centrifugal Cleaning - Primary**: Pump pulp through forward cleaners (conical centrifuges) at 15-20 PSI, separating particles heavier than water
18. **Heavy Reject Collection**: Collect sand, glass, metal particles (specific gravity >1.5) from bottom apex, typically 0.5-1.5% of throughput
19. **Reverse Cleaning**: Process accepts through reverse cleaners removing lightweight contaminants (specific gravity <1.0) including plastics, foam, wax
20. **Light Reject Removal**: Extract floating contaminants from top of reverse cleaners, collecting 0.3-0.8% of fiber as light rejects
21. **Fine Screening**: Pass cleaned pulp through pressure screens with 0.10-0.15 inch slots or holes, removing smaller contaminants
22. **Screen Rotor Operation**: Rotate screening rotor at 800-1200 RPM, creating pressure pulses that force good fibers through screen plate
23. **Reject Thickening**: Process screening rejects (5-15% of flow) through secondary screens to recover good fibers before disposal
24. **Slot Screening**: Employ slotted screens (0.010-0.015 inch slots) for final contaminant removal, particularly effective for plastic films
25. **Screen Accept Collection**: Collect screened pulp at 0.8-1.2% consistency in clean pulp chest with agitation preventing fiber settling
26. **Fractionation**: Optionally pass pulp through fractionator separating long fibers (strength) from short fibers (surface smoothness)
27. **Long Fiber Recovery**: Collect long fiber fraction (>1mm) for medium layer, providing flute strength and compression resistance
28. **Deflaking**: Process pulp through high-consistency deflaker (25-35% consistency) breaking fiber clumps while preserving individual fiber length
29. **Dispersion**: Heat pulp to 180-200°F and apply mechanical shear in disperser unit, breaking ink particles to <100 microns
30. **Ink Particle Reduction**: Disperse visible ink specks through 15-20 minutes of mechanical action at elevated temperature
31. **Flotation Deinking (Optional)**: Inject air bubbles to float ink particles and small contaminants to surface for removal
32. **Chemical Deinking**: Add surfactants (0.5-1.0%), sodium silicate, and fatty acids to enhance ink particle separation
33. **Foam Skimming**: Remove ink-laden foam from flotation cell surface, reducing ink content by 60-80% for whiter paper
34. **Washing**: Spray clean water on inclined screen to displace dirty water from pulp, removing dissolved contaminants and residual chemicals
35. **Final Consistency Adjustment**: Dilute cleaned pulp to 3-4% consistency for refining, adding white water recovered from paper machine

## Refining and Stock Preparation (Steps 36-55)

36. **Refining Preparation**: Pump cleaned pulp to refining area where mechanical treatment develops fiber properties for papermaking
37. **Conical Refiner Loading**: Feed pulp (3-4% consistency) into conical refiner with rotating and stationary plates having bar patterns
38. **Fiber Fibrillation**: Apply controlled mechanical action (30-50 HP per ton/day) creating micro-fibrils on fiber surface, increasing bonding
39. **Fiber Internal Treatment**: Partially delaminate fiber wall structure, increasing flexibility and conformability for better paper formation
40. **Fiber Length Preservation**: Carefully control refining intensity to develop bonding without excessive fiber cutting, maintaining strength
41. **Refining Energy Control**: Monitor specific refining energy (40-70 kWh/ton) adjusting plate gap and pulp consistency for optimal properties
42. **Freeness Testing**: Measure Canadian Standard Freeness (CSF) every 30 minutes, targeting 650-750 mL for corrugating medium
43. **Drainage Rate Adjustment**: Achieve target freeness balancing water removal rate on paper machine with required sheet strength
44. **Fiber Quality Analysis**: Test fiber length distribution, fines content (15-25%), and fiber coarseness ensuring consistency
45. **Stock Chest Storage**: Store refined pulp in large agitated chest (20,000-30,000 gallons) providing buffer between refining and machine
46. **Consistency Control**: Maintain stock chest consistency at 3.5-4.5% with automatic dilution water control system
47. **Starch Preparation**: Cook starch (corn or wheat) at 190-205°F converting granules to viscous solution for internal sizing
48. **Starch Addition**: Inject cooked starch (0.5-1.5% on fiber) into stock for internal bonding and strength development
49. **Retention Chemical Addition**: Add cationic polymer (0.01-0.05%) improving retention of fine fibers and starch on paper machine
50. **pH Adjustment**: Add alum (aluminum sulfate) or sulfuric acid adjusting pH to 7.0-8.0 for optimal retention and paper properties
51. **Final Blending**: Mix all additives thoroughly in machine chest with agitation ensuring uniform distribution before papermaking
52. **Stuff Box Storage**: Pump prepared stock to pressurized stuff box (5-10 PSI) maintaining consistent head pressure to paper machine
53. **Consistency Profiling**: Continuously monitor and control stock consistency within ±0.1% preventing basis weight variations
54. **Temperature Control**: Maintain stock temperature at 110-130°F for optimal fiber flexibility and water removal on paper machine
55. **Flow Rate Regulation**: Control stock flow to headbox at 400-800 gallons per minute matching paper machine speed (1000-2000 ft/min)

## Fourdrinier Forming Section (Steps 56-75)

56. **Headbox Distribution**: Distribute stock uniformly across machine width (120-200 inches) through tapered headbox with flow straighteners
57. **Slice Opening**: Control headbox slice opening (0.3-0.5 inches) maintaining constant pressure differential across entire width
58. **Jet Velocity Matching**: Adjust jet velocity to 95-105% of wire speed preventing fiber orientation and formation problems
59. **Breast Roll Formation**: Deposit fiber slurry onto moving Fourdrinier wire (60-80 mesh plastic weave) at breast roll
60. **Initial Dewatering**: Remove 60-70% of water in first 10 feet through wire mesh by gravity and fiber mat formation
61. **Table Roll Support**: Support forming wire on series of table rolls preventing wire sagging and ensuring uniform dewatering
62. **Foil Dewatering**: Apply oscillating foils creating vacuum pulses (0.5-2.0 inches Hg) removing additional water without sheet disturbance
63. **Vacuum Box Application**: Apply stationary vacuum boxes (3-6 inches Hg) removing water to reach 12-15% consistency
64. **Formation Monitoring**: Inspect sheet formation continuously for fiber clumps, holes, and uniformity using optical sensors
65. **Wire Return System**: Guide wire through return roll system with showers cleaning fiber fines and extending wire life (30-60 days)
66. **Dandy Roll Application**: Apply light dandy roll pressure compacting top surface and optionally imprinting watermark pattern
67. **Final Wire Consistency**: Achieve 18-22% consistency at couch roll where sheet transfers from wire to press section
68. **Couch Roll Vacuum**: Apply high vacuum (15-20 inches Hg) at couch roll for final wire dewatering and sheet attachment
69. **Sheet Transfer**: Transfer fragile wet sheet from wire to press felt at couch roll requiring precise vacuum and pressure timing
70. **Wire Shower Cleaning**: Spray high-pressure water (200-400 PSI) on return wire removing retained fibers and maintaining drainage
71. **Wire Tension Control**: Maintain wire tension (200-400 lbs) preventing wrinkles, wire tracking problems, and premature wear
72. **Basis Weight Control**: Adjust headbox slice opening and stock flow controlling target basis weight (26, 33, or 40 lb per 1000 sq ft)
73. **Cross-Direction Profiling**: Monitor basis weight profile across machine width adjusting slice lip for ±2% uniformity
74. **Formation Quality**: Achieve formation index <25 indicating uniform fiber distribution essential for consistent strength properties
75. **Retention Measurement**: Monitor first-pass retention (85-92%) indicating efficiency of fiber and fines capture on wire

## Press Section (Steps 76-85)

76. **Press Felt Conditioning**: Condition press felts (thick wool fabrics) maintaining moisture content and compressibility for water removal
77. **First Press Nip**: Pass sheet through first press nip (200-400 PSI linear load) removing water by mechanical compression
78. **Water Expression**: Compress sheet thickness from 0.040 to 0.025 inches expressing water into absorbent press felt
79. **Consistency Increase**: Increase sheet consistency from 20% to 35-38% through first press nip water removal
80. **Felt Water Removal**: Extract water from press felt using vacuum suction boxes (15-20 inches Hg) before felt returns to nip
81. **Second Press Nip**: Apply second press nip (400-600 PSI) achieving 42-48% consistency required for efficient drying
82. **Reverse Press Configuration**: Use reverse press design where sheet contacts different felt after first nip, improving two-sidedness
83. **Press Felt Cleaning**: Continuously shower press felts with high-pressure water removing fiber fines and maintaining porosity
84. **Nip Pressure Profiling**: Control cross-direction pressure profile using adjustable crown rolls achieving uniform moisture removal
85. **Press Section Exit**: Achieve target 45-48% consistency at press section exit, having removed 25-30% of original water mechanically

## Drying Section (Steps 86-105)

86. **Dryer Can Heating**: Heat cast iron dryer cylinders (4-6 feet diameter) with 40-60 PSI steam to 280-320°F surface temperature
87. **Sheet Threading**: Thread pressed sheet over alternating steam-heated cylinders maintaining contact through felt or vacuum
88. **Contact Drying**: Transfer heat from hot cylinder surface to sheet through conduction, evaporating water at 2-4 lbs water per lb steam
89. **Dryer Fabric Tension**: Maintain dryer fabric tension (300-500 lbs) holding sheet against hot cylinders for efficient heat transfer
90. **Multi-Cylinder Configuration**: Pass sheet over 30-50 dryer cylinders arranged in upper and lower tiers maximizing drying capacity
91. **Evaporation Rate Control**: Evaporate water at controlled rate (50-80 lbs water per hour per square foot) preventing sheet distortion
92. **Moisture Profile Monitoring**: Measure cross-direction moisture profile using infrared sensors targeting ±1% uniformity
93. **Steam Condensate Removal**: Remove condensate from cylinder interiors using rotary siphons maintaining heat transfer efficiency
94. **Pocket Ventilation**: Extract humid air from pockets between cylinders using hood ventilation system removing 20,000-40,000 CFM
95. **Dryer Can Groups**: Organize dryers into groups with independent steam pressure control allowing optimized drying curve
96. **Initial Drying Phase**: Apply high heat (300-320°F) in first dryer group removing bulk water rapidly
97. **Intermediate Drying**: Reduce temperature (280-300°F) in middle groups as moisture content decreases preventing overdrying
98. **Final Drying Control**: Carefully control final moisture content to 6-8% preventing brittleness while ensuring dimensional stability
99. **Sheet Tension Management**: Control sheet tension through dryer section (2-5 pounds per linear inch) preventing wrinkles and breaks
100. **Broke System Handling**: Collect sheet breaks through broke system returning pulp to machine chest for reprocessing
101. **Dryer Fabric Cleaning**: Continuously clean dryer fabrics with vacuum and showers preventing deposit buildup reducing efficiency
102. **Drying Energy Efficiency**: Achieve thermal efficiency of 60-70% converting steam energy to water evaporation
103. **Moisture Content Uniformity**: Control final moisture content to 7.0% ±0.5% essential for consistent corrugating performance
104. **Sheet Tension Release**: Gradually reduce sheet tension approaching reel allowing sheet to relax to equilibrium dimensions
105. **Surface Temperature**: Allow sheet surface to cool from 200-220°F to 110-130°F before reeling preventing moisture migration

## Corrugating Process - Flute Formation (Steps 106-125)

106. **Corrugating Medium Roll**: Mount parent roll (8-12 feet diameter, 20-50 tons) of medium paper on corrugator unwind stand
107. **Pre-Heating**: Pass medium through pre-conditioner rolls heated to 300-350°F softening fibers for flute formation
108. **Steam Application**: Apply direct steam injection increasing moisture content to 8-10% temporarily plasticizing paper
109. **Single Facer Entry**: Feed conditioned medium into single facer machine where corrugating rolls form flutes
110. **Upper Corrugating Roll**: Engage upper corrugating roll (12-18 inch diameter) with machined flute profile around circumference
111. **Lower Corrugating Roll**: Mesh lower corrugating roll with upper roll, both heated to 350-390°F for flute setting
112. **Flute Profile Formation**: Force medium paper between meshing corrugating rolls forming precise wave pattern determined by roll geometry
113. **A-Flute Specification**: Create A-flute profile with 1/4 inch flute height, 36 flutes per linear foot, providing maximum cushioning
114. **B-Flute Specification**: Form B-flute with 1/8 inch height, 50 flutes per foot, balancing strength and material efficiency
115. **C-Flute Specification**: Produce C-flute with 11/64 inch height, 39 flutes per foot, most common general-purpose flute
116. **E-Flute Specification**: Create E-flute with 1/16 inch height, 90 flutes per foot, for thin, printable packaging applications
117. **Flute Tip Gluing**: Apply hot starch adhesive (190-210°F, 22-25% solids) to flute tips using glue roll riding against corrugating roll
118. **Adhesive Metering**: Control adhesive application rate at 15-25 lbs per 1000 square feet ensuring complete tip coverage
119. **Liner Application**: Feed flat linerboard onto glued flute tips at precise speed matching corrugated medium advance
120. **Pressure Roll Bonding**: Apply pressure roll (80-120 PSI linear) bonding liner to flute tips while adhesive is hot and wet
121. **Heat Setting**: Maintain contact pressure and heat (350°F) for 0.5-1.0 seconds allowing starch to gelatinize and bond
122. **Single-Face Board**: Produce single-face corrugated board with fluted medium glued to one flat liner at 300-500 feet per minute
123. **Single-Face Loop**: Convey single-face board through overhead bridge allowing cooling and adhesive curing before double-backing
124. **Tension Control**: Maintain single-face web tension (10-20 pounds per linear inch) preventing crushing or buckling of flutes
125. **Single-Face Inspection**: Monitor flute formation quality, glue line integrity, and adhesion strength during production

## Double-Backer Assembly (Steps 126-145)

126. **Second Liner Unwind**: Mount second linerboard roll (outer liner) on double-backer unwind stand with tension control
127. **Liner Pre-Heating**: Heat second liner to 200-250°F on steam-heated pre-heat rolls improving adhesive bonding
128. **Glue Machine Station**: Apply starch adhesive to exposed flute tips of single-face board using rider roll glue applicator
129. **Adhesive Temperature**: Maintain adhesive at 180-210°F with 20-24% solids content for optimal penetration and bonding
130. **Glue Application Control**: Apply 18-22 lbs adhesive per 1000 square feet to exposed flute tips across full board width
131. **Second Liner Layup**: Precisely align second liner onto glued flute tips as single-face board and liner converge
132. **Double-Backer Entry**: Feed assembled sandwich (liner-flute-liner) into steam-heated double-backer section
133. **Steam Platen Heating**: Heat flat steam platens (hot plates) to 350-390°F supporting and heating board from bottom
134. **Top Belt Pressure**: Apply continuous top belt pressing board against hot platens with 3-6 PSI uniform pressure
135. **Dwell Time Control**: Maintain 15-30 second contact time between hot platens and board allowing adhesive gelatinization
136. **Moisture Content Management**: Control board moisture at 6-8% preventing warping, flute crushing, or poor adhesion
137. **Multi-Platen System**: Utilize 8-15 sequential heated platens creating continuous bonding zone 40-80 feet long
138. **Cooling Section**: Allow board to cool on non-heated platens or cooling rolls preventing thermal shock and warping
139. **Board Tension**: Maintain precise tension through double-backer preventing wrinkles while avoiding flute compression
140. **Adhesive Curing**: Achieve full starch adhesive cure (>90% bond strength) before board exits double-backer
141. **Board Temperature**: Control exit temperature to 140-160°F allowing further cooling before cutting operations
142. **Double-Wall Production**: Optionally laminate two single-face boards with center liner creating double-wall construction
143. **Triple-Wall Assembly**: Assemble three single-face sections with intermediate liners for maximum strength applications
144. **Production Speed**: Operate corrugator at 300-500 feet per minute producing 200,000-350,000 square feet per hour
145. **Continuous Operation**: Run corrugator continuously 20-24 hours daily with roll changes performed at full speed

## Scoring and Slitting (Steps 146-165)

146. **Slitter-Scorer Entry**: Feed continuous corrugated board web into slitter-scorer section for dimension cutting
147. **Scoring Wheel Setup**: Mount scoring wheels (male/female pairs) at specified locations creating fold lines for box assembly
148. **Score Line Creation**: Press scoring wheels (200-400 lbs per linear inch) into board creating controlled compression for clean folding
149. **Score Depth Control**: Adjust score depth to 40-60% of board thickness allowing folding without compromising strength
150. **Longitudinal Scoring**: Create scores parallel to machine direction for flaps and manufacturer's joint
151. **Cross-Direction Scoring**: Apply scores perpendicular to travel for box top and bottom panel folds
152. **Rotary Knife Setup**: Mount rotary slitter knives on shaft at precise spacing for width trimming
153. **Knife Sharpness**: Maintain knife edge sharpness (<1 micron radius) ensuring clean cuts without crushing flutes
154. **Slitting Pressure**: Apply minimal pressure (10-20 lbs per linear inch) cutting liners without deforming corrugated structure
155. **Trim Removal**: Extract edge trim strips (2-6 inches) through vacuum conveyance returning to pulper for recycling
156. **Sheet Length Control**: Cut continuous web into sheets using rotary cutoff knife synchronized with board travel
157. **Cut-Off Accuracy**: Maintain cutting accuracy ±1/16 inch ensuring proper box dimensions and material utilization
158. **Knife Anvil Adjustment**: Set knife-to-anvil clearance at 0.001-0.003 inches for clean cutting without excessive wear
159. **Multiple Width Production**: Simultaneously produce 2-4 different sheet widths from single web through gang slitting
160. **Sheet Stacking**: Receive cut sheets on conveyor and organize into neat stacks (3-6 inches high) for further processing
161. **Stack Shingling**: Overlap sheets slightly during stacking enabling automated feeding to converting equipment
162. **Count Control**: Accurately count sheets using optical sensors ensuring correct quantity per stack (50-200 sheets)
163. **Automatic Bundling**: Band stacks with plastic or metal straps creating bundles for inventory or shipment to converters
164. **Waste Minimization**: Optimize cutting patterns achieving 92-97% material utilization from parent rolls
165. **Order Matching**: Cut sheets to exact customer specifications for width, length, score locations, and flute type

## Quality Testing - Physical Properties (Steps 166-180)

166. **Sample Collection**: Collect representative samples every 30-60 minutes from production for comprehensive testing
167. **Conditioning**: Condition samples 24 hours at 73°F and 50% relative humidity before testing per TAPPI standards
168. **Basis Weight Testing**: Weigh precise 1 square foot samples determining basis weight within ±2% of target specification
169. **Caliper Measurement**: Measure board thickness (caliper) using precision micrometer (<0.001 inch resolution) across sample
170. **Edge Crush Test (ECT)**: Test resistance to compressive forces perpendicular to flutes indicating stacking strength
171. **ECT-32 Rating**: Certify 32 lb/inch edge crush strength suitable for boxes carrying 65 pounds in single-wall configuration
172. **ECT-44 Rating**: Verify 44 lb/inch edge crush for 95-pound capacity boxes using heavier basis weight linerboards
173. **ECT-51 Rating**: Validate 51 lb/inch edge crush for double-wall board supporting >120 pounds stacked height
174. **Burst Strength Test**: Apply hydraulic pressure measuring resistance to puncture (Mullen test) correlating with handling durability
175. **Flat Crush Test**: Compress board sample flat measuring resistance to crushing force indicating pallet load capacity
176. **Pin Adhesion Test**: Pull individual flute tips from liner measuring adhesive bond strength (>100 lb/inch² minimum)
177. **Wax Pick Test**: Evaluate surface strength by applying wax at increasing temperatures testing fiber-to-fiber bonding
178. **Moisture Content**: Measure moisture using oven drying method maintaining 6-8% for dimensional stability
179. **Bending Stiffness**: Test resistance to bending in both machine and cross directions indicating box shape retention
180. **Moisture Resistance**: Subject samples to 90% humidity for 48 hours measuring strength retention (>75% ECT maintained)

## Quality Testing - Dimensional and Functional (Steps 181-195)

181. **Flute Count Verification**: Count flutes per linear foot using hand counter verifying correct flute type within ±2%
182. **Flute Height Measurement**: Measure flute height using caliper gauge ensuring conformance to specification (±5%)
183. **Take-Up Factor**: Calculate take-up ratio (fluted length to flat length) verifying manufacturing efficiency by flute type
184. **A-Flute Take-Up**: Confirm 1.54:1 take-up ratio for A-flute meaning 154 inches medium produces 100 inches single-face
185. **C-Flute Take-Up**: Verify 1.43:1 ratio for C-flute balancing material usage with strength characteristics
186. **Score Line Inspection**: Examine score lines for proper depth, alignment (±1/16 inch), and freedom from crushing
187. **Fold Endurance**: Fold scored samples 180° repeatedly testing score line durability (>20 cycles without cracking)
188. **Box Compression Test**: Assemble representative boxes testing top-to-bottom compression strength under controlled conditions
189. **Stacking Load Simulation**: Calculate safe stacking height from BCT (box compression test) values considering humidity and time
190. **Print Surface Quality**: Evaluate surface smoothness and ink receptivity for flexographic printing applications
191. **Warp Testing**: Measure board flatness after conditioning identifying manufacturing issues causing dimensional instability
192. **Delamination Check**: Inspect for liner-to-flute separation indicating adhesive or bonding problems
193. **Color Consistency**: Monitor liner and medium color matching customer specifications for visual appearance
194. **Dimensional Accuracy**: Verify cut sheet dimensions within ±1/8 inch tolerance ensuring proper box forming
195. **Certification Documentation**: Issue mill certificates documenting ECT rating, basis weights, caliper, and compliance with specifications for customer quality assurance
