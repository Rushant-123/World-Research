Manufacturing amine curing agents for epoxy resin systems through multi-stage chemical synthesis and formulation.

## Ethylenediamine (EDA) Synthesis

1. Purify anhydrous ammonia to 99.8% purity by distillation at -33°C
2. Remove moisture from ammonia to <10 ppm water content using molecular sieves
3. Prepare ethylene oxide feedstock with purity >99.5%
4. Pre-cool ammonia to -10°C in stainless steel storage vessel
5. Transfer ammonia to high-pressure reactor rated for 50 bar operation
6. Establish ammonia molar excess of 15:1 relative to ethylene oxide
7. Heat reactor to 80°C under nitrogen blanket atmosphere
8. Pressurize reactor to 30 bar with nitrogen gas
9. Install rupture disc rated for 75 bar emergency pressure relief
10. Begin controlled ethylene oxide injection at 200 g/min flow rate
11. Monitor exothermic reaction temperature, controlling below 95°C
12. Maintain reaction pressure at 28-32 bar throughout addition
13. Add ethylene oxide over 4-hour period with continuous mixing at 300 rpm
14. Hold reaction mixture at 85°C for 2 additional hours after addition complete
15. Monitor ammonia conversion by gas chromatography, target >92%
16. Cool reactor contents to 40°C over 1-hour period
17. Depressurize reactor slowly to atmospheric pressure over 30 minutes
18. Transfer crude product to distillation staging tank
19. Analyze crude mixture showing EDA 45%, piperazine 8%, DETA 25%, unreacted ammonia 15%
20. Pump crude mixture through plate heat exchanger to 60°C

## Diethylenetriamine (DETA) Production

21. Configure distillation column with 40 theoretical plates for amine separation
22. Pack column with structured packing (Sulzer BX) for high efficiency
23. Install multiple side-draw points for intermediate amine fractions
24. Heat column reboiler to establish reflux at 3:1 ratio
25. Begin continuous distillation with overhead temperature 116°C
26. Remove unreacted ammonia overhead, condense and recycle to synthesis
27. Increase column temperature to 147°C for ethylenediamine collection
28. Draw ethylenediamine side-stream at 98.5% purity, 40% yield
29. Raise temperature to 198°C for diethylenetriamine recovery
30. Collect DETA fraction with purity >97%, assay by Karl Fischer titration
31. Continue to 207°C for triethylenetetramine (TETA) separation
32. Recover TETA at 95% purity with amine value 1510 mg KOH/g
33. Collect heavy fraction containing tetraethylenepentamine (TEPA)
34. Monitor residual water content, maintain <0.3% in all fractions
35. Cool distilled amines to 25°C in stainless steel receivers
36. Blanket all storage tanks with dry nitrogen to prevent oxidation
37. Analyze amine value by titration: DETA = 1620 mg KOH/g (theoretical 1628)
38. Determine equivalent weight: DETA = 20.6 g/equivalent (theoretical 20.6)
39. Measure viscosity: DETA = 7.2 cP at 25°C
40. Record density: DETA = 0.955 g/mL at 25°C

## Cycloaliphatic Amine Synthesis

41. Load isophorone diamine (IPD) reactor with cyclohexanone feedstock
42. Add ammonia and hydrogen to high-pressure hydrogenation reactor
43. Introduce Raney nickel catalyst at 3% loading by weight
44. Pressurize reactor to 150 bar with hydrogen gas
45. Heat reactor to 180°C with vigorous stirring at 500 rpm
46. Maintain hydrogen partial pressure at 120 bar during reaction
47. Monitor cyclohexanone conversion by online infrared spectroscopy
48. React for 8 hours until >98% conversion to isophorone diamine
49. Cool reactor to 60°C while maintaining pressure
50. Filter hot product through 5-micron cartridge to remove catalyst
51. Recover and regenerate Raney nickel catalyst for reuse
52. Vacuum distill crude IPD at 150°C and 10 mbar pressure
53. Collect purified isophorone diamine at >99% purity
54. Measure amine value: IPD = 660 mg KOH/g
55. Calculate equivalent weight: IPD = 42.5 g/equivalent
56. Analyze by GC-MS confirming 3-aminomethyl-3,5,5-trimethylcyclohexylamine structure
57. Test viscosity: IPD = 14 cP at 25°C (higher than aliphatic amines)
58. Record color: Gardner 1 maximum (water-white appearance)
59. Store IPD under nitrogen blanket to prevent carbamate formation
60. Package in amber bottles to minimize light exposure

## Modified Amine Adduct Preparation

61. Load reactor with purified DETA (1000 kg batch)
62. Add liquid epoxy resin (DGEBA, EEW 185-192) at 2:1 amine:epoxy ratio
63. Calculate stoichiometry: 5 active hydrogens per DETA molecule react with epoxide groups
64. Heat mixture slowly to 80°C with paddle mixing at 150 rpm
65. Monitor exotherm as amine-epoxide reaction proceeds
66. Control temperature at 90-100°C by external cooling jacket
67. Track epoxide conversion by infrared spectroscopy (epoxide peak 915 cm⁻¹)
68. React for 4 hours until >95% epoxide groups consumed
69. Analyze residual epoxide equivalent weight >5000 g/eq (essentially complete)
70. Cool adduct to 60°C and add diluent (benzyl alcohol 15% by weight)
71. Mix thoroughly for 30 minutes to ensure homogeneous solution
72. Measure final amine value: adduct = 320-360 mg KOH/g (reduced from pure amine)
73. Calculate equivalent weight: adduct = 85-95 g/equivalent
74. Test viscosity: adduct = 450 cP at 25°C (much higher than pure amine)
75. Adduct provides longer pot life (6-8 hours vs 20-30 minutes for pure DETA)
76. Verify reduced volatility: vapor pressure <0.01 mmHg at 25°C
77. Test for skin sensitization reduction compared to unmodified amine
78. Analyze color: Gardner 5 maximum (light amber)
79. Fill into 5-gallon pails with secure lids
80. Label with amine value, equivalent weight, and mix ratio information

## Mannich Base Formulation

81. Prepare phenolic component: load 500 kg nonylphenol into reactor
82. Add formaldehyde solution (37% aqueous) at 1.1:1 molar ratio to phenol
83. Heat to 60°C and add diethylenetriamine (DETA) catalyst 5% by weight
84. Maintain pH 8-9 by adding 10% sodium hydroxide solution as needed
85. React at 80°C for 6 hours with continuous stirring at 200 rpm
86. Monitor reaction progress by sampling and methylation to check hydroxymethyl formation
87. Add additional DETA (main amine component) at calculated stoichiometry
88. Heat reaction to 120°C to drive Mannich condensation
89. Remove water byproduct by azeotropic distillation with xylene
90. Continue heating 4 hours until water removal complete (Karl Fischer <0.5%)
91. Strip xylene by vacuum distillation at 60°C and 50 mbar
92. Cool Mannich base product to 50°C
93. Add reactive diluent (cresyl glycidyl ether 20%) to reduce viscosity
94. Blend thoroughly for 1 hour at 60°C
95. Measure amine value: Mannich base = 240-280 mg KOH/g
96. Calculate equivalent weight: Mannich base = 100-115 g/equivalent
97. Test viscosity: 1200-1800 cP at 25°C
98. Mannich bases provide excellent chemical resistance in cured epoxy
99. Test for accelerated cure properties at elevated temperatures
100. Package in steel drums with nitrogen headspace

## Polyamide Curing Agent Synthesis

101. Load reactor with dimerized fatty acid (C36 dimer acid)
102. Verify dimer acid specifications: 95% dimer content, acid value 195-200
103. Add polyethylene polyamine (TEPA or PEHA) at stoichiometric ratio
104. Calculate amine:acid ratio for complete amidization with slight amine excess
105. Heat reaction mixture to 160°C under nitrogen atmosphere
106. Begin polyamidation reaction with evolution of water byproduct
107. Remove water continuously using Dean-Stark trap
108. Monitor acid value reduction from 195 to <10 mg KOH/g
109. React for 10-12 hours at 180-200°C until amidation complete
110. Track viscosity increase as polyamide molecular weight builds
111. Cool polyamide resin to 120°C
112. Add excess polyethylene polyamine to provide reactive amine groups
113. Mix thoroughly for 2 hours at 100°C
114. Cool final polyamide curing agent to 60°C
115. Measure amine value: polyamide = 200-250 mg KOH/g
116. Calculate equivalent weight: polyamide = 112-140 g/equivalent
117. Test viscosity: 5000-15000 cP at 25°C (very viscous)
118. Polyamides provide flexible, impact-resistant epoxy cures
119. Excellent for coating applications requiring elongation
120. Test Gardner color: 10-12 (amber to dark amber)

## Amine Value Determination

121. Prepare 0.5 N hydrochloric acid standard solution, standardize with tris buffer
122. Dissolve phenolphthalein indicator in ethanol (1% solution)
123. Weigh 2.0-3.0 g amine sample accurately on analytical balance
124. Dissolve sample in 50 mL isopropanol in 250 mL Erlenmeyer flask
125. Add 5 drops phenolphthalein indicator (colorless initially)
126. Titrate with standardized 0.5 N HCl from burette
127. Swirl flask continuously during titration
128. Endpoint reached when pink color just disappears (pH ~8.3)
129. Record volume of HCl consumed to 0.05 mL precision
130. Calculate amine value: (mL HCl × N HCl × 56.1) / (sample weight in g)
131. Amine value represents mg KOH equivalent per gram sample
132. Run duplicate titrations with agreement within 2%
133. For tertiary amines, use perchloric acid in acetic acid solvent
134. Calculate equivalent weight: 56100 / amine value
135. Equivalent weight represents grams of amine per active hydrogen equivalent
136. Verify calculations against theoretical values for pure compounds
137. Document all measurements in quality control logbook
138. Acceptable amine value ranges: DETA 1600-1640, TETA 1450-1520
139. Out-of-spec material quarantined for reprocessing or blending
140. Certificate of analysis reports amine value for each batch

## Equivalent Weight Calculation

141. Determine number of active hydrogen atoms per molecule from structure
142. Calculate theoretical equivalent weight: molecular weight / active hydrogens
143. DETA (MW 103.2, 5 active H) theoretical EW = 20.6 g/eq
144. TETA (MW 146.2, 6 active H) theoretical EW = 24.4 g/eq
145. Isophorone diamine (MW 170.3, 4 active H) theoretical EW = 42.6 g/eq
146. Compare experimental EW from titration to theoretical values
147. Purity calculation: (theoretical EW / experimental EW) × 100%
148. Calculate epoxy resin mix ratio: PHR = (100 × EW amine) / (EW epoxy)
149. PHR = parts per hundred resin for stoichiometric mix
150. Example: EEW 185 epoxy with DETA EW 20.6 requires 11.1 PHR
151. Adjust for practical considerations: use 10-12 PHR for slight amine excess
152. Amine excess ensures complete epoxide conversion
153. Too much excess amine causes blush, reduced chemical resistance
154. Generate mix ratio tables for each curing agent product
155. Print mix ratios on technical data sheets for customer guidance
156. Software calculator tool provided for non-standard mix ratios
157. Account for amine adducts having higher EW (less reactive hydrogens)
158. Polyamides require 50-90 PHR due to high equivalent weights
159. Temperature affects cure stoichiometry (higher temp may need less hardener)
160. Document recommended mix ratios for room temp and elevated temp cures

## Pot Life Testing

161. Prepare 100 g epoxy resin (DGEBA) at 25°C
162. Add stoichiometric amount of amine hardener based on equivalent weight
163. Mix thoroughly for 2 minutes using mechanical stirrer at 500 rpm
164. Transfer mixed resin/hardener to 150 mL beaker
165. Insert calibrated thermometer or thermocouple into center of mass
166. Record initial temperature (typically 25-28°C after exotherm from mixing)
167. Monitor temperature every 5 minutes as cure reaction proceeds
168. Define pot life as time to reach +10°C above initial temperature
169. Alternative: pot life when viscosity doubles from initial value
170. DETA pot life: 20-30 minutes at 25°C (very fast curing)
171. DETA adduct pot life: 6-8 hours at 25°C
172. Polyamide pot life: 4-6 hours at 25°C
173. Cycloaliphatic amines (IPD) pot life: 90-120 minutes at 25°C
174. Record peak exotherm temperature (DETA can reach 180-200°C in 100 g mass)
175. Larger masses generate higher exotherms due to reduced heat dissipation
176. Test multiple mass sizes: 100 g, 500 g, 2 kg to establish scaling effects
177. Reduce batch size or use adducts for large castings to prevent thermal runaway
178. Cold mixing (5-10°C) extends pot life by 2-3× for fast amines
179. Document pot life data on technical data sheets for each product
180. Retest pot life quarterly to verify aging stability of curing agents
