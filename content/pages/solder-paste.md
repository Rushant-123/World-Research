# Solder Paste Manufacturing Process

## Phase 1: Raw Material Preparation and Quality Control (Steps 1-25)

1. Receive high-purity tin ingots (99.9%+ purity) and verify supplier certificates of analysis for trace element content including lead, bismuth, antimony, and iron impurities below 100 ppm each.

2. Conduct incoming inspection of silver powder (99.9% purity) using X-ray fluorescence spectroscopy to confirm noble metal content and absence of base metal contamination.

3. Inspect copper powder shipment for particle morphology using scanning electron microscopy, verifying spherical particle shape with minimal satellite formation.

4. Test flux vehicle components including rosin esters, activators, solvents, and surfactants for viscosity, density, and chemical composition using gas chromatography-mass spectrometry.

5. Verify rheology modifier additives including thixotropic agents, wetting agents, and anti-slump compounds meet specification for molecular weight distribution and functionality.

6. Store metal powders in nitrogen-purged sealed containers at controlled temperature (18-22°C) and humidity (<30% RH) to prevent oxidation and moisture absorption.

7. Quarantine all incoming raw materials in designated staging area pending completion of quality control testing and material review board approval.

8. Prepare clean room environment for powder handling with HEPA filtration, maintaining ISO Class 7 cleanliness and controlling electrostatic discharge hazards.

9. Calibrate precision analytical balances to 0.0001g accuracy using NIST-traceable calibration weights for accurate alloy composition formulation.

10. Set up particle size analysis equipment including laser diffraction analyzer and sieve shaker for powder characterization across 20-45 micron range for Type 3-4 paste.

11. Prepare sampling protocols for raw material lot traceability, assigning unique batch numbers to each powder and flux component shipment.

12. Conduct pre-production risk assessment for material compatibility, identifying potential chemical reactions between flux activators and metal powder surfaces.

13. Review material safety data sheets for all chemical components and establish handling procedures for flammable solvents, corrosive activators, and metal dust inhalation hazards.

14. Establish environmental controls for manufacturing area including temperature stability within ±2°C, humidity control to 40-60% RH, and positive air pressure to prevent contamination.

15. Prepare process documentation including batch manufacturing records, quality control checksheets, and material consumption tracking forms.

16. Set up statistical process control charts for monitoring powder particle size distribution, flux viscosity, and paste rheological properties.

17. Calibrate rheometers for measuring viscosity at controlled shear rates (2-60 RPM) and temperature (25°C) according to IPC-TM-650 test methods.

18. Prepare reference standards for color matching, ensuring paste appearance consistency across production batches using Munsell color system.

19. Establish chain of custody procedures for precious metal powders including silver, implementing dual-count inventory systems and secure storage protocols.

20. Configure manufacturing execution system for real-time batch tracking, recipe management, and electronic batch record generation.

21. Conduct operator training on powder handling safety, covering respirator use, grounding procedures for static control, and emergency spill response.

22. Prepare mixing equipment by cleaning three-roll mills, planetary mixers, and homogenizers using approved solvents and verifying cleanliness with residue analysis.

23. Install inline monitoring sensors for paste temperature, viscosity, and homogeneity during manufacturing process.

24. Establish acceptance criteria for each raw material including particle size distribution (D10, D50, D90 values), apparent density, flowability, and surface oxidation levels.

25. Create material release certificates for approved batches, documenting test results, inspector signatures, and authorization for production use.

## Phase 2: Alloy Powder Atomization and Processing (Steps 26-50)

26. Calculate precise SAC305 alloy composition: 96.5% tin, 3.0% silver, 0.5% copper by weight, accounting for oxidation losses during melting.

27. Charge induction melting furnace with weighed quantities of tin ingots, silver shot, and copper granules in controlled atmosphere chamber.

28. Purge melting chamber with high-purity argon gas (99.999%) to displace oxygen and prevent metal oxidation during melting cycle.

29. Ramp furnace temperature to 350°C over 45 minutes, monitoring melt pool temperature using Type K thermocouples with ±1°C accuracy.

30. Homogenize molten alloy at 350°C for 30 minutes with electromagnetic stirring at 60 RPM to ensure complete dissolution and uniform composition.

31. Sample molten alloy using vacuum suction into quartz crucible and prepare test coupon for composition verification by X-ray fluorescence analysis.

32. Adjust alloy composition if needed by adding pure element shots to achieve target SAC305 specification within ±0.1% tolerance.

33. Superheat molten alloy to 450°C for gas atomization process, ensuring sufficient thermal energy for complete droplet solidification.

34. Transfer molten alloy to atomization chamber under protective argon blanket, maintaining inert atmosphere throughout transfer operation.

35. Pressurize high-purity nitrogen atomizing gas to 5 MPa (725 psi) in accumulator tanks for consistent gas jet velocity during atomization.

36. Initiate gas atomization process by flowing molten alloy through ceramic nozzle while intersecting with converging high-velocity nitrogen jets.

37. Control atomization parameters including melt flow rate (2.5 kg/min), gas pressure (5 MPa), and gas-to-metal ratio (8:1) for target particle size.

38. Collect atomized powder in large collection chamber (3 meter diameter) allowing particles to cool and solidify during flight time of 2-3 seconds.

39. Monitor atomization process continuously for nozzle clogging, gas pressure fluctuations, and melt flow interruptions that affect powder quality.

40. Complete atomization run when all molten alloy is processed, then purge system with nitrogen and allow powder to cool to room temperature.

41. Discharge powder from collection chamber using pneumatic conveying system into sealed containers under nitrogen atmosphere.

42. Conduct particle size classification using multi-deck vibratory sieve shaker with 20, 25, 32, 38, and 45 micron aperture screens.

43. Collect Type 3 powder fraction (25-45 micron) for standard fine-pitch applications requiring 0.5mm pitch components.

44. Separate Type 4 powder fraction (20-38 micron) for extra-fine-pitch applications including 0.4mm pitch and micro-BGA assemblies.

45. Analyze particle size distribution using laser diffraction analyzer, measuring D10, D50, and D90 values across 100,000+ particles per sample.

46. Verify powder sphericity using automated image analysis system, calculating circularity ratio and rejecting irregular or satellite-laden particles.

47. Measure powder apparent density using Hall flowmeter funnel method per ASTM B212, targeting 4.2-4.5 g/cm³ for SAC305 alloy.

48. Test powder flowability using angle of repose measurement and flow rate through calibrated orifice per ASTM B213 standard methods.

49. Analyze powder surface oxide layer thickness using X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, ensuring oxide thickness below 50 angstroms for good wetting.

50. Package classified powder in moisture-barrier foil bags with nitrogen atmosphere, storing at controlled temperature pending flux mixing operations.

## Phase 3: Flux Vehicle Formulation and Preparation (Steps 51-75)

51. Formulate flux vehicle starting with rosin ester base material (45-55% by weight), selecting hydrogenated rosin for thermal stability and low residue formation.

52. Add activator system containing organic acids (5-8% by weight) including adipic acid, sebacic acid, and dicarboxylic acid derivatives for oxide removal.

53. Incorporate solvent blend (25-35% by weight) using isopropanol, ethylene glycol monobutyl ether, and dipropylene glycol monomethyl ether for vapor pressure control.

54. Blend surfactant package (2-4% by weight) including polyethylene glycol derivatives and fatty acid esters to reduce surface tension and improve wetting.

55. Add thixotropic agent (3-5% by weight) using hydrogenated castor oil or fumed silica to provide shear-thinning behavior and prevent slump after printing.

56. Incorporate antioxidant additives (0.5-1% by weight) including hindered phenols and phosphite esters to prevent flux degradation during storage.

57. Add rheology stabilizers (1-2% by weight) including cellulose derivatives to maintain viscosity stability across temperature variations.

58. Charge planetary mixer with rosin ester base and heat to 60°C to reduce viscosity and facilitate mixing of solid additives.

59. Gradually add powdered activators while mixing at 45 RPM under vacuum (50 mbar) to remove entrained air and ensure complete dissolution.

60. Increase mixing speed to 120 RPM and add solvent blend slowly over 15 minutes to control exothermic dissolution reaction and prevent temperature spikes.

61. Cool flux mixture to 40°C and add surfactant package while maintaining mixing at 90 RPM for uniform distribution.

62. Disperse thixotropic agent using high-shear mixer at 3000 RPM for 10 minutes to break up agglomerates and achieve nanoscale dispersion.

63. Add antioxidant and rheology stabilizer additives during final mixing stage at 60 RPM for 20 minutes.

64. Heat flux vehicle to 70°C under vacuum (20 mbar) for 60 minutes to remove dissolved gases, moisture, and residual volatile components.

65. Cool flux to room temperature and homogenize using high-shear mixer at 1500 RPM for 15 minutes to ensure uniform consistency.

66. Filter flux vehicle through 10 micron absolute filter to remove any undissolved particles or contaminants.

67. Measure flux vehicle viscosity at 25°C using Brookfield viscometer with spindle #4 at 10 RPM, targeting 150-250 Pa·s for Type 3 paste formulation.

68. Conduct acid value titration per IPC-TM-650 method 2.3.28 to verify activator content, targeting 80-120 mg KOH/g for no-clean flux classification.

69. Test flux vehicle halide content using ion chromatography to confirm halide-free status (<500 ppm chloride, <100 ppm bromide, <100 ppm fluoride).

70. Measure flux specific gravity using pycnometer method at 25°C, verifying density of 0.95-1.05 g/cm³ for proper paste formulation calculations.

71. Analyze flux composition using Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy to verify presence of expected functional groups and absence of contamination.

72. Conduct accelerated aging test by storing flux at 50°C for 7 days and measuring viscosity change, accepting <10% deviation from initial value.

73. Test flux wetting performance using spread test on copper coupons at 250°C, measuring spread ratio and verifying >80% coverage.

74. Verify flux residue resistivity using IPC-TM-650 method 2.6.3.3, testing extracted residue for ionic contamination levels below 1.56 μg NaCl equivalent/cm².

75. Package qualified flux vehicle in moisture-proof containers with nitrogen headspace, labeling with batch number, production date, and expiration date (12 months).

## Phase 4: Solder Paste Mixing and Homogenization (Steps 76-100)

76. Calculate paste formulation ratio of 89% metal powder to 11% flux vehicle by weight for Type 3 paste targeting optimal printability and reflow performance.

77. Condition metal powder and flux vehicle to 20-22°C for 24 hours before mixing to ensure temperature equilibrium and consistent rheology.

78. Charge three-roll mill with initial flux vehicle portion (30% of total), spreading uniformly across feed roll surface.

79. Gradually feed metal powder into flux vehicle on three-roll mill while operating at slow speed (20 RPM) to initiate wetting and prevent powder segregation.

80. Increase mill roll speed progressively to 60 RPM over 10 minutes as powder incorporates into flux, monitoring torque load to prevent overloading.

81. Adjust roll gap clearances: feed roll to center roll at 50 microns, center roll to apron roll at 25 microns for intensive shearing and particle dispersion.

82. Recirculate paste through three-roll mill for 3 complete passes to ensure thorough wetting of all powder particles and break up agglomerates.

83. Scrape paste from apron roll continuously using doctor blade, collecting in chilled stainless steel container to prevent temperature rise from shear heating.

84. Monitor paste temperature during milling using infrared thermometer, maintaining below 30°C to prevent flux vehicle viscosity reduction and powder settling.

85. Add remaining flux vehicle in two increments during second and third mill passes to achieve final 89:11 metal-to-flux ratio.

86. Transfer milled paste to planetary mixer for final homogenization and rheology adjustment under vacuum conditions.

87. Mix paste under vacuum (50 mbar) at 45 RPM for 30 minutes using dual asymmetric mixing blades to remove air bubbles trapped during milling.

88. Conduct mid-process viscosity check using viscometer at 10 RPM spindle speed, targeting 180-250 Pa·s for Type 3 paste formulation.

89. Adjust paste rheology if needed by adding small quantities (<1%) of flux vehicle or thixotropic agent while mixing under vacuum.

90. Increase mixing speed to 60 RPM for final 10 minutes to ensure complete uniformity of paste consistency throughout batch.

91. Sample paste from multiple locations in mixer (top, middle, bottom) and test for viscosity uniformity, accepting ±5% variation between samples.

92. De-aerate paste under deep vacuum (10 mbar) for 15 minutes while slowly rotating mixer at 10 RPM to allow air bubbles to rise and escape.

93. Discharge paste from mixer into clean stainless steel container, scraping mixer walls thoroughly to recover all product.

94. Conduct immediate post-mixing quality control testing including viscosity, metal content, tack, and slump characteristics.

95. Measure paste viscosity using spiral viscometer with #6 spindle at 5 RPM and 10 RPM, calculating thixotropic index from ratio of viscosities.

96. Verify metal content by weighing 5g paste sample, burning off flux in muffle furnace at 500°C, and calculating percentage metal from residual weight.

97. Test paste tack using calibrated tack probe pressed into paste surface and measuring force required to separate probe after 1 second contact.

98. Conduct slump test by printing 1mm diameter paste deposits on vertical surface and measuring deposit spreading after 5 minutes at 25°C.

99. Verify paste particle dispersion by examining sample under optical microscope at 100x magnification, checking for powder agglomerates or flux separation.

100. Document batch manufacturing record with all process parameters, raw material lot numbers, in-process test results, and operator signatures.

## Phase 5: Rheological Characterization and Testing (Steps 101-125)

101. Conduct comprehensive rheological profiling using controlled-stress rheometer with cone-and-plate geometry (40mm diameter, 2° cone angle).

102. Measure viscosity versus shear rate from 0.1 to 100 s⁻¹ at 25°C, plotting flow curve to characterize shear-thinning behavior and yield stress.

103. Analyze flow curve data using Herschel-Bulkley model to extract yield stress (τ₀), consistency index (K), and flow behavior index (n) parameters.

104. Verify paste exhibits pseudoplastic behavior with flow behavior index n=0.3-0.5, indicating strong shear-thinning character beneficial for stencil printing.

105. Measure yield stress targeting 100-150 Pa for Type 3 paste, ensuring sufficient resistance to slump while allowing smooth paste release from stencil.

106. Conduct oscillatory shear testing using parallel plate geometry, measuring storage modulus (G') and loss modulus (G") versus frequency from 0.1-10 Hz.

107. Verify paste exhibits predominantly elastic behavior with G' > G" across frequency range, indicating solid-like character that prevents slumping.

108. Calculate loss tangent (tan δ = G"/G') at 1 Hz frequency, targeting value of 0.3-0.5 indicating balanced viscoelastic properties.

109. Perform thixotropic recovery testing by applying high shear (100 s⁻¹) for 60 seconds then measuring viscosity recovery over 300 seconds at rest.

110. Verify paste recovers >80% of initial viscosity within 60 seconds after shear, demonstrating time-dependent structure rebuild necessary for print definition.

111. Test paste response to temperature variation by measuring viscosity at 15°C, 20°C, 25°C, and 30°C, documenting temperature coefficient of viscosity.

112. Verify viscosity increases <15% per 5°C decrease in temperature, ensuring paste maintains printability across typical manufacturing environment variations.

113. Conduct creep-recovery test by applying constant stress equal to 50% of yield stress for 60 seconds then removing stress and measuring strain recovery.

114. Analyze creep compliance curve to extract instantaneous elastic compliance, retarded elastic compliance, and viscous flow components.

115. Verify paste exhibits predominantly elastic recovery (>70% strain recovery) indicating good shape retention after printing.

116. Test paste response to multiple shear cycles by alternating high shear (50 s⁻¹) and low shear (5 s⁻¹) for 10 cycles of 30 seconds each.

117. Verify viscosity remains stable through repeated shear cycles with <10% viscosity change, demonstrating robustness for extended print runs.

118. Measure paste tack evolution over time by testing adhesive force at 0, 1, 5, 15, 30, and 60 minutes after printing using calibrated tack probe.

119. Verify tack remains within specification range (0.3-0.8 N) for at least 30 minutes, ensuring adequate component placement window during SMT assembly.

120. Conduct working life test by storing paste at 25°C with daily viscosity measurements, verifying <15% viscosity increase over 8 hour shift duration.

121. Test paste separation resistance by centrifuging sample at 3000 RPM for 15 minutes and visually inspecting for phase separation or flux bleeding.

122. Verify no visible separation or flux vehicle bleeding occurs, ensuring paste stability under mechanical stress during handling and printing.

123. Measure paste density using gas pycnometer to verify theoretical density calculation, confirming accurate metal loading and no entrapped air voids.

124. Conduct print release testing using laboratory stencil printer with stainless steel stencil, measuring paste transfer efficiency across various aperture sizes.

125. Verify >85% transfer efficiency for apertures with area ratio >0.66, confirming paste exhibits clean release and minimal bridging.

## Phase 6: Stencil Printing Performance Validation (Steps 126-150)

126. Prepare test vehicle PCB with variety of component footprints including 0201 resistors, 0.4mm pitch QFP, 0.5mm pitch BGA, and micro-BGA patterns.

127. Select laser-cut stainless steel stencil with 100mm thickness for Type 3 paste, nano-coated for improved paste release characteristics.

128. Calculate stencil aperture dimensions using 1:1 ratio for Type 3 paste on components >0.5mm pitch, reducing to 0.9:1 ratio for 0.4mm pitch.

129. Design aperture geometry with rounded corners (0.1mm radius) and electropolished walls to minimize paste adhesion and improve release.

130. Calculate area ratio for each aperture (aperture area / aperture wall area), verifying all critical apertures exceed 0.66 minimum threshold.

131. Mount stencil on semi-automatic printer with pneumatic tension system, applying 35 N/cm² tensioning force for optimal flatness.

132. Set up PCB support tooling with height-adjustable pins providing support within 5mm of all paste deposit locations to prevent board flexing.

133. Align stencil to PCB fiducial marks using vision system, achieving <25 micron alignment accuracy in X, Y, and theta axes.

134. Configure print parameters: squeegee pressure 0.5-0.7 kg/cm, print speed 25 mm/s, separation speed 1 mm/s, snap-off distance 0mm (contact printing).

135. Select metal squeegee blades with 60 durometer hardness and 60° attack angle for Type 3 paste printing.

136. Dispense sufficient paste volume onto stencil (approximately 1 cm³ per 10 cm² print area) in continuous bead along squeegee starting position.

137. Execute closed-loop print stroke with squeegee traversing across stencil at constant pressure and speed while monitoring force feedback.

138. Measure squeegee deflection during print stroke using laser displacement sensor, verifying uniform contact and <0.2mm deflection across print length.

139. Separate PCB from stencil using controlled separation speed and acceleration to prevent paste deformation and ensure clean aperture release.

140. Inspect printed deposits using automated optical inspection system with solder paste inspection (SPI) capability and coaxial laser triangulation.

141. Measure paste volume for each deposit location, verifying 100±25% of theoretical volume based on aperture dimensions and stencil thickness.

142. Measure paste height for each deposit, targeting 100±10 microns for Type 3 paste printed through 100 micron thick stencil.

143. Analyze paste deposit shape using SPI area measurement, rejecting deposits with <80% area coverage or irregular shapes indicating print defects.

144. Verify X-Y position accuracy of paste deposits relative to pad centers, accepting <25% pad dimension offset in each axis.

145. Check for bridging between adjacent fine-pitch pads (<0.5mm pitch) by measuring minimum solder mask spacing between deposits.

146. Inspect for insufficient paste (skips) caused by incomplete stencil filling, aperture clogging, or poor paste release from stencil walls.

147. Conduct print repeatability study by printing 10 consecutive boards without stencil cleaning, measuring volume consistency for critical deposits.

148. Calculate process capability index (Cpk) for paste volume and height, targeting Cpk >1.33 for stable manufacturing process.

149. Test stencil life by printing continuous run of 50 boards with paste rolling, measuring paste transfer efficiency degradation over print count.

150. Clean stencil using automated ultrasonic cleaning system with approved cleaning solvent, verifying complete paste removal from apertures and stencil surfaces.

## Phase 7: Component Placement and Tack Properties (Steps 151-165)

151. Prepare component placement testing using automated pick-and-place machine with vision-guided placement capability and force feedback.

152. Program placement machine with component library data including body dimensions, terminal pitch, placement force, and placement height parameters.

153. Load component feeders with variety of package types: 0201/0402 chip resistors, 0.4mm pitch QFP, 0.5mm CSP, and 0.8mm BGA components.

154. Calibrate placement nozzles for each component type, selecting appropriate nozzle size and vacuum pressure for secure component pickup without damage.

155. Set component placement parameters: placement force 50-200g depending on component size, placement speed 0.1 m/s, placement accuracy ±50 microns.

156. Place components onto printed paste deposits at various time intervals (0, 5, 15, 30, 45, 60 minutes) to evaluate tack life performance.

157. Measure component sinking depth into paste using laser profilometer, verifying terminals penetrate 50-75% of paste height for optimal contact.

158. Evaluate component self-centering behavior caused by surface tension forces, measuring final component position accuracy after placement.

159. Verify fine-pitch components (0.4mm QFP) exhibit <25 micron position shift from surface tension self-alignment during placement.

160. Test component retention force by applying calibrated lateral force to placed components and measuring force required to displace component.

161. Verify paste provides adequate retention force (>5g for 0402 chip, >20g for QFP) to prevent component movement during board handling.

162. Conduct drop test by placing components on paste-printed boards and subjecting to 50mm free-fall drop onto hard surface from various orientations.

163. Inspect components after drop test for displacement, verifying <100 micron movement from original placement position.

164. Evaluate paste tack degradation by measuring retention force at 0, 30, 60, 90, and 120 minute intervals after printing.

165. Verify paste maintains >50% of initial tack force for at least 60 minutes, providing adequate working time for typical SMT assembly operations.

## Phase 8: Reflow Profile Development and Optimization (Steps 166-185)

166. Configure reflow oven with 8 heating zones (4 top, 4 bottom) and establish initial temperature profile for SAC305 alloy paste.

167. Set preheat zone temperatures to establish gradual thermal ramp: Zone 1 = 120°C, Zone 2 = 140°C, Zone 3 = 160°C, Zone 4 = 180°C.

168. Program thermal soak zone to maintain 150-180°C temperature range for 60-90 seconds, allowing flux activation and volatile evaporation.

169. Set peak reflow zone temperature to 245-250°C with peak dwell time of 30-60 seconds above SAC305 liquidus temperature (217°C).

170. Configure cooling zones for controlled solidification rate of 2-4°C per second to promote fine-grain microstructure and joint strength.

171. Install thermocouples on test PCB at various thermal mass locations: small component, large component, board center, and board edge.

172. Run initial profile characterization with unloaded test board, recording time-temperature data from all thermocouple locations.

173. Analyze thermal profile compliance with IPC-1752 guidelines: ramp rate <3°C/s, time above liquidus 30-90s, peak temperature 230-250°C.

174. Optimize conveyor speed (typically 40-80 cm/min) to achieve target time above liquidus while maintaining acceptable peak temperature.

175. Adjust individual zone setpoints to compensate for thermal mass variations, ensuring all board locations meet profile requirements simultaneously.

176. Verify flux activation during thermal soak by observing paste color change from gray metallic to dull brown indicating rosin decomposition.

177. Monitor paste wetting behavior during reflow using high-speed camera, observing paste collapse, spreading, and formation of meniscus at component terminals.

178. Measure peak reflow temperature at multiple board locations using thermocouples, verifying uniformity within ±5°C across entire board.

179. Calculate time-temperature integral (°C·seconds above liquidus) for each board location, targeting 1500-3000 °C·s for complete intermetallic formation.

180. Inspect reflowed solder joints using X-ray imaging system, verifying void content <25% for BGA joints and <15% for critical reliability connections.

181. Cross-section representative solder joints and examine intermetallic compound layer thickness using scanning electron microscopy.

182. Measure Cu₆Sn₅ and Cu₃Sn intermetallic layers at copper pad interface, verifying 1-3 micron thickness indicating proper wetting and metallurgical bonding.

183. Evaluate joint fillet formation and wetting angle for surface mount components, accepting 20-40° contact angle for good wetting.

184. Test joint mechanical strength using micro-shear testing apparatus, measuring force required to fracture joints and verifying cohesive failure mode.

185. Optimize profile for minimal voiding by adjusting soak time (longer soak = more outgassing), peak temperature (higher temp = better void escape), and cooling rate.

## Phase 9: Quality Control and Final Testing (Steps 186-195)

186. Conduct comprehensive electrical testing of assembled boards using in-circuit test or flying probe test to verify all solder joints provide electrical continuity.

187. Perform automated optical inspection (AOI) on all reflowed assemblies, detecting defects including bridging, insufficient solder, missing components, and tombstoning.

188. Analyze common defect modes and correlate with paste properties: bridging indicates low yield stress, slumping indicates insufficient thixotropy, graping indicates poor wetting.

189. Execute ionic contamination testing per IPC-TM-650 method 2.3.28 by extracting flux residues and measuring resistivity of extract solution, accepting <1.56 μg NaCl/cm².

190. Subject assemblies to thermal cycling testing (-40°C to +125°C, 500 cycles) and monitor solder joint cracking using dye-and-pry or cross-sectioning analysis.

191. Perform accelerated aging testing at 125°C for 1000 hours and measure joint shear strength degradation, accepting <20% strength reduction.

192. Package qualified solder paste in 500g cartridges or jars with nitrogen atmosphere, sealing to prevent moisture ingress and oxidation during storage.

193. Label paste containers with batch number, alloy type (SAC305), particle size (Type 3), flux classification (no-clean), manufacturing date, and expiration date (6-12 months).

194. Store finished paste in refrigerated conditions (2-10°C) to extend shelf life and maintain rheological stability, warming to room temperature before use.

195. Issue certificate of conformance documenting batch test results including viscosity, metal content, particle size distribution, tack, reflow performance, and compliance with J-STD-005 specifications, releasing product for shipment to electronics assembly customers.
