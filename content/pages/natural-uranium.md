## Geological Survey and Site Selection (Steps 1-15)

1. Conduct airborne radiometric surveys using gamma-ray spectrometers mounted on helicopters to detect uranium anomalies across potential mining areas spanning thousands of square kilometers.

2. Perform ground-based geological mapping to identify rock formations known to host uranium deposits, particularly Athabasca Basin sandstone formations in Canada or sedimentary roll-front deposits in Kazakhstan.

3. Collect rock samples from surface outcrops and conduct handheld scintillometer readings to measure natural radioactivity levels and identify promising exploration targets.

4. Execute drilling programs using diamond core drilling rigs to extract cylindrical rock samples from depths ranging from 100 to 1,200 meters below surface.

5. Log drill cores using portable gamma-ray spectrometers to create continuous radioactivity profiles and identify uranium-bearing zones within the geological column.

6. Split drill cores in half lengthwise, with one half retained for permanent reference and the other half crushed and analyzed for uranium content using laboratory techniques.

7. Conduct chemical assays on pulverized core samples using inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) to determine precise uranium concentrations in parts per million.

8. Perform petrographic analysis using optical microscopy to identify uranium minerals such as uraninite, pitchblende, coffinite, and brannerite within the ore matrix.

9. Generate three-dimensional geological models using specialized mining software to interpolate uranium grades between drill holes and estimate total resource tonnage.

10. Conduct preliminary economic assessments calculating net present value based on estimated ore tonnage, average uranium grade, mining costs, and projected uranium prices.

11. Complete environmental baseline studies documenting pre-mining conditions including water quality, wildlife populations, vegetation communities, and traditional land use patterns.

12. Engage with Indigenous communities, local governments, and regulatory agencies through consultation processes to address concerns and obtain social license to operate.

13. Submit mine development applications to regulatory authorities including detailed plans for mining methods, waste management, water treatment, and eventual site reclamation.

14. Secure mining permits and environmental approvals after demonstrating compliance with radiation protection standards, environmental protection requirements, and safety regulations.

15. Establish mine infrastructure including access roads, electrical power lines, water supply systems, and accommodation facilities for mining personnel in remote locations.

23. Execute controlled blasting sequences using millisecond-delay detonators to fragment ore in progressive waves, minimizing ground vibration and optimizing rock breakage.

24. Operate load-haul-dump (LHD) vehicles with 6-cubic-meter buckets to gather blasted ore from drawpoints, loading the material into 40-tonne haul trucks.

25. Transport ore through underground haulage drifts to shaft loading pockets, where material is dumped into ore passes and gravity-fed to shaft bottom loading stations.

26. Hoist ore to surface in 20-tonne skips making round trips every 3-4 minutes, achieving daily hoisting capacities of 6,000-8,000 tonnes of ore.

27. Dump ore from skips onto surface stockpiles, segregating material by grade categories to enable selective processing based on uranium concentration.

28. Monitor radiation exposure of underground workers using electronic personal dosimeters that continuously measure gamma radiation and accumulated dose in millisieverts.

29. Implement radiation protection protocols limiting worker exposure to 20 millisieverts per year, well below regulatory limits, through job rotation and time-distance-shielding principles.

30. Sample ore during production using systematic channel sampling and assaying to verify that mined material matches predicted grades from resource models.

31. Pump mine water from underground sumps to surface treatment facilities, managing inflows of 100-300 cubic meters per hour depending on hydrogeological conditions.

32. Backfill mined-out stopes with cemented paste fill prepared from processed tailings mixed with 3-5% Portland cement, providing ground support and reducing surface waste.

33. Monitor ground stability using microseismic monitoring arrays that detect acoustic emissions from rock fracturing, providing early warning of potential rockburst conditions.

34. Maintain underground equipment including hydraulic systems, diesel engines, and electrical components in dedicated underground maintenance shops to minimize downtime.

39. Deploy automated ore sorting systems underground using real-time gamma detection to separate waste rock from ore, reducing tonnage hoisted and improving mill feed grade.

## In-Situ Leaching Operations (Steps 56-65)

57. Complete wells with slotted screen sections positioned across uranium-bearing intervals, allowing fluid communication between wellbore and permeable ore-bearing aquifers.

58. Inject lixiviant solution consisting of groundwater fortified with dissolved oxygen and sulfuric acid or sodium bicarbonate, oxidizing and mobilizing uranium from ore minerals.

59. Circulate lixiviant through ore zone at flow rates of 20-50 cubic meters per hour per injection well, maintaining hydraulic containment to prevent solution migration beyond mining area.

60. Recover pregnant leach solution from extraction wells using submersible pumps, collecting uranium-bearing solution containing 50-200 milligrams of uranium per liter.

61. Monitor solution chemistry in field laboratories analyzing pH, oxidation-reduction potential, uranium concentration, and dissolved ions to optimize leaching efficiency.

62. Maintain aquifer hydraulic balance by extracting slightly more fluid than injected, creating inward hydraulic gradients that prevent lixiviant migration into surrounding aquifers.

63. Install monitoring wells in rings surrounding wellfield perimeters, sampling groundwater monthly to verify containment effectiveness and detect any excursions of mining solutions.

82. Rotate SAG mills at 75% of critical speed (approximately 10 RPM), tumbling ore and grinding media in cascading motion that fractures particles through impact and attrition.

84. Discharge ground slurry from SAG mills through grate openings to classify particles, passing minus-12-millimeter material to downstream processing while retaining coarser rocks for further grinding.

85. Pump SAG mill discharge slurry to cyclone classifiers operating at 180 kPa inlet pressure, generating centrifugal forces that separate particles by size and density.

86. Direct cyclone underflow containing coarse particles to ball mills charged with 80 tonnes of forged steel grinding balls ranging from 25 to 75 millimeters diameter.

87. Operate ball mills in closed circuit with cyclones, regrinding underflow material and recycling to classification until target fineness of 80% passing 75 microns is achieved.

88. Monitor grinding circuit performance using particle size analyzers that automatically sample cyclone overflow every 15 minutes, adjusting mill variables to maintain consistent product size.

89. Control mill power draw using variable frequency drives that optimize rotational speed based on acoustic emissions and bearing pressure, maximizing energy efficiency of grinding.

90. Add grinding aids such as sodium silicate at 0.02% concentration to reduce slurry viscosity and prevent clay minerals from coating grinding media surfaces.

91. Maintain grinding media charge in mills using automated ball addition systems that deliver fresh steel balls weekly to replace balls consumed by abrasive wear.

92. Measure slurry density in grinding circuits using nuclear density gauges that emit gamma radiation through slurry-filled pipes, calculating solids concentration from radiation absorption.

93. Adjust fresh water addition to grinding circuits maintaining 50-55% solids in cyclone feed, balancing classification efficiency against pumping requirements and reagent consumption.

## Acid Leaching Process (Steps 96-115)

96. Pump ground slurry to agitated leach tanks measuring 15 meters in diameter by 15 meters tall, providing 4 hours hydraulic retention time for uranium dissolution reactions.

97. Add concentrated sulfuric acid to leach tanks achieving pH 1.5-2.0, creating strongly acidic conditions that dissolve uranium minerals as soluble uranyl sulfate complexes.

98. Inject compressed air through sparger pipes submerged in leach slurry, providing dissolved oxygen that oxidizes uranium from insoluble U(IV) to soluble U(VI) oxidation state.

99. Agitate leach slurry using mechanical impellers rotating at 40 RPM, suspending ore particles and maximizing contact between solid surfaces and acidic oxidizing lixiviant.

100. Maintain leach temperature at 45-50°C using steam injection or electric heating coils, accelerating dissolution kinetics and achieving 95-98% uranium extraction efficiency.

101. Add manganese dioxide or sodium chlorate as oxidizing agents when natural ore contains organic carbon that consumes dissolved oxygen and inhibits uranium oxidation.

102. Monitor leach solution chemistry using automated titrators that measure acid concentration, adjusting sulfuric acid addition to compensate for acid consumed by gangue minerals.

103. Conduct four-stage counter-current leaching with slurry flowing forward through tanks while leach solution flows backward, maximizing uranium recovery and minimizing acid consumption.

104. Remove dissolved silica from leach slurry by adding magnesium oxide forming magnesium silicate precipitates that prevent silica scaling in downstream equipment.

105. Sample leach discharge slurry for residual uranium analysis using X-ray fluorescence spectroscopy, verifying that solid tailings contain less than 0.01% uranium after leaching.

106. Neutralize excess acid in leach discharge by adding limestone (calcium carbonate) slurry, raising pH from 1.8 to 3.5 and reducing downstream neutralization requirements.

107. Separate pregnant leach solution from leached ore solids using thickeners 40 meters in diameter, achieving clarified overflow containing 2-5 grams uranium per liter.

108. Wash leached ore solids in counter-current decantation (CCD) circuits using six sequential thickeners, recovering 98% of dissolved uranium while removing entrained leach solution.

109. Flocculate slurry in thickeners by adding anionic polyacrylamide flocculants at 20 grams per tonne, aggregating fine particles into settleable flocs for enhanced clarification.

110. Monitor thickener performance using turbidity meters on overflow streams, adjusting flocculant dosage to maintain clarity below 100 NTU and minimize uranium losses.

111. Discharge washed tailings from final CCD thickener at 50% solids density, pumping to tailings disposal facilities while clarified solution proceeds to solvent extraction.

112. Control thickener rake torque using variable-speed drives that automatically adjust rotation to prevent overloading when bed density increases due to settling variations.

113. Recycle clarified CCD wash water back to grinding circuits, conserving fresh water and concentrating dissolved uranium by reducing overall water additions to process.

114. Test slurry samples from each leach tank for uranium concentration using portable X-ray fluorescence analyzers, confirming complete dissolution before advancing to solid-liquid separation.

115. Remove dissolved iron from pregnant leach solution by adding limestone and aerating to precipitate ferric hydroxide, preventing iron contamination in subsequent uranium recovery steps.

## Alkaline Leaching Process (Steps 116-125)

116. Pump ground slurry to alkaline leach tanks for processing ores containing high carbonate gangue minerals that would consume excessive acid in conventional acid leaching.

117. Add sodium carbonate and sodium bicarbonate to leach slurry achieving pH 9.5-10.5, creating alkaline conditions where uranium dissolves as soluble uranyl carbonate complexes.

118. Inject compressed air and oxygen through diffuser grids, oxidizing uranium to hexavalent state and providing dissolved oxygen for formation of uranyl tricarbonate complexes.

119. Heat alkaline leach slurry to 80-85°C using direct steam injection, accelerating leaching kinetics and achieving 85-92% uranium extraction efficiency within 24 hours.

120. Add hydrogen peroxide as supplementary oxidant when processing refractory uranium minerals or ores containing high organic carbon concentrations that deplete dissolved oxygen.

121. Operate alkaline leach circuits in series with four to six tanks providing 24-hour total retention time, allowing adequate residence for complete uranium dissolution.

122. Monitor alkaline leach solution chemistry measuring pH, carbonate concentration, and uranium content to optimize reagent additions and maintain stable leaching conditions.

123. Separate pregnant alkaline leach solution from leached solids using thickeners and counter-current decantation circuits similar to acid leaching flowsheets.

124. Acidify pregnant alkaline leach solution to pH 2-3 using sulfuric acid, destroying carbonate complexes and preparing solution for ion exchange or solvent extraction recovery.

125. Regenerate sodium carbonate reagent by treating spent alkaline solutions with lime forming calcium carbonate precipitate, filtering and calcining precipitate to recover carbonate.

## Solvent Extraction (Steps 126-150)

126. Clarify pregnant leach solution through multimedia filters containing anthracite coal and silica sand layers, removing suspended solids to below 50 mg/L to prevent emulsions.

127. Prepare organic extractant by diluting tertiary amine (Alamine 336) to 5% concentration in kerosene diluent, adding isodecanol modifier at 3% to improve phase separation.

128. Contact pregnant leach solution with organic extractant in mixer-settler units, forming uranium-loaded organic phase while raffinate remains as aqueous phase.

129. Pump aqueous and organic phases through centrifugal mixers creating high-shear turbulence that disperses phases into fine droplets, maximizing interfacial area for uranium transfer.

130. Allow mixed phases to separate by gravity in large settling tanks where organic phase rises to surface while heavier aqueous raffinate sinks to bottom.

131. Extract uranium from pregnant leach solution through three counter-current extraction stages, achieving 99% uranium recovery while rejecting dissolved impurities into raffinate.

132. Strip uranium from loaded organic phase using concentrated ammonium sulfate solution at pH 1.0, transferring uranium back to aqueous phase at 10-20 times higher concentration.

133. Operate stripping circuit in four counter-current stages, completely removing uranium from organic phase and regenerating extractant for recycling to extraction stages.

134. Scrub loaded organic phase with dilute sulfuric acid before stripping to remove co-extracted impurities including iron, molybdenum, vanadium, and phosphate.

135. Maintain organic phase inventory by adding fresh extractant to compensate for entrainment losses of 0.1% per cycle, controlling losses through efficient settler design.

136. Monitor phase separation efficiency using interface level controllers that detect organic-aqueous boundary position, adjusting flow rates to maintain stable interface position.

137. Heat strip solution to 50-60°C using shell-and-tube heat exchangers, improving stripping efficiency and reducing organic extractant losses through enhanced phase disengagement.

138. Recycle barren organic extractant continuously from strip circuit back to extraction circuit, maintaining closed-loop solvent circulation at 100-150 cubic meters per hour.

139. Treat raffinate solution from extraction circuit for environmental discharge by neutralizing with lime to pH 7, precipitating dissolved metals, and clarifying in settling ponds.

140. Remove entrained organic from aqueous raffinate using coalescing filters or flotation cells, recovering organic droplets and preventing environmental contamination.

141. Control crud formation at organic-aqueous interfaces by maintaining low suspended solids in feed solution and avoiding excessive mixing intensity that creates stable emulsions.

142. Periodically regenerate organic extractant by contacting with sodium carbonate solution to remove degradation products and accumulated impurities that reduce extraction capacity.

143. Monitor uranium concentration in strip solution using continuous online uranium analyzers based on X-ray fluorescence or laser-induced fluorescence techniques.

148. Prevent bacterial growth in aqueous circuits by maintaining acidic pH and periodically dosing biocides, preventing biofilm formation that can cause equipment fouling.

149. Monitor settler performance using turbidity meters on organic and aqueous exit streams, detecting entrainment problems that indicate need for coalescer maintenance.

150. Heat trace all piping carrying organic extractant during winter operations in cold climates, preventing solution freezing and maintaining fluid viscosity for proper phase separation.

## Ion Exchange (Alternative to Solvent Extraction) (Steps 151-165)

151. Pump clarified pregnant leach solution through fixed-bed ion exchange columns packed with strong-base anion exchange resin selective for uranyl sulfate complexes.

152. Load uranium onto resin columns in downflow mode at 6 bed volumes per hour, allowing uranyl sulfate anions to displace chloride ions from resin functional groups.

153. Monitor uranium breakthrough using online analyzers on column effluent, terminating loading cycle when effluent uranium concentration exceeds 10 mg/L indicating resin saturation.

154. Elute loaded resin columns using 1 molar nitric acid solution pumped upward through columns at 2 bed volumes per hour, displacing adsorbed uranium from resin.

155. Collect uranium eluate containing 5-10 grams uranium per liter in dedicated tanks, achieving 10-20 fold concentration increase compared to pregnant leach solution feed.

156. Regenerate eluted resin by converting from nitrate form back to chloride form using 2 molar sodium chloride solution, preparing resin for next loading cycle.

157. Rinse regenerated resin with deionized water removing excess salt solution, adjusting pH to match pregnant leach solution to prevent premature uranium elution.

158. Operate ion exchange in carousel mode with four to six columns cycling through load-elute-regenerate-rinse sequence, maintaining continuous uranium recovery from steady feed stream.

159. Backwash resin columns periodically using upflow water at high velocity, expanding resin bed to remove trapped fines and redistribute resin particles for uniform flow distribution.

160. Monitor resin condition by measuring resin capacity quarterly through controlled laboratory testing, replacing resin when capacity declines below 80% of initial specification.

161. Control bacterial growth on resin by periodically treating with sodium hydroxide solution at pH 12, sterilizing resin and removing accumulated organic foulants.

162. Adjust pregnant leach solution pH to 1.5-2.0 using sulfuric acid before ion exchange to optimize uranium speciation as anionic uranyl sulfate complexes strongly adsorbed by resin.

163. Remove dissolved silica from pregnant leach solution using magnesium oxide precipitation before ion exchange, preventing silica polymerization and resin fouling.

## Precipitation (Steps 166-180)

166. Transfer uranium-rich strip solution or ion exchange eluate to heated precipitation tanks equipped with mechanical agitators creating turbulent mixing conditions.

167. Slowly add concentrated ammonia solution or gaseous ammonia to precipitation tanks while vigorously agitating, raising pH from 1.5 to 7.0-7.5 over 30-minute period.

168. Precipitate uranium as ammonium diuranate (yellowcake) through neutralization reaction forming bright yellow solid particles suspended in mother liquor.

169. Alternatively add magnesia (magnesium oxide) or hydrogen peroxide to precipitate uranium as magnesium diuranate or uranium peroxide depending on process configuration.

170. Continue ammonia addition and agitation for additional 30 minutes after target pH reached, allowing complete precipitation and crystal growth to enhance filterability.

171. Heat precipitation slurry to 70-80°C using steam injection, promoting formation of larger, more readily filterable yellowcake crystals and increasing settling rates.

172. Settle yellowcake slurry in thickeners allowing gravity separation of dense uranium precipitate from clarified barren solution containing residual dissolved uranium below 5 mg/L.

173. Recycle clarified barren solution from precipitation to leaching circuits, recovering dissolved uranium values and conserving ammonia reagent partially remaining in solution.

174. Thicken yellowcake slurry to 30-40% solids concentration in underflow, reducing water content before filtration to improve filter throughput and reduce drying costs.

175. Pump thickened yellowcake slurry to vacuum drum filters or pressure filters achieving washed filter cake containing 15-20% moisture content.

176. Wash filtered yellowcake with fresh water sprays on filter surface, removing residual dissolved impurities including sulfate, sodium, and chloride salts.

177. Discharge filter cake containing 70-75% uranium oxide (U3O8 basis) from filters into drying feed hoppers, preparing material for final moisture removal.

178. Measure yellowcake mass on filters using integrated weigh scales, tracking uranium inventory and calculating precipitation efficiency from material balance.

179. Monitor precipitate properties including particle size distribution, filterability, and uranium content to optimize precipitation conditions for downstream processing.

180. Maintain precipitation pH control using automated ammonia addition systems with feedback from online pH meters, preventing under-precipitation (low pH) or excessive ammonia consumption (high pH).

184. Collect dried yellowcake from dryer discharge using pneumatic conveying systems that transport powder to storage hoppers using nitrogen or dry air carrier gas.

185. Control dryer exhaust temperature using variable-speed fans that adjust air flow, maintaining optimal heat transfer while preventing yellowcake overheating and uranium compound decomposition.

186. Scrub dryer exhaust gases through wet scrubbers using recirculating water sprays, capturing uranium-bearing dust particles and preventing atmospheric emissions.

187. Alternatively calcine yellowcake in fluidized bed roasters at 600-800°C converting ammonium diuranate to uranium trioxide (UO3) with improved chemical stability.

188. Cool calcined product using indirect air cooling in screw conveyors, reducing temperature from 600°C to 50°C before packaging to prevent container overheating.

189. Pulverize agglomerated dried yellowcake using hammer mills or pin mills, producing free-flowing powder suitable for packaging and transport.

190. Sample dried yellowcake systematically using automatic sampling devices, collecting representative samples for uranium assay, moisture determination, and impurity analysis.
