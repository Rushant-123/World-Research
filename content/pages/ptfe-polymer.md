Polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) is manufactured through controlled polymerization of tetrafluoroethylene monomer, producing a fluoropolymer with exceptional chemical resistance, low dielectric constant, and thermal stability. The process creates granular resin for molding applications and fine powder for paste extrusion, serving RF cable insulation, gaskets, seals, and non-stick coatings.

## TFE Monomer Synthesis

Chloroform undergoes gas-phase pyrolysis at 600-700°C to produce difluorocarbene intermediates. The pyrolysis reactor maintains precise temperature control to prevent decomposition to carbon and hydrogen fluoride. Difluorocarbene reacts with additional chloroform to form chlorodifluoromethane (HCFC-22). The HCFC-22 undergoes catalytic fluorination using antimony pentafluoride catalyst at 300°C. Fluorination converts all chlorine atoms to fluorine, producing tetrafluoroethylene monomer. The TFE gas stream passes through scrubbers removing hydrogen chloride byproducts. Caustic soda solution neutralizes acidic impurities in counter-current scrubbing tower. The purified TFE gas undergoes compression to 3-5 bar for storage. Cryogenic condensation at -40°C removes trace moisture and oxygen. The TFE purity reaches 99.95% minimum for polymerization feed. Storage vessels maintain refrigeration and oxygen exclusion to prevent spontaneous decomposition. Inhibitors like terpenes or limonene suppress free radical formation during storage.

## Emulsion Polymerization System

Deionized water fills stainless steel polymerization reactors with 10,000-20,000 liter capacity. Water quality maintains conductivity below 1 microsiemens/cm to prevent ionic interference. Perfluorooctanoic acid (PFOA) or alternative fluorinated surfactants disperse at 0.05-0.2% concentration. Modern processes use short-chain alternatives to legacy PFOA due to environmental concerns. The surfactant creates micelles with 5-10 nanometer diameter for monomer solubilization. Paraffin wax dispersions act as chain transfer agents controlling molecular weight. Wax concentration adjusts between 0.5-5% depending on desired polymer molecular weight. Disuccinic acid peroxide initiator dissolves in water at 0.01-0.1% concentration. The reactor vessel undergoes evacuation to remove dissolved oxygen completely. Multiple vacuum cycles with nitrogen purging ensure oxygen levels below 5 ppm. TFE monomer feeds continuously maintaining reactor pressure at 10-50 bar. Temperature control systems maintain polymerization at 60-90°C depending on grade.

## Polymerization Reaction Control

Free radical initiation begins when peroxide decomposes at elevated temperature. Disuccinic acid peroxide generates free radicals attacking TFE double bonds. The propagation reaction adds TFE monomers sequentially to growing polymer chains. Each addition creates new radical site for continued chain growth. The perfluorinated radical species exhibit exceptional stability enabling high molecular weights. Polymerization exotherm releases 171 kJ/mol requiring jacket cooling. Circulating chilled water or glycol maintains isothermal conditions within ±2°C. Reactor agitation at 100-300 rpm ensures uniform temperature and surfactant distribution. The particle size gradually increases from initial nucleation to 0.2-0.5 micron diameter. Conversion rates reach 90-95% over 6-12 hour batch cycles. Continuous TFE feed replaces consumed monomer maintaining constant pressure. Chain transfer to paraffin wax limits molecular weight to 2-10 million g/mol range. Without chain transfer agent, molecular weights exceed 20 million g/mol. The suspension remains stable throughout polymerization due to surfactant stabilization. Particle zeta potential maintains strong negative charge preventing coagulation. Surfactant concentration must exceed critical micelle concentration throughout reaction.

## Coagulation and Recovery

Completed polymerization batch undergoes pressure reduction venting unreacted TFE. The TFE recovery system condenses monomer for recycling to subsequent batches. Residual TFE in polymer suspension drops below 0.1% after degassing. Mechanical agitation increases to 500-800 rpm initiating controlled coagulation. Addition of magnesium sulfate or calcium chloride salts destabilizes particle suspension. Salt concentration of 0.5-2% neutralizes surface charges causing particle agglomeration. The PTFE coagulum forms white granular precipitate separating from aqueous phase. Settling time of 30-60 minutes allows complete phase separation. Decanting removes surfactant-containing water for treatment and recycling. Multiple water washes remove residual surfactant and salts from polymer granules. Counter-current washing reduces surfactant content below 50 ppm in final product. Centrifugation at 1000-2000 rpm dewaters coagulum to 60-70% solids content. The wet PTFE granules transfer to drying equipment via pneumatic or screw conveyors.

## Drying Process

Fluid bed dryers suspend PTFE granules in heated air stream. Air temperature of 120-180°C rapidly evaporates water without polymer degradation. PTFE thermal stability allows high temperature drying without melting or decomposition. Residence time of 1-3 hours reduces moisture content below 0.05%. Air velocity maintains particle suspension preventing agglomeration during drying. Cyclone separators recover entrained fine particles returning them to dryer bed. Exhaust air passes through filtration removing any remaining PTFE dust. The dried PTFE resin consists of agglomerated primary particles forming granules. Granule size typically ranges from 0.5-2 millimeters for easy handling. Rotary drum dryers provide alternative continuous drying for high volume production. Drum rotation tumbles granules through heated zone with 2-4 hour residence time. Moisture monitors continuously track drying progress ensuring specification compliance. Over-drying wastes energy while under-drying causes processing problems. The dried granular resin exhibits free-flowing characteristics for automated handling.

Apparent density measured under light compaction reaches 1800-2000 kg/m³. These flow properties determine hopper design and feeding system requirements. Static charge accumulation during handling requires grounding and humidity control.

## Fine Powder Production

Special polymerization conditions produce fine powder grades for paste extrusion. Lower agitation intensity maintains smaller primary particle sizes during polymerization. Reduced surfactant concentration limits particle growth to 0.2-0.3 micron diameter. The fine powder particles never undergo coagulation maintaining colloidal stability. Spray drying from suspension directly produces fine powder product. Atomization nozzles create 50-100 micron droplets containing multiple polymer particles. The fine powder structure easily breaks apart during paste extrusion compounding. Cyclone recovery collects dried powder preventing losses and contamination. ## Sintering Transformation

Sintering converts compacted PTFE powder into fully dense solid polymer. At 327°C PTFE undergoes crystalline transition becoming translucent gel. Above 340°C particles coalesce eliminating interfaces between original granules. Full densification requires temperatures of 360-380°C for 1-6 hours. Time depends on part thickness with thick sections requiring extended holds. Heat transfer limitations control sintering of large parts. The polymer viscosity remains 10¹⁰-10¹² Pa·s even in molten state. This extremely high viscosity prevents conventional melt processing. Void closure proceeds gradually as particles flow together at molecular level. Optical clarity increases as remaining porosity eliminates. Fully sintered PTFE exhibits 95-98% crystallinity with density of 2.14-2.20 g/cm³. The microstructure consists of spherulitic crystals 1-10 microns diameter. Crystal perfection and size influence mechanical and dielectric properties.

## Controlled Cooling Protocol

The final crystalline morphology determines electrical and mechanical performance. Well-crystallized PTFE exhibits superior dielectric stability and lower loss tangent.
