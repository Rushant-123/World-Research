## Fuel Reception and Storage

1. Coal or natural gas arrives at the power plant via rail, pipeline, or barge transport systems.
2. Fuel delivery is scheduled based on generation forecasts and inventory management systems.
3. Coal is unloaded from rail cars using rotary car dumpers or bottom-discharge systems.
4. Natural gas arrives through high-pressure interstate pipeline connections at the plant boundary.
5. Coal conveyor systems transport fuel from unloading areas to storage facilities.
6. Outdoor coal piles are arranged in windrows to minimize spontaneous combustion risk.
7. Bulldozers and front-end loaders manage coal pile inventory and reclaim material.
8. Coal storage capacity typically ranges from 30-60 days of full-load operation.
9. Natural gas is measured at custody transfer points using ultrasonic or turbine meters.
10. Gas pressure is regulated through pressure reduction stations before entering plant systems.
11. Gas chromatographs continuously analyze fuel composition for BTU content and contaminants.
12. Coal samples are collected and tested for moisture content, ash content, and heating value.
13. Fuel quality data is transmitted to the distributed control system for combustion optimization.
14. Coal reclaim systems feed material from storage piles to the plant at controlled rates.
15. Conveyors transport coal to crusher houses where it is sized for pulverizer systems.

## Fuel Preparation and Combustion

16. Coal enters pulverizers (mills) where rotating grinding elements reduce it to powder consistency.
17. Primary air flows through pulverizers to dry the coal and transport pulverized fuel to burners.
18. Coal fineness is controlled to 70-80% passing through a 200-mesh screen for optimal combustion.
19. Pulverized coal and air mixture flows through fuel pipes to burner assemblies in the boiler.
20. Natural gas flows through pressure control valves to gas burner manifolds and nozzles.
21. Forced draft fans supply combustion air to the boiler at pressures exceeding furnace draft.
22. Air preheaters recover heat from flue gas to warm incoming combustion air to 500-600°F.
23. Secondary air registers control air flow patterns around burners for complete combustion.
24. Burners inject fuel and air into the furnace in carefully controlled patterns for flame stability.
25. Ignition systems use oil or gas pilots to establish and maintain stable combustion conditions.
26. Furnace temperatures reach 2,700-3,000°F in the flame zone during normal operation.
27. Water-cooled furnace walls absorb radiant heat energy from the combustion process.
28. Flame scanners and thermocouples monitor combustion conditions continuously.
29. Combustion controls adjust fuel flow, air flow, and burner tilts to optimize efficiency.
30. Excess air levels are maintained at 15-20% to ensure complete combustion while minimizing losses.

## Boiler Water/Steam Circulation

31. Feedwater pumps deliver treated water to the boiler at pressures of 2,000-3,500 psi.
32. Economizer sections in the flue gas path preheat feedwater to near saturation temperature.
33. Feedwater enters the steam drum where it mixes with saturated water from the circulation system.
34. Downcomer tubes carry water from the steam drum to lower headers in the furnace wall.
35. Furnace wall tubes absorb radiant heat energy, causing water to boil and create steam bubbles.
36. The two-phase mixture of water and steam rises through furnace wall tubes by natural circulation.
37. Steam and water separate in the steam drum using cyclone separators and baffles.
38. Saturated steam exits the steam drum through outlet nozzles to superheater sections.
39. Primary superheater sections raise steam temperature above saturation point using convective heat transfer.
40. Attemperator spray systems inject feedwater to control final steam temperature precisely.
41. Secondary and finishing superheater sections elevate steam to final conditions of 1,000-1,050°F.
42. Reheater sections warm steam returning from the high-pressure turbine back to 1,000°F.
43. Steam drum level controls maintain optimal water inventory in the boiler circulation system.
44. Blowdown systems remove concentrated solids from the steam drum to control water chemistry.
45. Chemical feed systems inject phosphates, amines, and oxygen scavengers for corrosion control.

## Steam Turbine Operation

46. Main steam lines deliver high-pressure steam from the boiler to the turbine stop valves.
47. Turbine stop valves and control valves regulate steam admission to the high-pressure turbine.
48. High-pressure steam enters the turbine at approximately 2,400 psi and 1,000°F.
49. Steam expands through high-pressure turbine stages, rotating blades mounted on the rotor shaft.
50. Stationary nozzle diaphragms between stages convert pressure energy to velocity energy.
51. Rotating blades extract energy from high-velocity steam jets, producing mechanical work.
52. Steam exits the high-pressure turbine at approximately 500 psi and 600°F.
53. Cold reheat lines return partially expanded steam to the boiler reheater sections.
54. Reheated steam at 1,000°F flows to intermediate-pressure turbine inlet valves.
55. Intermediate-pressure turbine stages further expand steam to approximately 50-100 psi.
56. Extraction points along the turbine provide steam for feedwater heating systems.
57. Steam flows from the intermediate turbine to the low-pressure turbine crossover piping.
58. Low-pressure turbine stages expand steam to near-vacuum conditions of 1-4 inches Hg absolute.
59. Final turbine stages feature long blades to accommodate the high specific volume of low-pressure steam.
60. Exhaust steam leaves the low-pressure turbine and enters the condenser housing.

## Generator Operation and Excitation

61. The turbine rotor connects to the generator rotor through a rigid coupling assembly.
62. The generator rotor spins at precisely 3,600 rpm for 60 Hz systems (two-pole design).
63. Excitation systems supply DC current to the generator rotor field windings through slip rings.
64. Rotating magnetic fields in the rotor induce alternating current in the stator windings.
65. Generator output voltage is typically 18,000-24,000 volts at the stator terminals.
66. Three-phase current flows from the stator windings to isolated phase bus systems.
67. Generator neutral grounding transformers and resistors provide ground fault protection.
68. Voltage regulators automatically adjust field excitation to maintain constant terminal voltage.
69. Power factor controllers optimize reactive power production for grid voltage support.
70. Generator protection relays monitor electrical parameters and trip the unit for fault conditions.
71. Hydrogen or air cooling systems remove heat from generator stator and rotor components.
72. Stator water cooling systems circulate deionized water through hollow conductor strands.
73. Generator output power typically ranges from 300 to 1,300 megawatts for large units.
74. Isolated phase bus ducts carry high-amperage current from the generator to transformers.
75. Bus duct cooling systems remove resistive heating losses from enclosed conductors.

## Condenser and Cooling Systems

76. Exhaust steam from the low-pressure turbine condenses on the outside of condenser tubes.
77. Cooling water flows through thousands of tubes in the condenser shell, absorbing heat.
78. The condensation process creates vacuum conditions that increase turbine thermal efficiency.
79. Circulating water pumps move large volumes (300,000-600,000 gpm) through the condenser.
80. Intake structures draw cooling water from rivers, lakes, or cooling towers.
81. Traveling screens remove debris and aquatic organisms from the cooling water intake.
82. Condenser vacuum pumps remove non-condensable gases to maintain optimal vacuum levels.
83. Condensate pumps extract condensed water from the condenser hotwell at 80-100°F.
84. Condensate flows through polishing demineralizers to remove impurities and maintain water quality.

89. Cooling towers reject waste heat to the atmosphere through evaporative cooling processes.
90. Makeup water systems replace cooling water lost to evaporation, drift, and blowdown.

## Flue Gas Treatment and Emissions Control

91. Flue gas exits the boiler furnace carrying combustion products, fly ash, and heat energy.
92. Selective catalytic reduction (SCR) systems inject ammonia to reduce nitrogen oxide emissions.
93. SCR catalyst beds convert NOx and ammonia to nitrogen gas and water vapor at 650-750°F.
94. Air preheaters cool flue gas while heating incoming combustion air, improving efficiency.
95. Electrostatic precipitators (ESPs) apply high voltage to charge and collect fly ash particles.
96. ESP collection efficiency typically exceeds 99.5% for particulate matter removal.
97. Rapper systems periodically shake collecting plates to dislodge accumulated fly ash.
98. Ash hoppers below ESPs collect fly ash for pneumatic or mechanical transport systems.
99. Flue gas desulfurization (FGD) systems spray limestone slurry to remove sulfur dioxide.
100. Absorber towers provide contact time for SO2 to react with alkaline limestone slurry.
101. Gypsum forms as a byproduct of the FGD reaction and is dewatered for beneficial use.
102. FGD systems achieve 95-98% SO2 removal efficiency in modern power plants.
103. Mist eliminators remove entrained water droplets from clean flue gas leaving the absorber.
104. Induced draft fans overcome system pressure drop and exhaust flue gas to the stack.
105. Stack gas continuously monitored for opacity, SO2, NOx, CO2, and other emissions parameters.
