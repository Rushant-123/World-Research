# Heat Treatment Process for Mac Metal Components

**Market Leader:** [Bodycote](/companies/bodycote)



## Pre-Treatment Preparation

1. Receive incoming metal components from machining operations
2. Inspect components for dimensional accuracy and surface condition
3. Clean components using alkaline degreasing solution
4. Remove any cutting fluids, oils, or contaminants from surfaces
5. Perform ultrasonic cleaning for complex geometries
6. Rinse components with deionized water
7. Dry components completely using forced air or ovens
8. Identify material grade and specification requirements
9. Review customer heat treatment specifications and standards
10. Select appropriate heat treatment cycle based on material type
11. Verify component traceability and batch documentation
12. Check furnace availability and schedule production slot
13. Prepare heat treatment work order and process routing
14. Select appropriate fixturing and racking for components
15. Arrange components to ensure uniform heating during treatment

## Furnace Preparation and Setup

16. Inspect heat treatment furnace for cleanliness and condition
17. Check furnace refractory lining for cracks or damage
18. Verify furnace door seals and gasket integrity
19. Calibrate temperature control system and thermocouples
20. Test atmosphere gas delivery system and flow rates
21. Check cooling system functionality for quench operations
22. Verify vacuum pump operation for vacuum furnace applications
23. Load pyrometric cones or test pieces for temperature verification
24. Program temperature controller with heat treatment cycle parameters
25. Set heating rate, soak temperature, and hold time
26. Configure atmosphere gas composition and flow rates
27. Install carbon potential sensors for carburizing operations
28. Connect data logging system for process documentation
29. Perform furnace leak test for vacuum or atmosphere-controlled operations
30. Preheat furnace to loading temperature if required

## Annealing Process - Full Annealing

31. Load components into furnace at temperatures below 600°C
32. Close furnace door and verify seal integrity
33. Begin heating cycle at controlled rate of 50-100°C per hour
34. Monitor temperature uniformity across furnace chamber
35. Heat components to austenitizing temperature (typically 850-950°C for steel)
36. Hold at austenitizing temperature to ensure complete transformation
37. Soak for 1-2 hours depending on component section thickness
38. Allow sufficient time for carbide dissolution and grain refinement
39. Monitor atmosphere composition to prevent oxidation or decarburization
40. Begin slow controlled cooling at rate of 10-50°C per hour
41. Cool components through transformation range slowly
42. Continue furnace cooling to below 300°C
43. Maintain protective atmosphere throughout cooling cycle
44. Allow components to reach ambient temperature in furnace
45. Document time-temperature profile for quality records

## Annealing Process - Stress Relieving

46. Load machined components requiring stress relief
47. Heat components to stress relief temperature (550-650°C for steel)
48. Use heating rate of 100-150°C per hour for stress relief
49. Hold at temperature for 1 hour per 25mm of section thickness
50. Ensure uniform temperature distribution throughout load
51. Monitor for any dimensional changes during heating
52. Maintain temperature within ±10°C of target temperature
53. Cool components slowly in still air or furnace
54. Prevent rapid cooling that could induce new stresses
55. Remove components when cooled to safe handling temperature

## Normalizing Process

56. Load components for normalizing treatment
57. Heat to austenitizing temperature above upper critical point
58. Hold at normalizing temperature (900-950°C for carbon steel)
59. Soak for sufficient time to achieve uniform austenite formation
60. Remove components from furnace for air cooling
61. Cool components in still air to room temperature
62. Ensure adequate spacing for uniform air circulation
63. Monitor cooling rate to achieve desired microstructure
64. Document actual cooling rate for process verification
65. Inspect normalized components for scale formation

## Hardening Process - Austenitizing

66. Select appropriate hardening temperature based on steel grade
67. Load components into hardening furnace
68. Establish protective atmosphere to prevent scaling
69. Use endothermic gas, nitrogen, or vacuum atmosphere
70. Heat components to austenitizing temperature (800-900°C typical)
71. Use heating rate appropriate for component geometry
72. Monitor carbon potential for surface carbon control
73. Hold at austenitizing temperature for complete dissolution
74. Soak time calculated based on section thickness (1 hour per inch typical)
75. Verify temperature uniformity using multiple thermocouples
76. Ensure all components reach target temperature
77. Monitor for any hot spots or cold zones in furnace
78. Check atmosphere dew point and carbon potential readings
79. Document austenitizing time and temperature profile
80. Prepare quench medium while components are soaking

## Quenching Process - Water Quenching

81. Verify water quench tank temperature (typically 15-25°C)
82. Check water circulation and agitation system operation
83. Ensure adequate water volume for component load
84. Test water flow rate and distribution
85. Position components for rapid immersion into quenchant
86. Transfer components from furnace to quench tank quickly
87. Minimize transfer time to prevent air cooling (under 5 seconds ideal)
88. Immerse components completely and rapidly
89. Maintain vigorous agitation during quenching
90. Continue quenching until components reach quench temperature
91. Monitor for vapor blanket formation and ensure film boiling stage
92. Typical quench duration 30-60 seconds for through hardening
93. Remove components when temperature stabilizes
94. Inspect for quench cracks or distortion immediately
95. Measure quench hardness on test specimens

## Quenching Process - Oil Quenching

96. Verify oil quench tank temperature (typically 50-80°C)
97. Check oil condition and contamination level
98. Test oil viscosity and cooling characteristics
99. Ensure oil agitation system is operational
100. Preheat oil to operating temperature if cold
101. Transfer austenitized components to oil quench tank
102. Minimize transfer time to maintain austenitizing temperature
103. Immerse components with appropriate orientation
104. Maintain moderate agitation to ensure uniform cooling
105. Continue quenching for 2-5 minutes typical duration
106. Monitor oil temperature rise during quenching
107. Allow components to reach oil temperature
108. Remove components and drain excess oil
109. Clean oil residue from component surfaces
110. Inspect for soft spots or inadequate hardening

## Quenching Process - Polymer Quenching

111. Prepare polymer quenchant solution at specified concentration
112. Verify polymer concentration using refractometer
113. Check solution temperature (typically 30-50°C)
114. Test quench severity and cooling rate characteristics
115. Ensure adequate agitation for uniform cooling
116. Transfer components from furnace to polymer quenchant
117. Immerse components with controlled agitation
118. Monitor cooling rate through transformation range
119. Adjust agitation to control cooling rate and minimize distortion
120. Continue quenching until components reach quenchant temperature
121. Remove components when fully quenched
122. Rinse components with water to remove polymer residue
123. Dry components thoroughly before tempering
124. Inspect hardness distribution across component
125. Document quench parameters for process control

## Tempering Process

126. Load hardened components into tempering furnace immediately
127. Minimize time between quenching and tempering (under 1 hour)
128. Set tempering temperature based on required final hardness
129. Typical tempering range 150-650°C depending on application
130. Heat components at controlled rate to tempering temperature
131. Use air circulation for uniform temperature distribution
132. Hold at tempering temperature for minimum 1 hour
133. Typical tempering time 2 hours for most applications
134. Perform double tempering for critical applications
135. Monitor temperature accuracy within ±5°C
136. Maintain uniform temperature throughout furnace load
137. Allow martensitic transformation and stress relief
138. Cool components in still air after tempering
139. Perform second tempering cycle if specified
140. Document tempering time-temperature parameters

## Case Hardening - Carburizing Process

141. Load components into carburizing furnace
142. Establish carburizing atmosphere (endothermic gas with enrichment)
143. Heat components to carburizing temperature (900-950°C)
144. Introduce hydrocarbon enrichment gas (propane or methane)
145. Control carbon potential to achieve desired case depth
146. Monitor carbon potential using oxygen probe or shim stock
147. Maintain carbon potential between 0.8-1.0% typically
148. Calculate carburizing time based on required case depth
149. Typical rate of 0.1mm per hour case depth production
150. Hold at carburizing temperature for specified duration
151. Diffuse carbon into surface layer of components
152. Perform diffusion cycle to optimize case carbon gradient
153. Reduce carbon potential during diffusion cycle
154. Cool components in furnace or proceed directly to hardening
155. Document carbon potential profile during carburizing

## Case Hardening - Carbonitriding Process

156. Set up carbonitriding furnace with nitrogen addition capability
157. Heat components to carbonitriding temperature (800-900°C)
158. Introduce both carbon and nitrogen-bearing gases
159. Add ammonia gas to atmosphere for nitrogen diffusion
160. Control atmosphere composition for simultaneous diffusion
161. Maintain process for shorter time than pure carburizing
162. Achieve shallow case depth with improved hardenability
163. Monitor nitrogen potential and carbon potential
164. Cool and quench directly from carbonitriding temperature
165. Achieve case depth of 0.1-0.5mm typically

## Induction Hardening Process

166. Set up induction heating equipment with appropriate coil design
167. Select coil geometry matching component surface to be hardened
168. Program induction power and frequency parameters
169. Position component in induction coil with proper clearance
170. Apply induction heating power to austenitize surface layer
171. Heat surface to austenitizing temperature rapidly (1-5 seconds)
172. Control heating depth using frequency selection (1-10kHz typical)
173. Monitor surface temperature using pyrometer
174. Immediately quench heated area using integral spray quench
175. Apply water or polymer quenchant through spray nozzles
176. Achieve rapid cooling to form martensitic case
177. Move to next section for progressive hardening if required
178. Temper inductively hardened areas if specified
179. Inspect case depth using destructive or non-destructive methods
180. Verify hardness pattern across hardened zone

## Post-Treatment Operations

181. Remove components from tempering furnace and cool to ambient
182. Perform visual inspection for cracks, distortion, or defects
183. Clean components to remove any scale or residue
184. Shot blast or bead blast to remove surface oxides if required
185. Perform dimensional inspection to verify specifications
186. Check for distortion and measure critical dimensions
187. Conduct hardness testing using Rockwell or Vickers methods
188. Test surface hardness and core hardness as specified
189. Perform case depth measurement using microhardness traverse
190. Conduct metallurgical examination of microstructure if required
191. Prepare metallographic samples by sectioning and mounting
192. Polish and etch samples to reveal microstructure
193. Examine under optical microscope for phase identification
194. Document hardness results and metallurgical findings
195. Issue certificate of conformance and heat treatment report
