# Manufacturing Process: Gas Turbine Blades

## Raw Material Preparation (Steps 1-15)

1. Receive high-purity nickel superalloy ingots (Inconel 738, René 80, CMSX-4) from specialty metal suppliers
2. Verify chemical composition using optical emission spectroscopy for all alloying elements (Ni, Cr, Co, Al, Ti, Ta, W)
3. Check material certifications and trace batch numbers to original melting facility
4. Inspect ingots for surface defects, porosity, or segregation using visual and ultrasonic testing
5. Store superalloy materials in climate-controlled warehouse at 20°C ±2°C with <50% humidity
6. Calculate required material quantities based on blade geometry, gating system, and yield expectations
7. Cut ingots into appropriate charging sizes using precision band saw or abrasive cutting
8. Clean cut surfaces with acetone and isopropyl alcohol to remove cutting fluids
9. Weigh each charge precisely to ±0.1g to ensure correct furnace loading
10. Prepare master alloy additions for fine-tuning composition (Al, Ti, Ta boosters)
11. Document traceability from ingot heat number through to specific blade serial numbers
12. Prepare ceramic shell materials: fused silica, zircon, alumina in various mesh sizes
13. Mix colloidal silica binders with pH adjustment to 9.5-10.5 for optimal shell strength
14. Prepare stucco materials in graded particle sizes (30-50 mesh, 50-100 mesh, 100-200 mesh)
15. Condition all shell materials in humidity-controlled environment for 24 hours before use

## Wax Pattern Creation (Steps 16-30)

16. Inject wax blend (60% paraffin, 30% microcrystalline, 10% additives) into precision aluminum molds
17. Control wax injection temperature at 65-70°C and pressure at 50-100 psi
18. Maintain mold temperature at 35-40°C for optimal pattern reproduction
19. Allow wax pattern to solidify for 3-5 minutes before ejection
20. Carefully extract wax pattern using automated ejector pins to avoid distortion
21. Inspect wax pattern dimensions using coordinate measuring machine (CMM) with ±0.025mm accuracy
22. Check critical features: airfoil profile, root attachment, platform geometry, cooling holes
23. Verify wax pattern weight against target specification (typically 150-200g per blade)
24. Assemble wax patterns onto ceramic runner system with 6-8 blades per cluster
25. Attach wax pour cup at top of cluster and wax gates at base of each blade
26. Weld wax connections using heated spatula at 80-90°C to ensure strong joints
27. Add wax grain selector and starter crystal well at base of each blade for single crystal growth
28. Inspect assembled wax cluster for proper gate angles (45-60° preferred)
29. Clean wax cluster with soft brushes to remove particulates
30. Store completed wax assemblies in temperature-controlled room at 20°C until shell building

## Ceramic Shell Building (Steps 31-50)

31. Preheat wax cluster to 35°C to prevent thermal shock during first slurry application
32. Prepare prime coat slurry: colloidal silica with 200-325 mesh zircon flour (45-50% solids)
33. Add wetting agents (0.1-0.2% non-ionic surfactant) to ensure complete pattern coverage
34. Degas prime slurry under vacuum for 15 minutes to remove trapped air bubbles
35. Measure slurry viscosity using Zahn cup (target: 20-25 seconds)
36. Dip wax cluster into prime slurry ensuring complete immersion for 30-45 seconds
37. Withdraw cluster slowly (5-10 cm/minute) to ensure uniform coating thickness
38. Allow excess slurry to drain for 2-3 minutes in vertical orientation
39. Apply fine stucco (200-325 mesh zircon) in rainfall stucco booth for 60 seconds
40. Rotate cluster during stuccoing to ensure uniform coverage of all surfaces
41. Transfer shell to drying chamber at 20°C, 40% RH for 4 hours minimum
42. Apply second coat using intermediate slurry (100-200 mesh alumina flour)
43. Stucco with medium grade material (100-150 mesh) and dry for 4 hours
44. Apply backup coats (typically 5-7 additional coats) using coarse slurry and stucco
45. Increase shell thickness to 8-12mm for structural integrity during casting
46. Final coat uses coarse stucco (16-30 mesh) for maximum strength
47. Dry final shell for minimum 8 hours before dewaxing
48. Inspect shell for coating uniformity, no bridging between blades, complete coverage
49. Measure shell thickness using ultrasonic gauge at critical locations
50. Mark each shell with unique identification number for traceability

## Dewaxing and Shell Firing (Steps 51-65)

51. Load ceramic shells into dewax autoclave at room temperature
52. Seal autoclave and introduce steam at 0.3-0.5 MPa pressure
53. Heat autoclave to 120-140°C over 30-minute ramp period
54. Hold at dewax temperature for 60 minutes to completely melt out wax pattern
55. Collect melted wax in recovery system for recycling (typically 85-90% recovery)
56. Purge autoclave with additional steam to remove residual wax from mold cavity
57. Cool autoclave slowly to 80°C before opening to prevent thermal shock
58. Remove dewaxed shells and inspect for cracks or damage
59. Check that all wax has been removed using visual inspection and weight measurement
60. Load shells into high-temperature firing furnace with careful spacing
61. Heat shells at controlled rate of 50-100°C/hour to avoid thermal stress
62. Fire shells at 900-1100°C for 2-4 hours to achieve ceramic bonding
63. Hold at firing temperature to burn out all organic binders completely
64. Cool shells slowly at 50°C/hour to room temperature over 12-16 hours
65. Inspect fired shells for structural integrity, cracks, or spalling using dye penetrant testing

## Furnace Preparation (Steps 66-80)

66. Clean investment casting furnace chamber thoroughly to remove residual contaminants
67. Inspect furnace heating elements and replace any damaged coils
68. Verify furnace vacuum system achieves <10^-4 torr operating pressure
69. Calibrate thermocouples using reference standards traceable to NIST
70. Install new graphite susceptor and insulation package for single crystal casting
71. Position water-cooled copper chill plate at base of furnace
72. Set chill plate temperature to 10-15°C using recirculating chiller
73. Install ceramic radiation baffle above mold to control cooling rate
74. Adjust baffle height to achieve directional solidification gradient of 50-100°C/cm
75. Load pre-alloyed superalloy charges into ceramic crucible (typically 15-25 kg per heat)
76. Add grain refiner pellets or master alloy adjustments as needed
77. Preheat fired ceramic shells to 1450-1550°C in separate preheating furnace
78. Maintain shell preheat temperature ±10°C to ensure proper metal flow
79. Evacuate casting furnace to operating vacuum level (<10^-3 torr)
80. Backfill furnace with high-purity argon to 200-400 torr for casting

## Metal Melting and Pouring (Steps 81-95)

81. Initiate induction heating of superalloy charge at controlled power level
82. Monitor melt temperature using two-color optical pyrometer
83. Heat superalloy to 1500-1540°C (approximately 50-70°C above liquidus)
84. Hold at temperature for 10-15 minutes to ensure complete melting and homogenization
85. Add final alloying additions if required for composition adjustment
86. Degas molten metal under vacuum to remove dissolved hydrogen and nitrogen
87. Take temperature reading immediately before pour (target: 1520°C ±10°C)
88. Transfer preheated ceramic shells from preheat furnace to casting chamber
89. Position shells on water-cooled chill plate with grain selector properly oriented
90. Tilt furnace to begin controlled gravity pour into ceramic shells
91. Pour at rate of 1-2 kg/second to avoid turbulence and air entrapment
92. Fill all shell cavities completely, ensuring pour cup is filled to top
93. Monitor fill through observation port using dark filter protection
94. Initiate controlled withdrawal of casting platform into chill zone immediately after pouring
95. Withdraw at rate of 3-6 mm/minute to establish directional solidification

## Single Crystal Growth (Steps 96-110)

96. Maintain precise thermal gradient across solidification interface (80-100°C/cm)
97. Control withdrawal rate to achieve solidification velocity of 5-8 mm/minute
98. Monitor grain selector region where columnar grains compete for growth
99. Allow single grain to emerge from selector spiral geometry through geometric restriction
100. Continue controlled solidification up through blade root section
101. Maintain steady-state conditions as crystal grows through airfoil region
102. Adjust power input to heating elements to compensate for changing cross-section
103. Monitor thermocouple array to ensure isotherms remain parallel to withdrawal direction
104. Solidify through blade airfoil maintaining single crystal orientation <10° from <001>
105. Continue through platform region with careful thermal control
106. Complete solidification of pour cup and gating system
107. Hold casting at elevated temperature (1200-1300°C) for 30-60 minutes for homogenization
108. Begin controlled cooling at 50-100°C/hour to avoid thermal shock
109. Cool to <200°C before removing from furnace (typically 8-12 hour cooling cycle)
110. Verify furnace log data shows stable processing conditions throughout casting cycle

## Shell Removal and Initial Cleaning (Steps 111-125)

111. Remove casting cluster from furnace and allow to equilibrate to room temperature
112. Perform initial visual inspection for gross defects, incomplete fill, or surface irregularities
113. Cut off pour cup and gating system using abrasive wheel or bandsaw
114. Mark each blade with heat number and sequential casting identification
115. Remove ceramic shell using high-pressure water jet blasting (1500-2000 psi)
116. Direct water jet into blade cooling passages to clear ceramic core material
117. Continue water blasting until all external shell and internal core material is removed
118. Use chemical leaching (hot caustic solution, 10% NaOH at 80-90°C) for stubborn core remnants
119. Soak castings for 4-8 hours with periodic ultrasonic agitation
120. Rinse thoroughly with deionized water to remove all chemical residues
121. Dry blades in warm air oven at 80-100°C for 2 hours
122. Perform post-shell removal inspection for casting defects: porosity, cracks, inclusions
123. Use dye penetrant inspection on external surfaces to detect surface-breaking defects
124. Check critical dimensions using optical comparator or CMM
125. Separate blades into quality grades based on initial inspection results

## Heat Treatment - Solution Treatment (Steps 126-140)

126. Load castings into vacuum heat treatment furnace with proper spacing
127. Evacuate furnace to <10^-4 torr to prevent oxidation during heating
128. Heat at controlled rate of 100-150°C/hour to 1280-1320°C (solution temperature)
129. Monitor temperature uniformity across furnace load (±5°C maximum variation)
130. Hold at solution temperature for 2-4 hours to dissolve gamma-prime precipitates
131. Achieve complete solutioning of alloying elements into gamma matrix
132. Rapid cool (gas fan quench) at 150-200°C/minute to room temperature
133. Backfill furnace with high-purity argon during cooling for uniform heat transfer
134. Monitor cooling rate at blade mid-section using embedded thermocouples
135. Complete cooling to <100°C before opening furnace door
136. Remove blades and inspect for heat treatment distortion or cracking
137. Verify solution treatment creates supersaturated solid solution for subsequent aging
138. Document time-temperature profile for each heat treatment batch
139. Verify furnace calibration meets AMS2750 pyrometry requirements
140. Store solution-treated blades in inert atmosphere pending aging treatment

## Heat Treatment - Precipitation Aging (Steps 141-155)

141. Reload solution-treated blades into aging furnace for two-stage precipitation treatment
142. Heat to first aging temperature (1080-1120°C) at controlled rate
143. Hold for 2-4 hours to precipitate primary gamma-prime phase (Ni3Al, Ni3Ti)
144. Control cooling rate to second aging temperature (850-900°C)
145. Hold at secondary aging temperature for 16-24 hours
146. Precipitate fine secondary gamma-prime for optimal creep resistance
147. Achieve target microstructure: 60-70 vol% gamma-prime with bimodal distribution
148. Cool slowly to room temperature at 25-50°C/hour
149. Final microstructure: large primary precipitates (0.3-0.5 μm) plus fine secondary (50-100 nm)
150. Remove aged blades and verify hardness (typically 400-450 HV)
151. Perform metallographic sampling from run-out tabs for microstructure verification
152. Measure gamma-prime size distribution using scanning electron microscopy
153. Verify precipitation treatment achieved target strength properties
154. Confirm aging produces optimal balance of creep strength and fatigue resistance
155. Document complete heat treatment cycle for material certification

## Precision Machining (Steps 156-170)

156. Mount blade in precision 5-axis CNC machining center with custom fixturing
157. Establish coordinate system using blade root datum surfaces
158. Rough machine root attachment (fir-tree or dovetail profile) using carbide end mills
159. Apply cutting fluid (water-soluble synthetic) at 20 liters/minute flow rate
160. Machine at conservative speeds (50-80 SFM) to avoid work hardening nickel superalloy
161. Finish machine root profile to final dimensions ±0.025mm tolerance
162. Machine platform sealing faces and contact surfaces to Ra 0.8 μm finish
163. Create tip cap attachment features or squealer tip geometry as designed
164. Use electrochemical machining (ECM) to drill film cooling holes in airfoil
165. Position electrode precisely using CNC control, drill holes at 30-50 holes/minute
166. Achieve cooling hole diameters of 0.5-1.0mm with length-to-diameter ratios up to 20:1
167. Create shaped cooling holes (diffuser exits) using specialized EDM electrodes
168. Drill holes at specified compound angles (20-45° to surface normal)
169. Verify cooling hole positions using optical CMM or CT scanning
170. Deburr all machined surfaces using abrasive flow machining or manual methods

## Surface Preparation for Coating (Steps 171-180)

171. Clean machined blades using alkaline cleaning solution to remove machining oils
172. Rinse with deionized water and air dry at 80°C
173. Grit blast airfoil surfaces using aluminum oxide (120-220 grit) at 60-80 psi
174. Achieve surface roughness of Ra 3-6 μm for optimal coating adhesion
175. Mask root attachment and platform sealing surfaces using high-temperature tape
176. Protect cooling hole inlets from coating blockage using precision-fitted plugs
177. Clean grit-blasted surfaces with solvent wipe (isopropyl alcohol)
178. Perform final cleaning in ultrasonic vapor degreaser using Vertrel MCA
179. Dry blades in vacuum oven at 100°C for 1 hour to remove all moisture
180. Transfer immediately to coating chamber to prevent oxidation or contamination

## Bond Coat Application (Steps 181-190)

181. Load blades into electron beam physical vapor deposition (EB-PVD) chamber
182. Position blades on rotating fixture for uniform coating deposition
183. Evacuate chamber to <10^-5 torr base pressure
184. Preheat blades to 950-1000°C using radiant heaters
185. Evaporate MCrAlY bond coat material (M = Ni, Co, or NiCo) using electron beam
186. Control deposition rate at 3-5 μm/minute for dense, adherent coating
187. Rotate blades at 3-5 RPM for circumferential uniformity
188. Deposit bond coat to thickness of 75-150 μm
189. Monitor coating thickness in-situ using quartz crystal microbalance
190. Cool blades slowly in vacuum to prevent thermal stress in coating

## Thermal Barrier Coating (Steps 191-195)

191. Apply yttria-stabilized zirconia (7-8 wt% Y2O3-ZrO2) top coat using EB-PVD or air plasma spray
192. For EB-PVD: deposit columnar microstructure at 2-4 μm/minute to 125-250 μm thickness
193. Create strain-tolerant coating architecture with vertical cracks and porosity (15-20% porosity)
194. For APS: spray YSZ using controlled parameters (40 kW power, 40 SLPM argon, 12 SLPM hydrogen)
195. Achieve lamellar splat microstructure with horizontal cracks for strain tolerance and thermal insulation (thermal conductivity 0.8-1.5 W/m·K, enabling 150-200°C temperature drop across coating)
