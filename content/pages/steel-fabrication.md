# Structural Steel Fabrication

**Top Market Leaders:**
- [Nucor](/companies/nucor)
- [AISC Members](/companies/aisc-members)



Structural steel fabrication transforms raw steel materials into precision-engineered components for buildings, bridges, and infrastructure through cutting, forming, welding, and finishing operations.

## Steel Receiving and Inspection

1. **Material delivery scheduling**: Coordinate with steel mill for delivery of structural steel shapes including beams, channels, angles, plates, and tubes to fabrication facility.

2. **Receiving dock unloading**: Use overhead crane systems rated for 20-50 ton capacity to safely offload steel bundles from flatbed trucks onto receiving area.

3. **Material identification verification**: Check heat numbers, grade markings, and mill test reports against purchase orders to ensure correct steel specifications were delivered.

4. **Visual inspection**: Examine steel surfaces for surface defects, excessive rust, warping, or damage that occurred during transportation or handling.

5. **Dimensional verification**: Measure cross-sectional dimensions, lengths, and weights of sample pieces to confirm they meet ordered specifications and ASTM standards.

6. **Chemical composition verification**: Review mill certificates showing carbon content, manganese, silicon, and alloying elements meet specified grade requirements.

7. **Mechanical property verification**: Check mill test reports confirm yield strength, tensile strength, and elongation values meet project specifications.

8. **Material traceability tagging**: Attach identification tags to each steel piece linking it to specific heat numbers and test reports for complete traceability.

9. **Storage area organization**: Sort and stack steel by type, size, and grade in designated outdoor or covered storage yards with proper dunnage for air circulation.

10. **Inventory database entry**: Log all received material into fabrication shop management system with quantities, locations, heat numbers, and project allocations.

## Engineering and Planning

11. **Shop drawing review**: Examine detailed fabrication drawings showing all dimensions, hole locations, weld symbols, and finish requirements for each steel component.

12. **Material optimization planning**: Use nesting software to optimize cutting patterns and minimize steel waste when cutting multiple parts from stock material.

13. **Fabrication sequence planning**: Determine optimal order of operations including cutting, drilling, fitting, welding, and finishing to maximize efficiency and quality.

14. **Tooling requirements identification**: Identify all cutting tools, drill bits, welding electrodes, and fixtures needed for each fabrication operation.

15. **Quality control point designation**: Mark critical inspection hold points where dimensional checks or non-destructive testing must occur before proceeding.

16. **Work order generation**: Create detailed work orders for each component specifying material, operations, quality requirements, and target completion dates.

17. **CNC program development**: Generate computer numerical control programs for plasma cutting machines, drill lines, and angle lines based on shop drawings.

18. **Fixture and jig planning**: Design and fabricate specialized holding fixtures and welding jigs needed for complex assemblies requiring precise alignment.

19. **Material requisition**: Pull specific steel pieces from inventory and transport to fabrication area using overhead cranes or forklift trucks.

20. **Pre-fabrication meeting**: Brief production team on project requirements, quality standards, safety considerations, and schedule expectations.

## Surface Preparation and Marking

21. **Mill scale assessment**: Evaluate condition of oxide mill scale on steel surface to determine if removal is required before fabrication.

22. **Abrasive blasting setup**: Configure blast room with steel grit or shot media for removing mill scale, rust, and contaminants down to bare steel.

23. **Blast cleaning operation**: Direct high-velocity abrasive media stream across all steel surfaces achieving SSPC-SP 10 near-white metal finish.

24. **Surface profile measurement**: Use replica tape to verify surface roughness meets 1.5-3.0 mil profile requirement for optimal coating adhesion.

25. **Compressed air cleaning**: Blow off residual abrasive particles and dust from all surfaces, crevices, and holes using filtered compressed air.

26. **Layout marking preparation**: Clean specific areas where layout lines will be marked using acetone or degreaser to ensure marking ink adheres properly.

27. **Centerline marking**: Use height gauge and scribe to mark longitudinal and transverse centerlines on beams and plates for reference during fabrication.

28. **Hole location marking**: Transfer hole locations from shop drawings to steel surfaces using center punch or marking paint with precise measurements.

29. **Cut line marking**: Mark cutting lines for coping, trimming, or shaping operations using soapstone, marking paint, or laser projection systems.

30. **Identification stamping**: Use low-stress stamps to mark piece identification numbers on steel members in locations specified by project requirements.

## CNC Plasma Cutting

31. **Plate positioning**: Place steel plate on CNC plasma cutting table and secure with magnetic clamps or vacuum hold-down system.

32. **Machine calibration check**: Verify plasma torch height control, cutting speed parameters, and gas pressures are properly configured for material thickness.

33. **Nesting program loading**: Transfer optimized cutting pattern program from engineering workstation to CNC plasma cutting machine controller.

34. **Torch consumable inspection**: Check plasma torch electrode, nozzle, and shield cap for wear and replace if approaching end of service life.

35. **Gas supply verification**: Confirm adequate supply of compressed air or oxygen for plasma generation and nitrogen for shield gas.

36. **Program simulation**: Run cutting program in simulation mode to verify tool paths avoid collisions and cutting sequence is logical.

37. **Pierce height setting**: Configure initial pierce height at 150% of normal cutting height to prevent nozzle damage during arc start.

38. **Lead-in parameter optimization**: Program appropriate lead-in and lead-out paths to minimize edge defects and preserve part geometry.

39. **Automated cutting execution**: Initiate CNC program allowing plasma torch to automatically cut all parts from plate following optimized path.

40. **Real-time monitoring**: Observe cutting process for proper arc characteristics, consistent speed, and detection of any torch collisions or consumable failures.

41. **Kerf compensation verification**: Check that parts cut to correct dimensions accounting for 3-5mm plasma kerf width depending on material thickness.

42. **Edge quality inspection**: Examine cut edges for squareness, smoothness, and absence of excessive dross or notching defects.

43. **Part separation**: Remove cut parts from remaining skeleton material and separate finished pieces from scrap waste sections.

44. **Dross removal**: Use hand grinder or chipping hammer to remove any solidified metal dross adhering to bottom edges of plasma-cut parts.

45. **Dimensional verification**: Measure critical dimensions of cut parts using tape measures, calipers, or coordinate measuring equipment.

## Drilling and Punching Operations

46. **Drill line setup**: Position steel beam or plate on CNC drill line table with material stops ensuring proper alignment to machine axes.

47. **Clamping system engagement**: Activate hydraulic or pneumatic clamps securing workpiece against movement during drilling operations.

48. **Drill bit selection**: Install carbide-tipped or high-speed steel twist drills of specified diameter with appropriate point angle for steel drilling.

49. **Coolant system activation**: Start flood coolant system delivering cutting fluid to drill point for heat dissipation and chip evacuation.

50. **Reference edge establishment**: Use machine sensors or operator input to establish datum edges from which all hole coordinates are measured.

51. **Hole coordinate program loading**: Transfer drilling program containing X-Y-Z coordinates for all holes from engineering system to drill line controller.

52. **Pilot hole drilling**: Drill small pilot holes at each location to establish centering and reduce cutting forces for final diameter drilling.

53. **Final diameter drilling**: Drill holes to specified final diameter in single operation or progressive steps depending on hole size and material thickness.

54. **Through-hole verification**: Ensure drill completely penetrates material thickness and breaks through cleanly without deformation or tearing.

55. **Hole spacing verification**: Measure distances between adjacent holes using precision scales or calipers confirming dimensions match shop drawings.

56. **Countersink operations**: Apply chamfer or countersink to hole edges as specified for flush mounting of fastener heads.

57. **Deburring**: Remove burrs and sharp edges from holes using deburring tools or abrasive methods on both entry and exit sides.

58. **Punch press operations**: For thinner materials, use CNC punch press with turret tool changer to rapidly punch holes instead of drilling.

59. **Punch tool maintenance**: Inspect and sharpen punches and dies regularly to maintain clean hole edges and prevent material distortion.

60. **Hole quality inspection**: Check holes for proper diameter, position accuracy within tolerance, edge condition, and perpendicularity to material surface.

## Bending and Forming

61. **Press brake setup**: Select and install appropriate punch and die tooling matching bend radius and angle requirements for component.

62. **Back gauge positioning**: Set adjustable back gauge to control distance from material edge to bend line ensuring accurate bend location.

63. **Bend angle calculation**: Calculate required overbend angle to compensate for springback based on material thickness, grade, and bend radius.

64. **Tonnage calculation**: Determine press brake tonnage required based on material thickness, width of bend, yield strength, and die opening.

65. **Material positioning**: Place steel plate or angle on press brake bed against back gauge stops aligning bend line with punch centerline.

66. **Test bend execution**: Perform initial test bend on scrap material verifying angle, radius, and leg dimensions meet specifications.

67. **Production bending**: Execute production bends by lowering ram with punch into die, plastically deforming material to specified angle.

68. **Multi-bend sequencing**: For components requiring multiple bends, perform in planned sequence avoiding interference with already-formed features.

69. **Angle verification**: Measure bend angles using protractors or digital angle finders confirming they meet drawing specifications within tolerance.

70. **Inside radius measurement**: Verify bend radius meets minimum requirements to prevent cracking while not exceeding maximum allowed radius.

71. **Dimension checking**: Measure leg lengths and overall dimensions of bent components ensuring bending process maintained proper geometry.

72. **Section bending**: Use section rolls or induction bending equipment to curve beams, channels, or pipes to specified radius for curved structures.

73. **Rolling operation**: Pass structural shapes through adjustable rollers multiple times gradually increasing curvature until desired radius is achieved.

74. **Curve template verification**: Check rolled curve against full-size templates or use geometry measurement ensuring accurate radius throughout length.

75. **Twist and warp check**: Place formed components on flat surface checking for any unintended twisting or warping that occurred during forming.

## Fitting and Assembly

76. **Component gathering**: Collect all parts needed for assembly including main members, connection plates, stiffeners, and fasteners.

77. **Assembly fixture preparation**: Set up welding fixtures, strongbacks, or jigs designed to hold components in proper relationship during assembly.

78. **Fit-up sequence planning**: Determine order of component assembly to ensure accessibility for welding while maintaining dimensional accuracy.

79. **Main member positioning**: Place primary structural members in fixture or on assembly table establishing basic framework for assembly.

80. **Squareness verification**: Use framing squares and measuring diagonals to verify members are positioned at specified angles, typically 90 degrees.

81. **Connection plate fitting**: Position gusset plates, splice plates, and connection angles at designated locations matching shop drawing details.

82. **Gap measurement**: Check gaps between mating surfaces ensuring they fall within acceptable limits for welding procedures, typically 1/16" maximum.

83. **Temporary attachment**: Install tack welds or temporary clamps holding components in position for measurement and final welding.

84. **Dimensional inspection**: Measure overall assembly dimensions, hole alignments, and member relationships confirming compliance with drawings.

85. **Tack welding**: Apply small intermittent welds at strategic locations securing components without inducing excessive distortion from welding heat.

86. **Straightness checking**: Use taut string lines or laser alignment tools verifying members remain straight without bowing or hogging.

87. **Hold point inspection**: Stop for mandatory quality inspection before proceeding to production welding, documenting measurements and conformance.

88. **Weld joint preparation**: Clean tack weld areas and adjacent base metal removing mill scale, rust, grease, and moisture for quality welding.

89. **Backing bar installation**: Install temporary or permanent backing bars behind full-penetration groove welds to support molten weld metal.

90. **Preheat application**: Apply preheat to thick sections or high-carbon steels using propane torches or induction heating equipment as required by welding procedures.

## Welding Operations

91. **Welding procedure specification review**: Verify appropriate WPS is selected matching base metal grade, thickness, joint type, and welding process.

92. **Welder qualification verification**: Confirm assigned welder holds valid certification for specific welding process, position, and material combination.

93. **Welding equipment setup**: Configure welding power source parameters including voltage, amperage, wire feed speed, and gas flow rates.

94. **Electrode selection**: Select proper welding electrode or wire meeting AWS specification for base metal compatibility and mechanical properties.

95. **Shielding gas verification**: Confirm adequate supply of appropriate shielding gas (CO2, argon/CO2 mix) with proper flow rate for weld protection.

96. **Root pass welding**: Execute initial weld pass in groove welds establishing fusion with both sides of joint and backing or root opening.

97. **Inter-pass cleaning**: Remove slag, spatter, and oxidation from previous weld pass using chipping hammers and wire brushes before next pass.

98. **Fill pass welding**: Apply intermediate weld passes building up joint to near-flush with base metal surface using proper weave or stringer techniques.

99. **Cap pass welding**: Execute final weld pass creating smooth, uniform surface profile meeting size requirements and providing specified throat dimension.

100. **Weld bead profile inspection**: Verify weld reinforcement height, width, and contour meet dimensional requirements without excessive convexity or concavity.

101. **Undercut inspection**: Check weld toes for undercut defects where base metal has melted away creating groove adjacent to weld.

102. **Overlap inspection**: Examine weld edges ensuring proper fusion without cold lap where weld metal sits on but doesn't fuse with base metal.

103. **Crater filling**: Ensure all weld craters are properly filled at termination points without leaving shrinkage voids or cracks.

104. **Spatter removal**: Clean weld vicinity removing spatter balls that adhered to base metal surfaces using chipping hammers or grinders.

105. **Multi-pass welding sequencing**: For large welds, deposit passes in planned sequence to control heat input and minimize distortion.

## Weld Quality Control

106. **Visual weld inspection**: Examine all welds under adequate lighting for surface defects including cracks, porosity, undercut, and incomplete fusion.

107. **Weld gauge measurements**: Use fillet weld gauges and contour gauges measuring weld sizes, leg dimensions, and throat thicknesses.

108. **Liquid penetrant testing**: Apply PT to weld surfaces detecting surface-breaking cracks or porosity by capillary action of colored dye.

109. **Magnetic particle testing**: Use MT on ferromagnetic steel welds creating magnetic field that reveals surface and near-surface discontinuities.

110. **Ultrasonic testing**: Perform UT on critical full-penetration welds using sound waves to detect internal defects like lack of fusion or slag inclusions.

111. **Radiographic testing**: For highest criticality welds, use X-ray or gamma-ray imaging creating permanent film record of internal weld quality.

112. **Weld repair procedures**: For rejectable defects, prepare weld repair plan including defect removal method, re-welding procedure, and re-inspection requirements.

113. **Defect removal**: Use air-arc gouging or grinding to completely remove defective weld metal back to sound base metal or acceptable weld.

114. **Repair welding**: Re-weld repaired areas following approved procedures with proper preheat, inter-pass temperature control, and filler metal.

115. **Repair verification testing**: Re-inspect repaired welds using same NDT methods as original inspection confirming defects have been eliminated.

## Grinding and Finishing

116. **Weld profile grinding**: Use angle grinders with depressed center wheels smoothing weld reinforcement flush with base metal where specified.

117. **Transition grinding**: Create smooth transitions from weld metal to base metal eliminating sharp notches that could act as stress concentrations.

118. **Edge grinding**: Grind cut edges removing plasma cut taper, roughness, or dross creating smooth edge suitable for welding or finishing.

119. **Burr removal**: Eliminate all sharp edges, burrs, and fins from holes, cuts, and corners using hand files, grinders, or deburring tools.

120. **Grinding wheel safety**: Inspect grinding wheels for cracks before mounting and operate at proper speed to prevent wheel failure and injury.

121. **Grinding ventilation**: Use local exhaust ventilation or respirators capturing metal dust and fumes generated during grinding operations.

122. **Flush grinding technique**: Hold grinder at proper angle maintaining consistent pressure to achieve smooth, even surface without gouging base metal.

123. **Post-weld distortion correction**: Identify and measure any distortion that occurred from welding heat using straightedges and surveying equipment.

124. **Heat straightening**: Apply controlled localized heating with rosebud torch while components are restrained, inducing contraction to remove distortion.

125. **Mechanical straightening**: Use hydraulic presses or jacking systems applying force to cold work bent members back into straight alignment.

## Surface Preparation for Coating

126. **Final cleaning specification review**: Verify surface preparation requirements specified for coating system including cleanliness and profile standards.

127. **Solvent wiping**: Remove oil, grease, and soluble contaminants using clean rags and approved solvents before abrasive cleaning.

128. **Abrasive blast cleaning**: Re-blast all surfaces removing welding residue, rust, and contaminants achieving specified cleanliness standard.

129. **Surface profile verification**: Measure surface roughness using comparator standards or replica tape confirming profile matches coating requirements.

130. **Blast media selection**: Choose appropriate abrasive media (steel shot, steel grit, aluminum oxide) based on desired profile and cleanliness.

131. **Blast equipment maintenance**: Inspect blast nozzle for wear and ensure abrasive separator and dust collector systems are functioning properly.

132. **Blaster personal protection**: Ensure blast operators wear supplied-air respirators, protective suits, gloves, and boots preventing dust exposure.

133. **Visible contamination check**: Visually inspect blasted surfaces under bright light confirming complete removal of rust, mill scale, and old coatings.

134. **Soluble salt testing**: Test surface for chloride and sulfate contamination using test strips or conductivity meters, cleaning if levels exceed limits.

135. **Post-blast protection**: Protect freshly blasted surfaces from moisture, contamination, and flash rust until coating application begins.

## Painting and Coating

136. **Paint specification review**: Verify coating system specification including product, colors, dry film thickness, and number of coats required.

137. **Environmental conditions check**: Measure air temperature, surface temperature, relative humidity, and dew point ensuring conditions support proper curing.

138. **Paint mixing**: Thoroughly mix coating components following manufacturer instructions using mechanical agitators ensuring pigments are uniformly dispersed.

139. **Induction time compliance**: After mixing multi-component coatings, observe specified induction time allowing chemical reaction to begin before application.

140. **Viscosity adjustment**: Thin coating to proper viscosity for application method using specified thinners, measuring with viscosity cup or gauge.

141. **Spray equipment setup**: Configure airless or conventional spray equipment with appropriate tip size, air pressure, and fluid pressure settings.

142. **Primer application**: Apply first coat (shop primer or specified primer) in uniform thickness covering all surfaces without sags or holidays.

143. **Wet film thickness measurement**: Check coating thickness while wet using wet film gauge ensuring proper application to achieve specified dry film thickness.

144. **Edge and corner coverage**: Apply extra attention to edges, corners, and welds ensuring adequate coating thickness on these critical areas.

145. **Overspray control**: Use spray techniques and equipment settings minimizing overspray waste while achieving proper coverage and appearance.

146. **Flash-off time**: Allow adequate time between coats for solvents to evaporate and coating to cure following manufacturer's recoat window requirements.

147. **Second coat application**: Apply intermediate or finish coats to specified thickness ensuring complete coverage and uniform appearance.

148. **Stripe coating**: Apply additional hand-applied stripe coats to edges, welds, corners, and fasteners before or between spray coats.

149. **Holiday detection**: Use electronic holiday detector testing dried coatings for pinholes or thin spots that would compromise corrosion protection.

150. **Dry film thickness measurement**: Measure cured coating thickness using magnetic thickness gauge at specified test frequency documenting compliance.

## Galvanizing Preparation (Alternative to Paint)

151. **Galvanizing design review**: Verify components are designed for hot-dip galvanizing with proper venting, drainage holes, and thickness considerations.

152. **Vent hole installation**: Drill holes at high points and closed sections allowing zinc to enter and air to escape during galvanizing immersion.

153. **Drainage hole installation**: Drill holes at low points enabling drainage of molten zinc and cleaning solutions from sealed cavities.

154. **Sharp edge removal**: Grind sharp corners and edges to minimum 2mm radius preventing zinc coating cracking at stress concentration points.

155. **Masking threaded areas**: Apply thread protectors or masking plugs to precision threads preventing zinc build-up that would interfere with assembly.

156. **Assembly for galvanizing**: Join components together before galvanizing if they will not be accessible for welding after coating application.

157. **Transportation to galvanizer**: Load fabricated steel onto trucks for transport to hot-dip galvanizing facility ensuring proper tie-down and protection.

158. **Caustic cleaning**: At galvanizer, immerse steel in hot alkaline solution removing oil, grease, paint marks, and organic contaminants.

159. **Acid pickling**: Immerse steel in dilute hydrochloric or sulfuric acid bath dissolving rust, mill scale, and other iron oxides.

160. **Water rinse**: Flush steel with clean water removing residual acid and dissolved iron salts that could contaminate flux or zinc bath.

161. **Fluxing**: Immerse steel in aqueous zinc ammonium chloride solution coating surfaces with flux that prevents oxidation and promotes zinc bonding.

162. **Drying**: Allow fluxed steel to dry or pre-heat before zinc immersion preventing violent reaction when moisture contacts molten zinc at 850Â°F.

163. **Zinc bath immersion**: Lower steel into molten zinc bath ensuring complete immersion and allowing sufficient dwell time for coating formation.

164. **Zinc drainage**: Slowly withdraw steel from zinc bath allowing excess zinc to drain back into kettle while coating is still molten.

165. **Air cooling**: Allow galvanized steel to cool in air until zinc coating solidifies and temperature reduces to safe handling levels.
