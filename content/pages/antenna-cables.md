# How to Make Antenna RF Cable (Coaxial)

**Top Market Leaders:**
- [Amphenol](/companies/amphenol)
- [Molex](/companies/molex)
- [Murata](/companies/murata)



Miniature coaxial cable connecting WiFi/Bluetooth/cellular antennas to radio modules in Mac computers. Ultra-thin 50Ω impedance-controlled transmission line for 2.4-6 GHz signals. Typical construction: 0.81mm (MHF I/U.FL) or 1.13mm (MHF II/U.FL 2) outer diameter, stranded or solid copper center conductor, PTFE or polyethylene dielectric, stainless steel or copper braid shield, micro coaxial connectors (<2mm). Requirements: low insertion loss (<0.5 dB/10cm at 5 GHz), high shielding effectiveness (>65 dB), flexibility for routing in tight spaces. Global production: 500+ million RF antenna cables/year (smartphones, laptops, IoT devices, routers).

**Step 1-15: Center [Copper Wire](copper-wire) Conductor Production**
Ultra-fine copper wire for RF signal transmission. **Wire gauge:** 40-44 AWG (0.08-0.05mm diameter, hair-thin, 1-5 strands). Single solid conductor (simpler, consistent impedance) or 7-strand (0.03mm × 7 strands, more flexible, fatigue-resistant). **Drawing process:** Start with 1.0mm copper wire rod (OFC 99.99% Cu, low oxygen <10 ppm, minimizes RF loss from grain boundary scattering). Progressive drawing: 1.0mm → 0.5mm → 0.25mm → 0.12mm → 0.08mm (final 40 AWG), 15-20 passes total, each pass 20-30% reduction. Dies: synthetic diamond (harder than tungsten carbide, for ultra-fine wire <0.1mm, longer life 100,000+ meters). Intermediate annealing: hydrogen atmosphere 400-500°C every 5-6 passes (prevent embrittlement, maintain ductility). Lubrication: mineral oil or synthetic ester (extreme pressure, prevents die scoring). Drawing speed: 500-1,500 m/min for fine wire (slower for <0.08mm due to breakage risk, tensile strength 40 AWG solid wire 0.3-0.5 kg). **Final anneal:** 250-300°C continuous furnace (set temper, electrical conductivity 100-102% IACS, resistivity 0.017 Ω·mm²/m). **Surface quality critical:** No scratches, nicks, oxidation (surface roughness affects skin-effect loss at GHz frequencies, RF current concentrates in outer skin depth δ = 1.3 µm at 5 GHz in copper). **Stranding (if multi-strand):** Tubular or planetary strander twists 7× 0.03mm wires (7/48 AWG configuration), lay length 1-3mm (tight lay, maintains round cross-section, improves flexibility without significantly increasing RF loss vs solid - skin effect already confines current to surface, so stranding penalty <0.1 dB/m at 5 GHz). **Tinning (optional):** Electroplate tin 0.5-1.0 µm over copper (solder protection, prevents oxidation, adds ~0.02 dB/m loss at 5 GHz due to tin's lower conductivity 15% IACS vs Cu 100% - trade-off accepted for reliability).

**Step 16-35: [PTFE Dielectric](ptfe-polymer) Insulation Extrusion**
Dielectric material surrounds center conductor, provides insulation and sets coaxial impedance. **Material selection:** PTFE (polytetrafluoroethylene, Teflon®, relative permittivity εᵣ 2.03-2.08, loss tangent tan δ <0.0002 at 5 GHz, best RF performance), expanded PTFE (εᵣ 1.4-1.6, foamed structure, even lower loss/dispersion, more expensive), solid polyethylene PE (εᵣ 2.26, tan δ 0.0003, lower cost, acceptable for <3 GHz). PTFE advantages: ultra-low loss, stable εᵣ over wide frequency/temperature range (–200 to +260°C), chemically inert, but high cost $30-50/kg vs PE $2-5/kg. **PTFE extrusion challenges:** PTFE does not melt (decomposes at 327°C before melting), requires paste extrusion or sintering. (1) **Paste extrusion:** Mix PTFE fine powder (particle size 20-500 µm) with hydrocarbon lubricant (15-25% by weight, naphtha or mineral spirits). Ram extruder (hydraulic pressure 10-50 MPa) forces paste through die (annular gap, center conductor passes through). Extrudate diameter controlled by die gap and draw-down ratio (pull speed vs extrusion rate). (2) **Sinter:** Pass extruded PTFE-coated wire through sintering oven (380-420°C, above PTFE crystalline melting point 327°C, particles fuse into continuous solid dielectric, lubricant vaporizes/burns off in controlled atmosphere). Cooling: air or water quench solidifies PTFE, shrinks onto conductor (adhesion poor - PTFE non-stick, but mechanical interlocking with conductor surface roughness or stranding holds dielectric in place). **Dimensional control:** Coaxial impedance Z₀ = (138/√εᵣ) × log(D/d), where D = shield inner diameter, d = dielectric outer diameter (which equals conductor outer diameter for solid dielectric). Target 50Ω: for 0.08mm conductor, dielectric OD must be 0.26mm (D/d ratio 3.25 with PTFE εᵣ 2.05). Tolerance ±0.01mm (±1-2Ω impedance variation). Laser diameter gauge (continuous monitoring, resolution 1 µm) feeds back to extruder draw speed controller. **Alternative PE extrusion:** Thermoplastic extrusion at 180-220°C (simpler than PTFE paste extrusion, solid polyethylene melts cleanly, lower cost equipment, but higher RF loss 0.3-0.5 dB/m at 5 GHz vs PTFE 0.2-0.3 dB/m).

**Step 36-58: Shield Layer Construction - Foil and Braid**
Coaxial shield: outer conductor (completes transmission line, return path for RF current, provides electromagnetic shielding against interference). **Shield design options:** (1) **Single braid:** Tinned copper or bare copper braid (48 AWG individual wire ends, 16-32 carriers, 85-92% coverage, flexible, good shielding 60-75 dB). (2) **Foil + braid (dual-shield):** Aluminum or copper foil inner layer (100% coverage, bonded to polyester film 5-10 µm, drain wire 44 AWG), plus outer braid (shielding effectiveness SE >80 dB, better than braid alone). (3) **Stainless steel braid:** Higher strength, corrosion resistance (marine, medical applications), lower conductivity (7-10% IACS vs Cu 100%), acceptable for short cables <20cm where loss penalty <0.1 dB. (4) **Spiral shield:** Single or multiple copper wires (0.05-0.08mm) wrapped helically around dielectric (90-95% coverage, very flexible, lower cost, SE 50-65 dB). **Foil application (if used):** Aluminum/polyester laminate foil (7 µm Al + 10 µm PET, total 17 µm) wrapped longitudinally around PTFE dielectric (overlap 5-10%, aluminum side inward contacts dielectric, drain wire 44-46 AWG tinned copper positioned in overlap seam). Foil serves with tape or adhesive. **Braiding:** Braiding machine (vertical or horizontal, 16-32 spindles/carriers) weaves tinned copper wires over foil (or directly over dielectric if no foil). Braid wire: 48-50 AWG (0.03-0.025mm), 5-10 ends per carrier, 120-320 total ends. Braid angle: 30-45°, pick count 40-80 picks/inch (crossovers per unit length, higher pick = tighter weave = better coverage but less flexible). Coverage calculation: C = 2(n×w) – (n×w)² / (D×cos α), where n = number of carriers, w = wire width, D = diameter, α = braid angle. Target 90-95% for premium RF cables. **Braid tinning (optional):** Pre-tin braid wires before weaving (flux + hot solder bath 240-260°C, coat wires with tin-lead 63/37 or SAC305, improves solderability at connector termination, prevents oxidation, adds cost $0.20-0.40/m).

**Step 59-78: Outer Jacket and Cable Completion**
**Jacket material:** (1) PVC (low-cost, flame-retardant, flexible, adequate for static routing inside device chassis, $0.05-0.10/m). (2) Polyethylene PE (lower dielectric loss if jacket close to shield, moisture resistant, $0.08-0.12/m). (3) Fluoropolymer FEP or PFA (high-temperature 200°C, chemical resistant, low outgassing for vacuum applications, $0.50-1.00/m). (4) No jacket (some ultra-miniature cables omit jacket, bare braid shield, used only inside shielded enclosures or with external sleeving). **Jacket extrusion:** Extruder (screw diameter 25-45mm, for thin jacket 0.05-0.15mm), temperature 160-200°C for PVC, 180-220°C for PE, 300-360°C for FEP. Crosshead die: coaxial construction, shield-covered core passes through center, molten polymer coats. Final OD: 0.81mm (MHF I, very thin, flexible, for tight spaces), 1.13mm (MHF II, slightly thicker shield, lower loss), 1.37mm (MHF III/MHF 4, even better performance, less common), 1.32mm (U.FL, similar to 1.13mm, different connector standard). Jacket thickness 0.05-0.10mm (just enough for mechanical protection, abrasion resistance, color coding). **Color coding:** Black (standard, carbon black pigment), white, gray, red/blue/yellow (identify antenna function: WiFi 2.4 GHz, WiFi 5 GHz, Bluetooth, LTE, GPS). **Cable flexibility:** Miniature RF cables highly flexible (1-2mm bend radius possible without damage, allows routing around components, through tight gaps in laptop chassis). Flexibility from: thin wires, stranded center conductor, fine braid, soft PTFE dielectric (not rigid like solid ceramics). **Cut to length:** Precision cutting machine (rotary or guillotine blade, clean perpendicular cut, no crushing of coax, critical for controlled impedance at cable end). Typical lengths: 50-200mm (internal laptop antenna cables), 100-300mm (desktop/router antenna extensions).

**Step 79-100: [Micro Coaxial Connector](micro-coaxial-connector) Manufacturing**
Ultra-miniature RF connectors <2mm outer dimension. **Standards:** (1) U.FL (Hirose, most common, plug 2.4×1.9×1.0mm, receptacle 2.2×2.0×1.1mm, snap-on mating, 30 mating cycles rated, up to 6 GHz). (2) MHF I/MHF II/MHF 4 (I-PEX/JAE, similar to U.FL, slightly different dimensions, 6-10 GHz capability). (3) IPEX MHF4 (1.6×1.6×0.7mm, smallest, 10 GHz). (4) Murata MM series (automotive-grade, vibration-resistant). **Plug connector parts:** (1) Center contact pin: Brass or phosphor bronze, diameter 0.4-0.6mm, length 3-5mm, stamped and formed, solder cup or crimp barrel at back. Gold plating 0.3-1.0 µm over nickel 1-2 µm (contact area). (2) Dielectric insulator: PTFE or LCP, molded or machined, centers pin, insulates from shell, dimensions set 50Ω impedance (precise ID/OD ratio). (3) Outer shell/ferrule: Stainless steel or brass, stamped 0.1-0.15mm thick, cylindrical or rectangular, snap-fit tabs (locking mechanism). Gold or tin plating. (4) Crimp sleeve (cable termination): Copper or brass ferrule crimps onto cable shield braid. **Receptacle (PCB-mount):** Surface-mount or through-hole, center pin (spring contact, mates with plug pin, phosphor bronze or beryllium copper, 20-50 grams normal force), dielectric body (LCP, holds pin, isolates from ground), ground terminals (SMT pads or through-hole legs, solder to PCB ground plane). Receptacle profile height 1.0-1.5mm above PCB (low-profile for thin devices). **Machining/stamping:** Precision metal stamping (progressive die, 0.01mm tolerance) or micro-machining (Swiss-type CNC lathe for turned pins <0.5mm diameter). **Insert molding:** Center pin positioned in mold, inject LCP (340-360°C), forms dielectric insulator body, encapsulates pin retention features.

**Step 101-120: Cable-to-Connector Termination (Plug Assembly)**
**Manual assembly (low-mid volume):** Operator uses stereo microscope (20-40× magnification), precision tweezers, micro-soldering iron. (1) **Strip cable:** Strip outer jacket 5-8mm from end (thermal stripper or razor blade under microscope, very careful not to nick braid). Fold braid back over jacket. (2) **Expose dielectric:** Cut away exposed braid to 2-3mm from jacket end. Slide crimp ferrule over folded braid. (3) **Strip dielectric:** Cut PTFE dielectric to expose 1.0-1.5mm center conductor (hot blade or precision cutter, PTFE cuts cleanly). (4) **Solder center pin:** Connector center contact has solder cup (0.6mm ID). Insert tinned center conductor into cup, apply flux, touch with fine-tip soldering iron (0.3mm tip, 320-360°C, 2-3 seconds). Solder flows, wets conductor and cup. Inspect under microscope: concave fillet, no voids, excess, or bridges. (5) **Crimp shell:** Position connector shell over braid/ferrule. Crimp ferrule with precision crimping tool (hand crimper or pneumatic, 3-10 N force, ferrule compresses around braid, locks shield to connector shell ground). Verify crimp: pull test 5-10 N, connector should not separate. (6) **Snap shell:** Connector shell halves snap together (capture dielectric body, lock over crimped ferrule, complete assembly). **Automated assembly (high volume >10,000/day):** Semi-automated machines: (1) Strip station: Rotary blade/thermal stripper precisely removes jacket, braid, dielectric to programmed lengths. (2) Solder station: Induction heating or laser pulse solders center conductor to pin cup (3-5 seconds cycle, consistent quality). (3) Crimp station: Pneumatic crimper applies calibrated force to ferrule. (4) Vision inspection: Camera checks solder joint, crimp, dimensions (reject defects). Cycle time 15-30 seconds per connector (one operator supervises machine, loads cables, unloads finished assemblies).

**Step 121-142: Receptacle PCB Mounting and Mating Interface**
**Receptacle types:** (1) **Vertical PCB mount:** Center pin and ground terminals oriented perpendicular to PCB (plug mates from above, common for antennas mounted on PCB edge or external pigtails). (2) **Right-angle mount:** Mating direction parallel to PCB (saves height, plug cable routes along PCB surface). (3) **Board-to-board:** Both halves SMT-mounted (cable eliminated for ultra-short connections <10mm, stack height 1-3mm). **SMT reflow process:** Solder paste stencil printed on PCB pads (Type 4 or Type 5 paste, 25-38 µm spheres, SAC305 or SnPb eutectic). Pick-and-place machine positions receptacle (±0.05mm accuracy, vision alignment). Reflow oven: ramp to 150-180°C (preheat), soak 60-120 seconds (activate flux), ramp to 240-260°C peak (20-40 seconds above liquidus, solder melts, wets pads), cool. Nitrogen atmosphere (prevent oxidation, better wetting). Post-reflow inspection: X-ray for internal voids (center pin solder joint), AOI for tombstoning, misalignment. **Mating operation:** Plug pushed onto receptacle (perpendicular, 2-5 N insertion force), snap-fit tabs engage (audible/tactile click, retention force 2-8 N prevents accidental disconnect). Center pin makes contact first (hot-pluggable), outer shell grounds last (ESD protection). **Mating cycles:** U.FL/MHF connectors rated 30 cycles (not designed for frequent connect/disconnect, semi-permanent installation, service/repair only). Wear mechanisms: gold plating friction wear (1 µm plating good for 50-100 cycles, thinner 0.3 µm wears through by 20-30 cycles, nickel underplate exposed, contact resistance increases), spring contact fatigue (beryllium copper retains 80% spring force after 30 cycles, phosphor bronze 60-70%). Higher-durability variants: MHF 4L (50 cycles), specialized gold alloy hard gold plating (100 cycles), but higher cost $1-2 premium per connector.

**Step 143-160: RF Performance Testing with [Network Analyzer](network-analyzer)**
**Return loss (S₁₁):** Measures impedance matching. Vector network analyzer (VNA, Keysight E5071C or Rohde & Schwarz ZNB) applies swept RF signal 100 MHz to 10 GHz at cable input, measures reflected power (reflection coefficient Γ = (Z – Z₀)/(Z + Z₀), converted to S₁₁ in dB = 20 log |Γ|). Specification: S₁₁ < –10 dB (VSWR <2:1) over 2-6 GHz band for WiFi/BT cable. S₁₁ < –15 dB (VSWR <1.4:1) premium grade. Poor return loss indicates impedance discontinuity (connector transition, cable crush, dielectric thickness variation). **Insertion loss (S₂₁):** Forward transmission, measures signal attenuation. Apply signal at port 1, measure output at port 2, S₂₁ in dB (negative value, signal loss). Typical: 10cm cable at 2.4 GHz: –0.2 to –0.4 dB, at 5 GHz: –0.3 to –0.6 dB. Longer cable: loss scales with length (α dB/m frequency-dependent, increases with √f due to skin effect and dielectric loss). Specification: S₂₁ > –1.0 dB at 5 GHz for 15cm cable (allows system link budget, antenna gain minus cable loss must exceed receiver sensitivity). **VSWR (Voltage Standing Wave Ratio):** Alternate metric for impedance matching, VSWR = (1 + |Γ|) / (1 – |Γ|). Ideal 1:1 (perfect match), acceptable <2:1 (return loss –10 dB), good <1.5:1 (–14 dB). **Phase stability:** Measure electrical delay τ = phase(S₂₁) / (2πf). Group delay variation <±30 ps over band (important for wideband signals like WiFi 802.11ac 80 MHz channels, prevents inter-symbol interference). **Shielding effectiveness (SE):** Radiated emission test, apply 0 dBm signal inside cable, measure field strength 10cm away with EMI receiver (3-axis loop antenna). SE in dB = signal level inside – signal level outside. Specification: SE >65 dB (dual-shielded foil+braid), SE 50-60 dB (single braid). Validates shield integrity (no gaps at connectors, braid coverage adequate).

**Step 161-175: Quality Control, Reliability Testing, Environmental**
**Mechanical tests:** (1) **Pull test:** Apply axial tensile force 10-30 N (depending on cable size, connector type) for 1 minute. Measure connector displacement (<0.5mm allowed), verify no intermittent contact (monitor S₂₁ continuously during pull, fluctuation <0.2 dB). (2) **Flexing:** Clamp cable 20mm from connector, bend ±90°, 100-1,000 cycles (accelerated life). Check S₂₁ degradation (<0.5 dB increase), continuity (no opens), visual (no jacket cracking, braid breakage). (3) **Twist/torsion:** Rotate connector ±180° around cable axis, 50 cycles. Verifies strain relief, prevents conductor breakage inside connector. **Environmental:** (1) **Temperature cycling:** –40 to +85°C, 100 cycles (MIL-STD-202 Method 107), dwell 30 min each extreme. Electrical test at room temp after cycling (ΔS₂₁ <0.3 dB). Validates material CTE mismatch (PTFE, copper, steel expand differently, no delamination, cracking). (2) **Humidity:** 85°C/85% RH, 168-1,000 hours (IEC 60068-2-78). Check insulation resistance (>1 GΩ), S₂₁ (<0.3 dB change), corrosion on gold plating (no red plague, oxide spots). (3) **Thermal shock:** –55°C to +125°C, 15-minute transfer, 10 cycles. Extreme for automotive/aerospace (consumer laptops typically 0-50°C operating, –20 to +70°C storage). (4) **Vibration:** 10-500 Hz sweep, 1-5 G acceleration, 30 minutes per axis (MIL-STD-810 or IEC 60068-2-6). Monitors for intermittent contact (use oscilloscope trigger on S₂₁ dropout >1 dB, indicates loose connector, broken wire). **ESD immunity:** IEC 61000-4-2 contact discharge ±4 kV, air ±8 kV to connector shell. Should not damage cable or disrupt data link (WiFi module behind connector has ESD protection diodes, but cable must not become antenna that couples ESD energy into sensitive circuits). **Acceptance criteria:** <0.1% failure rate on mechanical/environmental (MTTF target >100,000 hours at 25°C, Arrhenius acceleration factor for elevated temp testing).

**Step 176-185: Assembly into Mac and Antenna Connection**
**Routing in laptop chassis:** Antenna cables routed from logic board (WiFi/BT module RF connector) to display hinge area (antennas embedded in display bezel, top/side edges of screen for best omnidirectional pattern). Cable length 80-150mm (13-inch MacBook Air), 120-200mm (16-inch MacBook Pro). Multiple cables: 3-4 antennas typical (2×2 MIMO WiFi main/aux, Bluetooth shared or separate, cellular modem in some models). **Installation:** (1) Snap connector onto WiFi module U.FL receptacle (press perpendicular, click, verify seated - incomplete mating causes high return loss, intermittent connection). (2) Route cable through channels, guides, or adhesive clips molded into chassis (prevents movement, abrasion against metal edges). (3) Dress cable with 5-10mm bend radius minimum (tighter bends deform coax, impedance discontinuity, increased loss). (4) Secure with Kapton tape or adhesive dots at 2-3 points along route. (5) Far end connects to antenna element (PCB trace antenna, laser-direct structuring LDS antenna molded into plastic, or flex antenna). Connection types: U.FL receptacle on antenna PCB (snap-on, same as module end), or solder joint (permanent, cable stripped, center conductor/shield soldered to antenna feed point and ground). **Antenna types in Mac:** (1) WiFi 2.4/5 GHz: Monopole or inverted-F antenna (IFA), 20-40mm length, omnidirectional pattern. (2) Bluetooth 2.4 GHz: Often shared with WiFi or separate diversity antenna. (3) UWB (Ultra-Wideband): 6-9 GHz for AirTag/spatial audio, small ceramic chip antenna, short cable 30-50mm. **Troubleshooting:** Poor WiFi signal → check antenna cable seating (reseat connector, S₂₁ test with VNA if available), inspect for pinch/crush damage (cable routed under screw boss, crushed during assembly - cable flat spot, impedance step, –3 to –6 dB loss increase). Cable replacement: disconnect both ends, route new cable same path, reconnect (repair complexity: moderate, requires display removal on MacBook to access antenna area).
