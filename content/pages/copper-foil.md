## Introduction to Electrolytic Copper Foil

1. Understand that electrolytic copper foil is a thin copper sheet produced through electrodeposition, primarily used in printed circuit boards (PCBs) and lithium-ion batteries.

2. Recognize that Mitsui Mining & Smelting is a leading Japanese manufacturer with expertise in high-quality copper foil production for electronics applications.

3. Note that electrolytic copper foil offers superior uniformity, surface smoothness, and electrical conductivity compared to rolled copper foil.

4. Identify the two main market segments: standard thickness (18-35μm) for PCBs and ultra-thin (6-12μm) for flexible circuits and battery collectors.

5. Understand that the global copper foil market is driven by increasing demand for consumer electronics, electric vehicles, and renewable energy storage systems.

## Raw Material Preparation and Quality Control

6. Source high-purity copper sulfate pentahydrate (CuSO4·5H2O) with copper content above 25.5% and impurity levels below 50 ppm.

7. Verify copper sulfate quality through ICP-MS analysis to detect trace contaminants like iron, nickel, arsenic, and lead that could affect foil properties.

8. Prepare the electrolyte bath by dissolving copper sulfate in deionized water with resistivity exceeding 15 MΩ·cm to prevent contamination.

9. Add sulfuric acid (H2SO4) at concentrations of 80-120 g/L to increase electrolyte conductivity and maintain proper pH between 0.5-1.5.

10. Monitor electrolyte temperature continuously, maintaining it between 45-60°C for optimal copper ion mobility and deposition rate.

11. Filter the electrolyte solution through 1-5 μm cartridge filters to remove particulate matter that could cause surface defects.

12. Implement continuous electrolyte circulation at flow rates of 2-4 m/s to ensure uniform copper ion concentration near the cathode surface.

13. Add organic additives including gelatin, chloride ions, and proprietary brighteners at concentrations of 10-100 ppm to control grain structure and surface finish.

14. Prepare the titanium drum cathode by inspecting for surface defects, corrosion, or mechanical damage that could transfer to the copper foil.

15. Clean the titanium drum using alkaline detergent solution at 60-70°C, followed by acid pickling in 10% sulfuric acid to remove oxides.

## Cathode Drum Preparation and Setup

16. Measure the titanium drum diameter precisely, typically 1.5-3.0 meters, to calculate foil production speed and thickness control parameters.

17. Polish the drum surface to a mirror finish with surface roughness Ra below 0.1 μm using progressive grades of abrasive compounds.

18. Apply a chromium or chromium-nickel release layer to the drum surface through electroplating or PVD coating to facilitate foil separation.

19. Inspect the release layer for uniformity and adhesion using cross-hatch tests and visual examination under bright lighting.

20. Mount the titanium drum on precision bearings capable of handling loads up to 5000 kg while maintaining concentricity within 0.05 mm.

21. Install drive motors with variable frequency drives (VFD) to control drum rotation speed between 1-3 meters per minute with accuracy of ±0.1%.

22. Connect the drum to the negative terminal of the DC rectifier system, establishing it as the cathode where copper will deposit.

23. Position the drum partially submerged in the electrolyte bath, with immersion depth typically 30-50% of the drum circumference.

24. Verify electrical contact resistance between the drum shaft and power supply, maintaining below 10 milliohms to prevent voltage drops.

25. Install proximity sensors and encoders to monitor drum rotation speed, position, and any vibration anomalies during operation.

## Anode System Configuration

26. Select insoluble anodes made from lead-tin alloy, titanium with mixed metal oxide coating, or dimensionally stable anodes (DSA) for oxygen evolution.

27. Position anode plates parallel to the drum surface at distances of 40-80 mm to create uniform electric field distribution.

28. Calculate the anode-to-cathode surface area ratio, typically maintaining 1.2-1.5:1 to ensure adequate current distribution without excessive voltage.

29. Install anode bags made from polypropylene or acrylic fabric to contain any anode degradation products and prevent contamination.

30. Connect anodes to the positive terminal of the rectifier system using heavy-duty copper bus bars with current capacity exceeding peak demand by 25%.

31. Implement current distribution shields or auxiliary anodes at the edges to compensate for edge effects and maintain uniform thickness across foil width.

32. Monitor anode condition regularly for oxide layer degradation, mechanical damage, or excessive wear that could compromise performance.

33. Design the anode array to allow individual section control, enabling compensation for thickness variations across the drum width.

34. Install reference electrodes (saturated calomel or silver-silver chloride) to monitor electrode potentials and detect electrochemical imbalances.

35. Ensure proper anode spacing consistency using adjustable mounting brackets with positioning accuracy of ±2 mm.

## DC Power Supply and Rectifier System

36. Select industrial-grade DC rectifiers with output capacity of 10,000-50,000 amperes at voltages between 6-15 volts DC.

37. Specify rectifiers with ripple factor below 5% to minimize AC component that could cause dendrite formation or rough deposits.

38. Install thyristor or IGBT-based switching systems for precise current control with response time under 100 milliseconds.

39. Implement multi-zone current control systems allowing independent adjustment of current density across different drum sections.

40. Set current density parameters between 20-60 A/dm² depending on target foil thickness, electrolyte conditions, and desired properties.

41. Calculate theoretical copper deposition rate using Faraday's law: mass = (current × time × atomic weight) / (valence × Faraday constant).

42. Program current profiles for startup, steady-state operation, and shutdown to prevent shock loading and surface defects.

43. Install current shunts and Hall effect sensors for real-time monitoring of current distribution with accuracy of ±0.5%.

44. Implement over-current protection systems with circuit breakers and emergency shutdown capability to prevent equipment damage.

## Electrodeposition Process Control

67. Set drum rotation speed between 1.0-3.0 meters per minute depending on target thickness, with slower speeds for thicker foils.

68. Calculate foil thickness based on current density, rotation speed, and immersion arc length using the formula: thickness = (current × time × 0.635) / (area × density).

69. Monitor the depositing copper surface continuously using optical inspection systems or cameras to detect defects in real-time.

71. Maintain steady-state operation for continuous production, with process stability typically achieved after 30-60 minutes of operation.

## Foil Stripping and Collection

88. Set peeling angle between 90-135 degrees to minimize stress on the foil during separation from the drum surface.

89. Apply consistent tension force of 0.1-0.5 kg per cm of width, monitored using load cells and tension sensors.

90. Guide the stripped foil through dancer rollers or tension control systems to maintain uniform stress and prevent wrinkles.

91. Rinse the stripped foil immediately with deionized water sprays to remove electrolyte residue and prevent staining.

92. Inspect the shiny drum-side surface and the matte deposited-side surface for defects, scratches, or particles.

93. Dry the foil using hot air blowers or infrared heaters at temperatures of 80-120°C to remove moisture completely.

## Surface Treatment - Roughening Treatment (RTF)
