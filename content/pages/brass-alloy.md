## Manufacturing Process: Brass Alloy Production

### Raw Material Preparation
1. Receive high-purity copper cathodes from electrolytic refining facility (99.99% Cu minimum).
2. Inspect copper cathodes for surface oxidation, contamination, or physical damage.
3. Store copper cathodes in climate-controlled warehouse maintaining 40-60% relative humidity.
4. Receive zinc ingots from electrolytic zinc smelter (Special High Grade, 99.995% Zn).
5. Verify zinc ingot certification for impurity levels (Fe, Pb, Cd below specifications).
6. Store zinc ingots separately from copper to prevent cross-contamination.
7. Receive lead metal for leaded brass grades (99.94% Pb minimum purity).
8. Quarantine all incoming metals in receiving area pending quality verification.
9. Sample copper cathodes using diamond coring drill for chemical analysis.
10. Perform ICP-OES (Inductively Coupled Plasma Optical Emission Spectroscopy) on copper samples.
11. Sample zinc ingots by drilling representative cores from multiple ingots.
12. Analyze zinc composition using atomic absorption spectroscopy.
13. Test lead metal for bismuth content (critical impurity affecting properties).
14. Document all incoming material certificates of analysis in quality management system.
15. Calculate copper cathode weight requirements based on production schedule.
16. Weigh copper cathodes on calibrated 5-ton platform scale (±0.1% accuracy).
17. Transport copper cathodes to melting department using overhead crane.
18. Calculate zinc ingot quantity based on target brass composition.
19. Weigh zinc ingots on calibrated scale for precise alloy formulation.
20. Prepare lead additions for free-machining brass grades (typically 0.5-3.5% Pb).

### Alloy Composition Design
21. Select target brass composition based on customer requirements (C26000, C36000, C27400).
22. Design C26000 cartridge brass composition: 70% Cu, 30% Zn for deep drawing applications.
23. Formulate C36000 free-machining brass: 61.5% Cu, 35.5% Zn, 3% Pb for automatic screw machines.
24. Calculate C27400 yellow brass composition: 65% Cu, 35% Zn for hardware and fasteners.
25. Design C28000 Muntz metal composition: 60% Cu, 40% Zn for high-strength structural applications.
26. Formulate admiralty brass (C44300): 71% Cu, 28% Zn, 1% Sn for corrosion resistance.
27. Account for zinc volatilization losses during melting (typically 2-4% excess zinc).
28. Calculate scrap metal contribution to overall chemistry if using recycled brass.
29. Prepare compositional correction materials for mid-stream adjustments.
30. Generate batch melting sheet with precise weights for each constituent metal.
31. Review historical data for previous heats to optimize zinc recovery.
32. Adjust composition for seasonal temperature variations affecting zinc losses.
33. Calculate oxygen content targets for degassing operations.
34. Determine phosphorus deoxidizer additions (15-20 ppm for oxygen control).
35. Plan grain refiner additions if required for casting structure.

### Induction Melting Operations
36. Preheat induction melting furnace to 200°C to drive off moisture from refractory lining.
37. Inspect furnace crucible for cracks or erosion requiring maintenance.
38. Load copper cathodes into 15-ton capacity induction furnace crucible.
39. Activate medium-frequency induction heating system (1000 Hz, 3000 kW power).
40. Monitor copper melting progression using infrared thermal imaging camera.
41. Achieve complete copper melting at 1100°C (copper melting point: 1085°C).
42. Superheat molten copper to 1150°C to ensure complete liquidity.
43. Skim oxide dross from copper surface using perforated graphite skimmer.
44. Add zinc ingots gradually to molten copper to minimize vaporization losses.
45. Submerge zinc below copper surface using mechanical plunger to reduce vapor exposure.
46. Monitor zinc addition rate at 150-200 kg/minute to control temperature drop.
47. Observe temperature decrease as zinc melting absorbs heat (zinc melting point: 420°C).
48. Compensate heat loss by increasing induction power during zinc additions.
49. Continue zinc additions until achieving target composition minus volatilization allowance.
50. Add lead metal for free-machining grades by plunging below melt surface.
51. Stir molten brass using electromagnetic stirring function of induction furnace.
52. Achieve homogeneous melt at 1050°C holding temperature.
53. Measure melt temperature using disposable Type-K thermocouples.
54. Sample molten brass using vacuum sampling probe for compositional analysis.
55. Pour sample into chilled copper mold for rapid solidification.

### Degassing and Refining
56. Analyze poured sample using X-ray fluorescence spectrometry for rapid chemistry results.
57. Confirm copper, zinc, and lead percentages match target specification.
58. Add corrective copper or zinc if composition deviates beyond ±0.5% tolerance.
59. Introduce nitrogen gas through rotary degassing lance to remove dissolved hydrogen.
60. Bubble nitrogen through melt at 15 liters/minute for 10 minutes.
61. Observe gas bubble evolution indicating hydrogen removal from solution.
62. Add phosphorus-copper deoxidizer (15% P) at 0.02% to scavenge dissolved oxygen.
63. Allow deoxidation reaction to proceed for 5 minutes before stirring.
64. Skim deoxidation products (phosphorus oxides) from melt surface.
65. Monitor melt fluidity improvement as gas content decreases.
66. Check melt temperature maintaining 1040-1060°C optimal casting range.
67. Add boron or zirconium grain refiners (10-50 ppm) for naval brass grades.
68. Allow grain refiners to dissolve and distribute throughout melt.
69. Perform final temperature adjustment to 1050°C for casting operations.
70. Take final chemistry sample using vacuum probe for archival analysis.

### Continuous Casting Process
71. Preheat continuous casting machine graphite mold to 300°C using resistance heating.
72. Apply boron nitride mold release coating to casting mold interior surfaces.
73. Position water-cooled copper chill blocks at mold exit.
74. Set casting machine withdrawal speed at 250 mm/minute for 150 mm diameter billets.
75. Tilt furnace using hydraulic tilting mechanism to pour molten brass into tundish.
76. Allow brass to flow through refractory tundish into casting mold cavity.
77. Initiate water spray cooling system delivering 120 liters/minute to chill blocks.
78. Monitor billet surface temperature at mold exit maintaining 650-700°C.
79. Observe solidification front progression through transparent mold section.
80. Withdraw solidified brass billet continuously using chain-driven pinch rolls.
81. Measure billet diameter using laser micrometer every 10 seconds.
82. Adjust casting speed based on diameter feedback to maintain 150 mm ±1 mm tolerance.
83. Monitor casting machine oil hydraulic pressure ensuring consistent withdrawal force.
84. Inspect billet surface for cracks, ripples, or surface defects during casting.
85. Cut cast billet into 6-meter lengths using traveling shear mechanism.
86. Transfer cast billets to cooling bed using roller conveyor system.
87. Allow billets to air cool to below 200°C before handling (approximately 30 minutes).
88. Visually inspect cooled billets for surface quality and straightness.
89. Mark each billet with heat number, alloy grade, and production date using metal stamps.
90. Store cast billets in designated area organized by composition and heat number.

### Hot Rolling Operations
91. Select cast brass billets designated for sheet or strip production.
92. Load billets into gas-fired reheating furnace maintaining 700°C atmosphere.
93. Soak billets at temperature for 60 minutes to achieve uniform heat distribution.
94. Monitor furnace atmosphere preventing excessive oxidation (slightly reducing atmosphere).
95. Extract heated billet from furnace using automated charging table.
96. Transfer glowing billet to two-high reversing hot rolling mill (1200 mm width).
97. Set roll gap to 100 mm for first rolling pass (starting from 150 mm billet).
98. Execute first hot rolling pass achieving 33% thickness reduction.
99. Reverse billet direction and reduce roll gap to 70 mm for second pass.
100. Continue hot rolling passes reducing thickness 25-30% per pass.
101. Monitor brass temperature maintaining above 600°C during hot rolling.
102. Return billet to reheating furnace if temperature drops below 550°C.
103. Achieve final hot rolled thickness of 10 mm after 6-8 rolling passes.
104. Observe increasing length from 6 meters to approximately 90 meters during rolling.
105. Coil hot rolled brass strip using mandrel coiling system.
106. Allow coiled strip to air cool on cooling racks in production floor.
107. Measure hot rolled strip thickness using hand-held ultrasonic gauge (multiple locations).
108. Visually inspect for edge cracks, surface tears, or laminations.
109. Weigh coiled strip verifying material yield versus starting billet weight.
110. Calculate hot rolling yield (typically 96-98% after scale loss and cropping).

### Cold Rolling Process
111. Descale hot rolled brass coil using mechanical wire brushing operation.
112. Pickle brass coil in 10% sulfuric acid solution at 60°C for 5 minutes.
113. Rinse pickled coil thoroughly in running water spray to remove acid residue.
114. Dry brass coil using forced hot air blowers before cold rolling.
115. Mount pickled coil on uncoiler mandrel of tandem cold rolling mill.
116. Thread brass strip through four-stand tandem rolling mill (each stand 1500 mm wide).
117. Apply neat rolling oil to work rolls using recirculating lubrication system.
118. Set first stand roll gap to 7.5 mm achieving 25% reduction (from 10 mm).
119. Set second stand roll gap to 5.5 mm achieving 27% additional reduction.
120. Set third stand roll gap to 3.8 mm achieving 31% reduction.
121. Set fourth stand roll gap to 2.5 mm achieving final 34% reduction.
122. Run cold rolling mill at 300 meters/minute processing speed.
123. Monitor rolling force on each stand maintaining 800-1200 kN per stand.
124. Observe strip temperature rise from cold working (reaching 150-180°C).
125. Cool work rolls using internal water cooling passages (flow rate 200 L/min per roll).
126. Apply tension between stands using bridle rolls preventing slack or buckling.
127. Measure strip flatness using laser flatness measurement system.
128. Detect and correct strip camber (sideways curvature) using roll tilting.
129. Monitor strip edges for cracking indicating excessive reduction or poor lubrication.
130. Collect rolling oil drainage for filtration and recirculation to coolant system.
131. Recoil cold rolled brass strip at exit using tension-controlled recoiler.
132. Achieve 75% total cold work reduction creating work-hardened brass strip.
133. Label cold rolled coil with temper designation H04 (hard temper per ASTM B36).
134. Transfer cold rolled coil to annealing department for softening treatment.
135. Clean cold rolled brass using alkaline cleaner removing residual rolling oil.

### Annealing Operations
136. Load brass coil into continuous annealing furnace (roller hearth type).
137. Heat brass strip to 450°C in controlled atmosphere (nitrogen or dissociated ammonia).
138. Maintain annealing temperature for 2 hours dwell time for complete recrystallization.
139. Observe grain structure transformation from elongated cold worked to equiaxed grains.
140. Cool annealed brass gradually to 150°C within furnace cooling zone.
141. Exit annealed coil from furnace at handling temperature below 100°C.
142. Measure hardness of annealed brass using Rockwell B scale (target: RB 45-60).
143. Compare hardness to cold worked state (H04 temper typically RB 75-85).
144. Verify softening achieved reducing strength and increasing ductility.
145. Designate annealed material as O61 temper (soft anneal per ASTM B36).
146. Process batch annealing alternative in bell furnace for thicker gauges.
147. Load stacked brass coils on annealing base under protective atmosphere bell.
148. Heat batch to 500°C and hold for 4 hours for complete stress relief.
149. Allow batch to furnace cool to room temperature over 24 hours.
150. Remove annealed coils from bell furnace protecting against oxidation.
