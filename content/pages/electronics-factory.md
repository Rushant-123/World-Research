# How to Make Electronics Factory

**Top Market Leaders:**
- [Foxconn](/companies/foxconn)
- [Samsung](/companies/samsung)
- [SK Hynix](/companies/sk-hynix)



An electronics factory is a specialized manufacturing facility for producing semiconductors, circuit boards, and electronic components. Modern electronics factories combine cleanrooms, automated assembly lines, and precision testing equipment to manufacture billions of components annually.

## What is it?

A multi-story facility (100,000-1,000,000 sq ft) housing cleanrooms with Class 1-100 air quality, automated production equipment, semiconductor fabrication tools, and quality control systems. Processes silicon wafers, assembles circuit boards, tests components. Operates 24/7 with 1,000-50,000 employees. Used by companies like Foxconn (Apple products), TSMC (chips), Samsung (memory). Investment: $500M-$20B depending on technology node.

## Steps to Make:

**Site Selection and Planning:**
1. Identify location with stable power grid, water supply, skilled workforce
2. Requirements: flat land (20-100 acres), low seismic activity, minimal vibration
3. Proximity to airports for component shipping, near tech hubs for talent
4. China (Shenzhen, Chengdu), Taiwan (Hsinchu), South Korea (Seoul area) common
5. Assess environmental regulations: wastewater treatment, chemical handling
6. Plan for expansion: reserve additional land for future capacity increases
7. Total construction timeline: 18-36 months from groundbreaking to production


**Foundation and Structural Design:**
8. Excavate site: remove 10-20 feet of soil for stable foundation
9. Install deep pilings: 40-100 feet deep to minimize vibration
10. Vibration critical: semiconductor equipment sensitive to 0.1 micron movement
11. Pour reinforced concrete foundation: 3-6 feet thick
12. Foundation must support: 50-100 tons per square meter for heavy equipment
13. Install seismic dampers in earthquake-prone regions
14. Level foundation surface: ±1mm flatness across floor area
15. Install floor drains: for chemical spills, water leaks, emergency flooding
16. Apply epoxy floor coating: chemical-resistant, conductive for ESD protection
17. Build multi-story structure: typically 3-5 floors
18. Interstitial floors: space between floors for utilities, piping, ductwork
19. Ceiling height: 15-20 feet in cleanrooms for air handling systems


**Building Construction:**
20. Source [Factory Building](industrial-building) construction services
21. Steel frame construction: more flexible than concrete for heavy equipment loads
22. Reinforce floors: extra support under lithography machines (100+ tons)
23. Install vibration-isolated pads under critical equipment areas
24. Build external walls: insulated panels for temperature control
25. Install loading docks: for receiving 300mm wafer shipments, chemical deliveries
26. Construct wafer storage areas: climate-controlled, ESD-protected
27. Build chemical storage: separate building with containment, fire suppression


**Cleanroom Infrastructure:**
28. Install [Cleanroom Infrastructure](cleanroom-facility) in manufacturing areas
29. Cleanroom classes: Class 1 (semiconductor fab), Class 10-100 (assembly)
30. Class 1: less than 1 particle >0.5 micron per cubic foot of air
31. Raised floor system: 2-4 feet high for air return and utility routing
32. Perforated floor tiles: allow filtered air to return to air handling units
33. Cleanroom walls: modular panels with smooth, non-particle-shedding surfaces
34. Install cleanroom ceilings: HEPA/ULPA filter modules cover entire ceiling
35. HEPA filters: 99.99% efficient at 0.3 microns
36. ULPA filters: 99.9995% efficient at 0.12 microns (for advanced nodes)


**Air Handling and HVAC:**
37. Install [HVAC and Air Filtration](air-handling-system) system
38. Air recirculation rate: 300-600 air changes per hour in cleanrooms
39. Calculate air volume: 10× room volume per minute turnover
40. Install air handler units (AHUs): 50-200 units per factory
41. Each AHU: 10,000-50,000 CFM capacity
42. Primary filters: remove large particles before HEPA/ULPA
43. Install chilled water system: maintain 20-23°C temperature (±0.1°C)
44. Humidity control: 40-45% relative humidity (±1%)
45. Install chemical filters: activated carbon removes airborne molecular contamination
46. Vibration isolation: mount AHUs on springs to prevent transmission


**Temperature and Humidity Control:**
47. Temperature critical: affects photoresist coating, chemical reactions
48. Install precision chillers: 500-2,000 ton capacity per building
49. Cooling towers: dissipate heat from chillers (5-20 MW heat rejection)
50. Install humidifiers: ultrasonic or steam injection
51. Dehumidifiers: desiccant or cooling-based for humidity control
52. Zone control: different areas may need different conditions
53. Lithography areas: tighter control (±0.05°C) than assembly areas
54. Monitor 24/7: real-time sensors report temperature/humidity deviations


**Electrical Infrastructure:**
55. Install [Power Infrastructure](electrical-distribution) system
56. Power requirement: 50-500 MW depending on factory size
57. TSMC Fab 18 (Taiwan): 200+ MW continuous power draw
58. Build dedicated substation: 161 kV or 345 kV high-voltage feed
59. Install transformers: step down to 13.8 kV for distribution
60. Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS): 10-60 seconds backup power
61. UPS critical: prevents wafer loss during power glitches
62. Diesel generators: 50-200 MW backup for extended outages
63. Generator startup time: 10 seconds (UPS bridges the gap)
64. Install power distribution panels: 480V, 208V, and 120V circuits
65. Dedicated circuits for critical equipment: isolated grounds, surge protection
66. Power quality monitoring: track voltage, harmonics, transients


**Water Infrastructure:**
67. Ultra-pure water (UPW) system: most critical utility in electronics factory
68. Water consumption: 2-4 million gallons per day for large fab
69. Install water intake: municipal water or well water as source
70. Pre-treatment: remove suspended solids, chlorine, organics
71. Reverse osmosis (RO): first purification stage, removes 95-99% contaminants
72. Electrodeionization (EDI): further purification, removes ions
73. UV oxidation: breaks down organic molecules
74. Ultra-filtration: 0.01 micron filters remove remaining particles
75. Final polish: mixed-bed ion exchange resins
76. Result: 18.2 megohm-cm resistivity water (ultra-pure)
77. Recirculation loop: UPW continuously circulates to prevent stagnation
78. Storage tanks: 50,000-200,000 gallon capacity with nitrogen blanketing


**Chemical Distribution Systems:**
79. Install chemical delivery infrastructure for acids, solvents, gases
80. Sulfuric acid (H2SO4): for cleaning, etching (thousands of gallons)
81. Hydrofluoric acid (HF): for oxide etching (extremely hazardous)
82. Phosphoric acid, nitric acid: various etching processes
83. Install double-wall piping: leak detection between walls
84. Chemical cabinets: ventilated storage at each tool
85. Install bulk chemical tanks: 5,000-20,000 gallon tanks outside building
86. Secondary containment: tanks sit in concrete containment basins
87. Chemical delivery pumps: pneumatic or peristaltic for precision
88. Automated monitoring: sensors detect leaks, pressure loss


**Gas Distribution Systems:**
89. Install specialty gas distribution: nitrogen, oxygen, hydrogen, argon, silane
90. Nitrogen (N2): inert atmosphere in tools, purging, cleanroom pressurization
91. N2 consumption: 100,000-500,000 SCFM for large factory
92. On-site nitrogen generation: cryogenic air separation or membrane separation
93. Install high-pressure cylinders or bulk tanks for specialty gases
94. Silane (SiH4): for depositing silicon films (pyrophoric - auto-ignites in air)
95. Diborane (B2H6): p-type dopant (toxic)
96. Install gas cabinets: ventilated, fire-suppression, leak detection
97. Gas purifiers: remove oxygen, moisture to ppb levels
98. Welded stainless steel tubing: electropolished interior for purity
99. Automatic valve manifolds: computer-controlled gas delivery


**Wastewater Treatment:**
100. Electronics manufacturing generates acidic, alkaline, organic wastewater
101. Segregate waste streams: acids, bases, fluorides, organics, general
102. Install neutralization tanks: pH adjustment with NaOH or H2SO4
103. Fluoride precipitation: add calcium to precipitate calcium fluoride
104. Heavy metal removal: hydroxide precipitation, filtration
105. Organic destruction: UV oxidation, ozonation
106. Final pH adjustment: 6-9 pH before discharge
107. Continuous monitoring: pH, conductivity, temperature, heavy metals
108. Typical discharge: 1-3 million gallons per day after treatment


**Equipment Installation - Semiconductor Fabrication:**
109. Install [Semiconductor Equipment](fab-equipment) for chip manufacturing
110. Lithography machines: ASML EUV ($150M each), DUV ($50M each)
111. Position lithography machines: vibration-isolated pads, temperature-controlled zone
112. Install plasma etchers: 50-100 units per fab for various materials
113. Chemical vapor deposition (CVD) machines: deposit thin films
114. Physical vapor deposition (PVD) sputtering: deposit metal layers
115. Ion implanters: dope silicon with impurities
116. Chemical mechanical polishing (CMP): planarize wafer surfaces
117. Rapid thermal anneal (RTA): activate dopants with brief high heat
118. Each tool requires: power, water, gases, chemicals, exhaust, control network


**Equipment Installation - Assembly:**
119. Install [Automated Assembly Lines](assembly-automation) for components
120. Pick-and-place machines: mount chips on circuit boards (50,000 parts/hour)
121. Reflow ovens: solder components to boards (250°C peak temperature)
122. Wave soldering: for through-hole components
123. Automated optical inspection (AOI): verify component placement
124. X-ray inspection: check hidden solder joints, BGA balls
125. In-circuit test (ICT): verify electrical connectivity
126. Functional test: power up boards and run diagnostics
127. Conformal coating: apply protective polymer layer
128. Depanelization: separate individual boards from panels


**Equipment Installation - Testing:**
129. Install [Testing Systems](testing-equipment) for quality control
130. Wafer probers: test individual dies before dicing
131. Probe cards: custom designed for each chip design ($50,000-500,000 each)
132. Automated test equipment (ATE): final chip testing
133. Burn-in ovens: stress test chips at elevated temperature (125°C, 48-168 hours)
134. Test handlers: automate chip insertion/removal from test sockets
135. Environmental chambers: test at extreme temperatures (-40°C to 150°C)
136. Reliability testing: accelerated life testing, thermal cycling
137. Electron microscopy: failure analysis on defective units


**Cleanroom Garments and ESD Control:**
138. Establish gowning procedures: all personnel entering cleanroom must gown
139. Cleanroom suits (bunny suits): full-body Tyvek suits with hood
140. Booties: cover shoes to prevent particle shedding
141. Gloves: nitrile or latex, powder-free
142. Face masks: prevent breath moisture and particles
143. Hair nets: contain hair and scalp particles
144. Install air showers: blow high-velocity filtered air to remove particles
145. Sticky mats: capture particles from shoe bottoms before entering
146. ESD control: all surfaces, floors, workstations grounded
147. ESD wrist straps: workers connect to ground when handling components
148. Ionizers: neutralize static charges on wafers and tools


**Automation and Material Handling:**
149. Install overhead hoist transport (OHT) system in cleanrooms
150. OHT: computer-controlled monorail system carries wafer cassettes between tools
151. Track length: 1-5 km in large fabs
152. Automated guided vehicles (AGVs): transport materials on floor level
153. Stocker systems: automated storage and retrieval of wafer lots
154. Capacity: 10,000-50,000 wafer slots per stocker
155. Robot load ports: automated loading/unloading of wafer cassettes into tools
156. Reduces human handling: improves yield and throughput
157. Real-time tracking: RFID tags on wafer carriers, barcode scanning


**Manufacturing Execution System:**
158. Install factory control software: Manufacturing Execution System (MES)
159. MES tracks: every wafer lot through 500-1,000 process steps
160. Recipe management: each tool receives specific process parameters
161. Statistical process control (SPC): real-time monitoring of yield, defects
162. Equipment tracking: monitor tool uptime, maintenance schedules
163. Material tracking: chemicals, gases, consumables inventory
164. Integration with ERP: order management, shipping, invoicing
165. Real-time dashboards: management views factory performance metrics
166. Yield analysis: identify process steps causing defects


**Metrology and Inspection:**
167. Install inline metrology tools: measure wafer parameters after key steps
168. Critical dimension scanning electron microscope (CD-SEM): measures feature sizes
169. Accuracy: ±0.1 nm for advanced nodes
170. Ellipsometers: measure thin film thickness (0.1 nm precision)
171. Overlay metrology: ensure layers aligned within nanometers
172. Defect inspection: optical and e-beam scanners detect particles, scratches
173. Sampling strategy: measure 5-100% of wafers depending on process maturity
174. Electrical test: measure sheet resistance, capacitance, leakage
175. X-ray fluorescence (XRF): measure elemental composition of films


**Quality Control and Yield Management:**
176. Establish quality control protocols: ISO 9001, IATF 16949, ISO 13485
177. Incoming inspection: verify raw materials meet specifications
178. In-process inspection: check after critical steps
179. Final inspection: 100% testing or statistical sampling
180. Failure analysis lab: investigate defects with SEM, TEM, FTIR
181. Root cause analysis: 5-why, fishbone diagrams to identify issues
182. Corrective action: implement process changes to prevent recurrence
183. Yield ramp: new processes start at 30-50% yield, ramp to 80-95%
184. Yield learning curves: takes 6-24 months to achieve mature yield


**Environmental Health and Safety:**
185. Install safety systems: gas detection, fire suppression, emergency shutdown
186. Chemical safety: MSDS for all chemicals, spill kits, eyewash stations
187. Pyrophoric gas safety: silane burns spontaneously in air
188. Install flame detectors: shut off gas flow if ignition detected
189. Fire suppression: clean agent (FM-200) in equipment areas
190. Emergency ventilation: scrubbers treat exhaust before release
191. Personal protective equipment (PPE): face shields, chemical aprons, respirators
192. Training: all workers trained in chemical safety, emergency response
193. Regulatory compliance: EPA, OSHA, local environmental regulations
194. Waste disposal: contract with hazardous waste disposal companies
195. Sustainability initiatives: recycle water (90% recirculation), solar panels, heat recovery
    - Modern fabs achieve: 80-90% water recycling
    - Energy efficiency: 1.2-1.5 PUE (power usage effectiveness)
    - Chemical recycling: regenerate acids for reuse
    - Waste reduction: 95%+ of waste diverted from landfills
    - Carbon footprint: transitioning to renewable energy sources
    - LEED certification: green building standards
    - Community engagement: monitor emissions, noise, traffic impact


**Production Ramp and Commissioning:**
- Tool qualification: run test wafers to verify equipment performance
- Recipe development: optimize process parameters for yield
- Pilot production: manufacture small batches to validate processes
- High-volume manufacturing: ramp to target capacity over 6-12 months
- Continuous improvement: kaizen, six sigma methodologies


**Economics:**
- Construction cost: $500M (mature node), $5-20B (leading-edge fab)
- TSMC Fab 18 (3nm): estimated $20B investment
- Equipment cost: 60-70% of total investment
- Annual operating costs: $200M-2B (labor, utilities, materials)
- Depreciation: 5-7 years for equipment
- Capacity: 10,000-100,000 wafer starts per month
- Revenue per wafer: $1,000-10,000 depending on chip complexity

Electronics factories represent the pinnacle of manufacturing complexity and precision. They combine architectural, mechanical, electrical, chemical, and software engineering to create facilities that operate at the limits of physics and human capability. The cleanrooms are 100,000× cleaner than typical outdoor air, and the equipment positions wafers with sub-nanometer accuracy—equivalent to shooting a golf ball from New York to Los Angeles and landing within 1 inch of the target.
