Manufacturing motor permanent magnets for BLDC applications requires specialized processing of neodymium-iron-boron (NdFeB) materials to achieve high magnetic performance, precise dimensions, and reliable multi-pole magnetization patterns.

## Raw Material Preparation

Receive NdFeB ingot material containing approximately 32% neodymium, 64% iron, 1% boron, plus dysprosium and praseodymium additions for grade control. Verify material certification showing rare earth composition and trace impurities below 0.1%. Store ingots in controlled humidity environment below 40% RH to prevent oxidation. Inspect ingot surface for cracks or porosity using visual examination and ultrasonic testing. Cut ingots into smaller sections using diamond wire saw for hydrogen decrepitation processing. Clean cut surfaces with isopropyl alcohol to remove cutting residue.

## Hydrogen Decrepitation

Load ingot sections into hydrogen decrepitation furnace with capacity for 50 kg batch processing. Evacuate chamber to 10^-3 torr base pressure using rotary vane pump followed by turbomolecular pump. Heat chamber to 200°C at 5°C/min ramp rate while maintaining vacuum. Introduce hydrogen gas at 99.999% purity to pressure of 1 atmosphere. Monitor hydrogen absorption causing lattice expansion and mechanical fracturing of ingot. Observe material breaking into coarse powder fragments of 1-10 mm size over 2-4 hour exposure period. Cool chamber to room temperature under hydrogen atmosphere. Evacuate hydrogen and backfill with argon for safe material handling.

## Dehydrogenation

Transfer hydrogenated powder to vacuum dehydrogenation furnace to remove absorbed hydrogen preventing embrittlement. Heat material to 600°C under vacuum below 10^-2 torr. Hold temperature for 1 hour allowing hydrogen to desorb from NdFeB crystal structure. Monitor vacuum pressure increase indicating hydrogen release. Cool material to 200°C before backfilling with argon. Unload dehydrogenated coarse powder maintaining inert atmosphere exposure.

## Jet Milling

Feed coarse powder into jet mill system using argon carrier gas for oxygen-free milling. Accelerate powder particles to supersonic velocities in opposing gas jets causing particle collision and size reduction. Classify particles using cyclone separator collecting fine fraction below 5 microns. Target mean particle size of 3-4 microns for optimal pressing and sintering behavior. Monitor particle size distribution using laser diffraction analyzer sampling output every 30 minutes. Adjust jet mill pressure and feed rate to maintain consistent D50 particle size. Collect milled powder in sealed containers under argon atmosphere.

## Powder Characterization

Sample milled powder for particle size analysis verifying D10, D50, and D90 values meet specification. Measure specific surface area using BET method confirming 0.8-1.2 m²/g range. Analyze oxygen content using inert gas fusion showing below 2000 ppm oxygen. Test powder flowability and apparent density for pressing process optimization. Verify composition using X-ray fluorescence spectroscopy confirming neodymium and dysprosium concentrations. Store characterized powder in sealed containers with desiccant maintaining below 10% relative humidity.

## Pressing Die Preparation

Select pressing die geometry matching motor magnet arc segment design with typical dimensions 25mm length, 15mm width, 5mm thickness. Machine die cavity from hardened tool steel with surface finish below 0.4 micron Ra. Install electromagnetic coil around die cavity capable of generating 1.5-2.0 Tesla alignment field. Calibrate field strength using Hall effect sensor positioned at die center. Clean die surfaces with acetone removing any residual material or contamination. Apply release agent to die walls facilitating green compact removal.

## Magnetic Field Alignment Pressing

Load milled NdFeB powder into die cavity filling to predetermined volume for target green density. Position die assembly in hydraulic press with electromagnetic coil energized generating alignment field. Apply magnetic field perpendicular to pressing direction aligning powder particles' easy magnetization axes. Increase hydraulic pressure gradually to 50-100 MPa compacting powder while maintaining alignment field. Hold pressure for 30 seconds allowing particle rearrangement and achieving green density of 4.2-4.5 g/cm³. Release pressure and deactivate alignment field. Eject green compact from die cavity carefully to prevent cracking or chipping.

## Green Compact Inspection

Measure green compact dimensions using digital calipers verifying length, width, and thickness within ±0.2mm tolerance. Inspect compact surface for cracks, chips, or laminations indicating pressing defects. Test green strength by controlled flexure measurement confirming adequate handling strength. Weigh compact on precision balance calculating green density from mass and volume. Mark each compact with batch identification for traceability through sintering process. Place compacts on molybdenum setter plates for furnace loading.

## Vacuum Sintering Furnace Loading

Arrange green compacts on molybdenum setter plates with 5mm minimum spacing preventing adhesion during sintering. Stack setter plates in vertical configuration maximizing furnace capacity for batch processing. Load stack into vacuum sintering furnace chamber measuring 600mm diameter by 900mm height. Position thermocouples adjacent to compact stack monitoring temperature uniformity across load. Close furnace chamber and seal door with high-temperature gasket. Connect vacuum system with turbomolecular pump backed by rotary vane pump.

## Vacuum Pumpdown

Initiate vacuum pumpdown starting with rotary vane pump achieving rough vacuum of 10^-2 torr. Activate turbomolecular pump reducing pressure to 10^-4 torr base vacuum. Monitor leak rate confirming chamber integrity below 5 micron/hour. Heat chamber slowly to 200°C at 3°C/min driving off adsorbed moisture and organics from compacts and fixtures. Observe pressure increase from outgassing with turbomolecular pump maintaining 10^-3 torr during low temperature bakeout. Hold 200°C for 2 hours completing debinding and desorption.

## Sintering Heat Treatment

Increase furnace temperature from 200°C to 1060°C at controlled ramp rate of 5°C/min. Monitor temperature uniformity across furnace zone confirming ±5°C maximum variation. Reach sintering temperature of 1060°C where liquid phase formation occurs between NdFeB grains. Hold sintering temperature for 4 hours allowing grain growth and densification to theoretical density above 95%. Control vacuum during sintering maintaining below 10^-3 torr preventing oxidation of reactive neodymium. Monitor temperature control stability within ±2°C during hold period.

## Sintering Densification

Observe compact shrinkage of approximately 18-20% linear dimension during sintering as porosity is eliminated. Liquid phase of neodymium-rich material wets grain boundaries promoting densification and grain bonding. Control grain growth to 10-30 micron final size affecting coercivity and magnetic properties. Maintain vacuum preventing formation of neodymium oxide which degrades magnetic performance. Complete densification achieving final sintered density of 7.5-7.6 g/cm³ for standard grades.

## Post-Sintering Cool Down

Cool sintered magnets from 1060°C to 600°C at controlled rate of 3°C/min preventing thermal shock and cracking. Maintain vacuum during initial cooling stage to 600°C. Backfill furnace with argon at 600°C to atmospheric pressure protecting magnets from oxidation. Continue cooling from 600°C to room temperature at natural furnace rate over 6-8 hours. Monitor cooling uniformity preventing temperature gradients exceeding 10°C across magnet mass.

## Aging Heat Treatment

Transfer sintered magnets to aging furnace for precipitation heat treatment optimizing coercivity. Heat magnets to 500°C at 5°C/min under argon atmosphere. Hold temperature for 2 hours precipitating fine Nd-rich phase at grain boundaries. Cool to 450°C and hold for additional 2 hours completing precipitation sequence. Control cooling rate from 450°C to room temperature at 2°C/min preventing crack formation. Aging treatment increases coercivity by 10-15% through grain boundary phase optimization.

## Sintered Magnet Inspection

Measure sintered magnet dimensions recording shrinkage from green compact state. Weigh magnets calculating sintered density from mass and volume measurements. Inspect magnet surfaces for cracks, voids, or incomplete densification using visual examination and 10x magnification. Test surface hardness using Rockwell C scale expecting HRC 55-60 range. Perform ultrasonic inspection detecting internal cracks or porosity. Sort magnets by visual grade separating premium quality from acceptable and rejected parts.

## Grinding Process Setup

Mount sintered magnets on vacuum chuck of surface grinder for precision machining to final dimensions. Select diamond grinding wheel with 400 grit for rough grinding and 800 grit for finish grinding. Dress grinding wheel to achieve true running surface below 5 micron runout. Set grinding depth of cut to 0.05mm per pass for rough grinding and 0.01mm for finish grinding. Configure water-based coolant flow at 10 liters/minute providing cooling and chip removal. Establish grinding parameters for magnet material considering its brittle ceramic-like behavior.

## Rough Grinding

Perform rough grinding removing 0.5-1.0mm per surface to achieve near-final dimensions and remove sintering scale. Grind top and bottom surfaces parallel within 0.02mm tolerance. Control surface temperature during grinding preventing thermal damage above 150°C. Monitor grinding forces adjusting depth of cut to prevent chipping or cracking. Inspect surface finish after rough grinding confirming removal of sintering defects. Measure magnet thickness using micrometer verifying approach to target dimension within 0.1mm.

## Finish Grinding

Switch to fine grit diamond wheel for finish grinding achieving final dimensions and surface quality. Reduce depth of cut to 0.01mm per pass minimizing subsurface damage. Grind surfaces to final thickness tolerance of ±0.05mm. Achieve surface finish of 0.8 micron Ra suitable for coating adhesion. Grind arc profile for motor magnets matching rotor curvature within 0.03mm form tolerance. Control edge condition preventing chipping on sharp corners through optimized wheel infeed.

## Dimensional Measurement

Measure finished magnet dimensions using coordinate measuring machine (CMM) with accuracy of 2 microns. Verify length, width, thickness, and arc radius against specification tolerances. Check perpendicularity of ground surfaces confirming squareness within 0.05mm over part length. Measure surface finish using profilometer confirming Ra below 1.0 micron. Record dimensional data in quality database enabling statistical process control. Sort magnets into dimension groups for paired assembly into motor rotors.

## Electrical Discharge Machining

For magnets requiring complex profiles or tight tolerances, use EDM wire cutting as alternative to grinding. Mount sintered magnets on EDM work holding fixture ensuring secure positioning. Thread EDM wire electrode of 0.25mm diameter brass through magnet cutting path. Program CNC path cutting magnet profile with ±0.02mm tolerance. Set EDM parameters using low energy settings preventing thermal damage to magnetic material. Execute cutting operation with deionized water dielectric cooling and flushing cutting zone. Achieve surface finish of 1.5 micron Ra with minimal heat affected zone.

## Ultrasonic Cleaning

Immerse ground or EDM cut magnets in ultrasonic cleaning tank containing alkaline detergent solution at 50°C. Activate ultrasonic transducers at 40 kHz frequency generating cavitation for contaminant removal. Clean magnets for 10 minutes removing grinding swarf, coolant residue, and surface particles. Rinse magnets in deionized water spray removing detergent residue. Perform secondary ultrasonic rinse in pure deionized water for 5 minutes. Dry magnets using forced air at 80°C preventing water staining on magnet surface.

## Surface Coating Selection

Evaluate coating requirements based on motor operating environment and temperature conditions. Select nickel plating for standard applications providing corrosion resistance up to 150°C. Choose epoxy coating for cost-sensitive applications with moderate corrosion protection. Specify aluminum coating for applications requiring non-conductive surface. Consider phosphate conversion coating as primer for enhanced adhesion of subsequent coatings. Select multi-layer nickel-copper-nickel system for severe corrosion environments.

## Nickel Plating Preparation

Activate magnet surfaces using acid etch solution containing 10% nitric acid at room temperature. Immerse magnets for 30 seconds removing surface oxides and enhancing coating adhesion. Rinse magnets thoroughly in deionized water removing acid residue. Neutralize surfaces using weak alkaline solution preventing residual acidity. Perform final deionized water rinse preparing surfaces for electroplating. Rack magnets on plating fixture ensuring electrical contact and uniform current distribution.

## Electroless Nickel Strike Layer

Immerse racked magnets in electroless nickel plating bath at 85°C containing nickel sulfate and hypophosphite reducer. Deposit 1-2 micron strike layer over 10 minute immersion providing conductive base for electroplating. Monitor bath pH at 4.5-5.0 maintaining optimal deposition rate. Rinse magnets in deionized water removing drag-out solution. Inspect strike layer coverage confirming uniform deposition on all surfaces including edges and corners.

## Electrolytic Nickel Plating

Transfer racked magnets to electrolytic nickel plating tank containing nickel sulfamate electrolyte at 50°C. Connect magnets to negative cathode current while nickel anode provides metal source. Apply current density of 3-5 A/dm² depositing bright nickel at 15 microns/hour rate. Plate for 60 minutes achieving 15-20 micron total nickel thickness. Agitate plating solution using air bubbling maintaining uniform concentration at cathode surface. Monitor current efficiency and plating thickness using calibrated test coupons.

## Copper Intermediate Layer

For enhanced corrosion protection, deposit 3-5 micron copper intermediate layer between dual nickel layers. Transfer magnets to copper pyrophosphate plating bath at 50°C. Apply current density of 2-3 A/dm² depositing ductile copper layer over 20 minute plating cycle. Copper layer provides barrier effect and stress relief between nickel layers. Rinse magnets thoroughly removing copper electrolyte preventing contamination of subsequent nickel bath.

## Top Nickel Layer

Deposit final nickel layer providing corrosion resistant outer surface. Return magnets to nickel plating bath applying current density of 3-5 A/dm². Plate for 30 minutes depositing 8-10 micron top nickel layer. Total coating system achieves 25-30 micron thickness (Ni-Cu-Ni) providing excellent corrosion protection. Rinse plated magnets in deionized water removing electrolyte residue. Dry magnets at 80°C for 30 minutes preventing water staining on nickel surface.

## Epoxy Coating Application

For applications specifying epoxy coating, prepare magnet surfaces using alkaline cleaning and phosphate conversion coating. Mix two-component epoxy resin system with 10:1 resin to hardener ratio. Apply epoxy coating using electrostatic spray achieving uniform 25-30 micron wet film thickness. Control spray parameters including voltage (60-80 kV), flow rate, and spray distance. Ensure complete coverage of all magnet surfaces including edges and corners. Allow flash-off period of 10 minutes at room temperature for solvent evaporation.

## Coating Cure

Transfer coated magnets to curing oven maintaining 150°C temperature. Cure epoxy coating for 30 minutes cross-linking polymer network. Monitor oven temperature uniformity confirming ±5°C variation across cure zone. Control heating rate to prevent thermal shock or coating defects. Cool magnets gradually to room temperature over 1 hour period. Inspect cured coating for defects including pinholes, holidays, or insufficient coverage.

## Coating Thickness Measurement

Measure coating thickness using eddy current gauge calibrated for coating over ferromagnetic substrate. Verify thickness at multiple locations on each magnet including flat surfaces, edges, and corners. Confirm coating thickness meets specification range of 20-30 microns. Record thickness data documenting coating process control. Identify thin areas requiring rework or rejection based on minimum thickness requirement.

## Coating Adhesion Testing

Perform adhesion testing on sample magnets using cross-hatch tape test per ASTM D3359. Cut grid pattern through coating to substrate using sharp blade. Apply adhesive tape over grid and remove rapidly at 90 degree angle. Evaluate coating adhesion by rating removed coating percentage. Require 5B rating (0% coating removal) for production release. Test coating flexibility using mandrel bend test for epoxy coated magnets.

## Salt Spray Corrosion Testing

Subject sample magnets to accelerated corrosion testing per ASTM B117 salt spray exposure. Place coated magnets in salt spray chamber maintaining 35°C temperature and 5% NaCl solution. Expose samples for 48-96 hours depending on coating specification. Inspect magnets after exposure for evidence of corrosion, blistering, or coating failure. Require no visible corrosion or coating degradation for lot acceptance. Document corrosion resistance performance for customer qualification.

## Pre-Magnetization Testing

Measure unmagnetized magnet physical properties before magnetization processing. Test sample magnets for density confirming 7.5-7.6 g/cm³ target range. Measure electrical resistivity using four-point probe expecting 130-160 microhm-cm. Verify composition using X-ray fluorescence confirming neodymium, iron, and dysprosium concentrations. Test Curie temperature using vibrating sample magnetometer measuring 310-340°C transition. Perform microstructure analysis using SEM examining grain size and grain boundary phase distribution.

## Magnetizer System Setup

Configure impulse magnetizer system capable of generating peak fields above 30 kOe for complete magnetization. Install magnetizing fixture designed for motor magnet pole pattern with alternating north-south poles. Connect capacitor bank storing 50-100 kJ energy for discharge through magnetizing coil. Set charge voltage based on required magnetizing field strength and coil inductance. Calibrate magnetizing field using Hall probe measurement verifying field distribution. Position magnet indexing fixture for sequential multi-pole magnetization.

## Multi-Pole Magnetization Pattern

Design magnetization pattern matching motor rotor requirements with typical 8-12 pole configuration. Calculate pole pitch and pole arc angles for uniform flux distribution around motor circumference. Program magnetizer to create alternating north-south poles with sharp transition regions. Control pole width symmetry within ±0.5mm preventing motor torque ripple. Minimize interpolar leakage flux through optimized magnetizing field distribution. Design pole pattern accommodating sensor magnet position feedback requirements.

## Magnetization Process

Position unmagnetized motor magnet in magnetizing fixture aligning magnet axis with magnetic field direction. Secure magnet preventing movement during high force magnetization impulse. Charge capacitor bank to calculated voltage providing required magnetizing energy. Trigger discharge sending high current pulse through magnetizing coil. Generate peak magnetic field of 25-30 kOe magnetizing magnet to saturation. Complete magnetization within 10 millisecond pulse duration. Remove magnetized magnet carefully controlling attraction to steel fixturing. Index fixture to magnetize next pole position repeating until complete pole pattern achieved.

## Magnetization Uniformity

Control magnetizing field uniformity ensuring consistent magnetic properties across all poles. Measure field distribution using Hall effect sensor array mapping field during magnetization pulse. Verify each pole receives magnetizing field above 25 kOe minimum for complete saturation. Adjust capacitor voltage or magnetizing current compensating for coil heating effects over multiple cycles. Monitor coil temperature preventing overheating above 80°C requiring cooling delays. Achieve pole-to-pole flux uniformity within ±3% for low-cogging motor performance.

## Gaussmeter Flux Measurement

Measure magnetized magnet surface flux density using calibrated gaussmeter with Hall effect probe. Position probe on magnet pole face at standardized distance of 0mm (contact) or 1mm air gap. Measure peak flux density at pole center recording values for each pole. Verify flux density meets grade specification (N35: 11,000-12,200 gauss surface flux typical). Map flux distribution across pole face identifying variations from design intent. Record measurement data for each magnet establishing traceability and process control.

## Flux Uniformity Inspection

Evaluate flux uniformity across multiple poles of motor magnet ensuring balanced magnetic forces. Calculate percent variation between maximum and minimum pole flux densities. Require pole-to-pole variation below ±5% preventing motor vibration and cogging torque. Measure flux at pole edges verifying sharp transition to adjacent opposite poles. Check for residual magnetization in designed unmagnetized regions. Sort magnets by flux uniformity grade for paired assembly in precision motor applications.

## Helmholtz Coil Testing

Place magnetized magnet in Helmholtz coil magnetometer system for detailed magnetic characterization. Position magnet in uniform field region between parallel coils separated by coil radius distance. Rotate magnet measuring three-axis magnetic moment components using fluxgate sensors. Calculate total magnetic moment from vector sum of orthogonal components. Verify magnetic moment meets specification confirming complete magnetization. Measure magnetic center offset from geometric center indicating magnetization alignment quality.

## BH Loop Measurement

Characterize magnet material magnetic properties using hysteresisgraph or permeameter measurement system. Place sample magnet in closed magnetic circuit with C-frame yoke and flux sensor. Apply varying magnetizing field while measuring resulting flux density. Trace complete BH hysteresis loop from saturation through demagnetization to reverse saturation. Extract key magnetic parameters from measured BH curve including remanence, coercivity, and energy product.

## Remanence Measurement

Determine remanence (Br) as residual flux density after removing magnetizing field from saturation. Measure Br from BH loop intercept at H=0 representing maximum achievable flux density. Verify remanence meets grade specification (N35: Br ≥ 11.7 kG, N42: Br ≥ 12.8 kG, N52: Br ≥ 14.3 kG). Higher remanence grades provide increased motor torque and efficiency. Control remanence through composition optimization and grain size refinement. Temperature dependence of remanence typically -0.11%/°C for standard grades.

## Coercivity Measurement

Extract intrinsic coercivity (Hci) from BH loop as reverse field required to reduce magnetization to zero. Measure Hci at intersection of intrinsic BH curve with H-axis. Verify coercivity meets grade specification (N35: Hci ≥ 11 kOe, N42: Hci ≥ 12 kOe, N52: Hci ≥ 14 kOe). Higher coercivity provides better resistance to demagnetization in motor operation. Control coercivity through dysprosium addition (0.1% Dy increases Hci by 100 Oe) and grain boundary phase optimization. Temperature dependence of coercivity typically -0.6%/°C for standard grades.

## Energy Product Calculation

Calculate maximum energy product (BH)max as maximum value of B×H product from demagnetization curve. Locate (BH)max point at knee of demagnetization curve where magnet operates most efficiently. Verify energy product meets grade specification (N35: 33-36 MGOe, N42: 40-43 MGOe, N52: 50-53 MGOe). Energy product directly relates to magnetic stored energy and motor power density. Higher grade magnets enable motor downsizing or increased performance. Report (BH)max in units of MegaGaussOersted (MGOe) or kJ/m³ (1 MGOe = 7.96 kJ/m³).

## Demagnetization Curve

Analyze second quadrant demagnetization curve shape indicating magnet load line behavior. Verify linear demagnetization curve with minimal knee indicating stable operation against demagnetizing fields. Measure permeance coefficient where load line intersects demagnetization curve. Calculate demagnetization resistance factor from curve slope and coercivity ratio. Identify operating point for motor application ensuring adequate margin above knee region. Characterize demagnetization curve temperature dependence for thermal operating range.

## Temperature Coefficient Testing

Measure temperature coefficients of remanence (α) and coercivity (β) characterizing temperature stability. Heat magnet sample in controlled temperature chamber from 20°C to 100°C. Measure remanence and coercivity at 20°C intervals during heating cycle. Calculate α (typical -0.11%/°C) and β (typical -0.6%/°C) from linear regression of data. Verify temperature coefficients match grade specification tolerances. Lower magnitude temperature coefficients indicate better temperature stability for motor applications.

## Maximum Operating Temperature

Determine maximum operating temperature based on coercivity margin and demagnetization risk. Specify standard grade temperature limits: N (80°C), M (100°C), H (120°C), SH (150°C), UH (180°C), EH (200°C). Higher temperature grades incorporate dysprosium and terbium additions increasing coercivity. Test sample magnets at maximum temperature for 1000 hours verifying flux retention above 95%. Measure irreversible flux loss after thermal aging quantifying long-term stability. Specify appropriate grade for motor operating temperature ensuring adequate demagnetization margin.

## Curie Temperature Measurement

Measure Curie temperature (Tc) where ferromagnetism transitions to paramagnetism using thermomagnetic analyzer. Heat magnet sample in applied field measuring magnetization versus temperature. Identify Curie point where magnetization approaches zero typically at 310-340°C for NdFeB. Curie temperature represents absolute thermal limit for magnetic operation. Verify Tc measurement confirming proper composition and phase structure. Curie temperature relatively insensitive to dysprosium additions unlike coercivity.

## Thermal Cycling Test

Subject sample magnets to thermal cycling between -40°C and maximum operating temperature. Program thermal chamber for 100 cycles with 30 minute soaks at temperature extremes. Measure flux density before and after thermal cycling quantifying irreversible flux loss. Require less than 2% flux loss after 100 thermal cycles for production qualification. Inspect coating integrity after thermal cycling verifying no cracking or delamination. Thermal cycling exposes coating adhesion issues and magnetic stability problems.

## Mechanical Shock Testing

Mount magnetized magnet in fixture and subject to mechanical shock per MIL-STD-810 requirements. Apply shock pulse of 100G peak acceleration for 11 millisecond half-sine duration. Impact magnet along three orthogonal axes testing directional shock sensitivity. Measure flux density before and after shock test verifying no demagnetization or cracking. Inspect magnet visually using 10x magnification checking for chips, cracks, or coating damage. Brittle NdFeB material requires careful handling preventing mechanical damage in motor assembly.

## Vibration Resistance Testing

Secure magnet in vibration fixture simulating motor operating conditions. Apply sinusoidal vibration sweep from 10-2000 Hz at 5G acceleration amplitude. Vibrate magnet for 2 hours along each of three axes totaling 6 hour test duration. Monitor magnet condition during vibration using accelerometer detecting delamination or cracking. Measure flux density after vibration test confirming no demagnetization or damage. Test coating adhesion after vibration verifying no delamination or coating loss.

## Pulse Demagnetization Testing

Evaluate magnet resistance to demagnetizing fields using controlled reverse field pulses. Apply calibrated reverse magnetic field pulses of increasing amplitude to magnetized magnet. Measure flux density after each pulse quantifying progressive demagnetization behavior. Determine knee field strength where significant flux loss begins indicating coercivity limit. Plot flux retention versus reverse field characterizing demagnetization resistance. Correlate pulse demagnetization behavior with motor start-up current and fault condition survival.

## AC Demagnetization Testing

Test magnet exposure to alternating demagnetizing fields simulating motor fault conditions. Apply AC magnetic field at motor operating frequency with controlled amplitude. Measure flux loss as function of AC field amplitude and exposure duration. Determine AC demagnetization threshold field for 5% flux loss limit. Verify adequate margin between motor operating fields and demagnetization threshold. AC demagnetization more severe than DC due to minor loop traversal and heating effects.

## Eddy Current Loss Measurement

Characterize magnet eddy current losses under AC magnetic field excitation relevant to motor operation. Place magnet in AC field coil applying sinusoidal field at motor frequency (100-500 Hz). Measure calorimetric temperature rise indicating eddy current heating power. Calculate eddy current loss per volume and frequency for motor loss prediction. Segment magnets or use bonded magnet construction for high-frequency applications reducing eddy current losses. Electrical resistivity of 130-160 microhm-cm provides moderate eddy current loss for most motor applications.

## Magnetic Field Mapping

Create detailed three-dimensional magnetic field map around magnetized motor magnet using automated scanning. Mount magnet on non-magnetic positioning stage with three-axis Hall probe scanner. Scan probe through volume around magnet measuring Bx, By, Bz components on fine grid. Calculate total field magnitude and direction at each measurement point. Generate contour plots and 3D visualizations showing field distribution. Compare measured field to finite element analysis predictions validating magnetization pattern and pole geometry.

## Motor Assembly Simulation

Test magnet assembly into motor rotor simulating manufacturing process forces and alignment. Measure insertion force required to assemble magnet into rotor steel lamination pockets. Verify retention force using pull test confirming adhesive bond or mechanical retention. Measure assembled rotor balance using dynamic balancing machine requiring less than 0.5 gram-mm imbalance. Test rotor assembly withstanding centrifugal force at maximum motor speed with 20% safety margin. Verify magnet pole alignment with rotor position sensors ensuring proper commutation timing.
